## Introduction

Zakat is one of the pillars of Islam and holds a very important position in Islamic law. It is not merely an act of worship related to wealth, but also a means of purifying the soul, distributing welfare, and strengthening social bonds within society. For this reason, discussions of zakat in classical books of fiqh are always given a prominent place and explained in detail, both in terms of textual evidence, conditions, and specific legal rulings.

In the study of fiqh, *zakat al-mal* is explained as an obligation related to specific types of wealth that meet particular conditions. Scholars have clarified the types of wealth that are subject to zakat, the conditions that make zakat obligatory, and the legal details for each category of wealth based on the Qur’an, the Sunnah, and the consensus (*ijma‘*) of scholars.

## Definition and Legal Basis of Zakat

Linguistically, zakat means growth, increase, blessing, and abundant goodness. The expression *zakā az-zar‘u* means that a plant grows and flourishes, while the expression *zakā fulān* means that a person possesses much goodness.

In Islamic law, zakat refers to a specific portion of wealth that is given to specific categories of recipients under certain conditions. It is called zakat because the wealth from which zakat is given becomes blessed and grows, and because the one who gives it receives prayers from those who receive it. Allah says:

“…And whatever you give in zakat seeking the pleasure of Allah – it is they who will receive manifold increase.” (Qur’an, Ar-Rum: 39)

The obligation of zakat is established by the Qur’an, the Sunnah, and scholarly consensus. Allah says:

“And establish prayer and give zakat.” (Qur’an, Al-Baqarah: 43)

The Messenger of Allah ﷺ also said:

“Islam is built upon five pillars.”

Among these five pillars is zakat. Therefore, zakat is one of the pillars of Islam. Whoever denies its obligation is considered a disbeliever, except for someone who has recently embraced Islam and does not yet know its ruling. As for someone who refuses to pay zakat while acknowledging its obligation, zakat may be taken from him by force.

Zakat is divided into two types: zakat related to the body, namely *zakat al-fitr*, and zakat related to wealth, namely *zakat al-mal*.

## Types of Wealth Subject to Zakat

Zakat on wealth is obligatory on five categories: livestock, gold and silver, crops, fruits, and trade goods. This classification is based on the determinations of scholars in classical fiqh literature.

As for livestock, zakat is only obligatory on three types: camels, cattle, and sheep/goats. This is based on the consensus of scholars and various textual evidences. These three types are specified because their meat may be consumed and distributed to the poor, they are numerous, beneficial, and reproduce quickly. Other animals are not subject to zakat unless there is specific evidence indicating otherwise.

## Conditions that Make Zakat Obligatory

Scholars explain that zakat becomes obligatory with six conditions: Islam, freedom, complete ownership, reaching the minimum threshold (*nisab*), the passage of one lunar year (*haul*), and grazing for livestock. If all these conditions are fulfilled, zakat becomes obligatory without disagreement among scholars.

The first condition is Islam. A non-Muslim is not obligated to pay zakat because zakat is an obligation specifically directed toward Muslims. This is understood from the statement of the Prophet ﷺ that zakat is a form of charity required from Muslims. Therefore, a non-Muslim is not required to pay zakat while remaining a disbeliever, and if he later embraces Islam he is not required to make up the zakat he neglected before becoming Muslim. This ruling is similar to the obligation of prayer.

As for an apostate, the obligation of zakat that existed while he was Muslim does not fall from him. If a full year passes over his wealth and he then becomes apostate, scholars differ in opinion. The stronger opinion states that his wealth is left as it is; if he returns to Islam he must pay the zakat, but if he does not return, then no zakat is required from him.

The second condition is freedom. A slave is not obligated to pay zakat because he does not possess wealth independently, even if his master or someone else authorizes him to manage property. According to the correct opinion, such wealth remains the property of the master. A *mudabbar* slave and a female slave who has borne her master's child have the same ruling as other slaves, and zakat is not obligatory upon them. Likewise, a *mukatab* slave is not obligated to pay zakat because his ownership is considered weak.

If a slave is later freed and possesses wealth, the calculation of the year (*haul*) begins from the time he becomes free. If he returns to the ownership of his master and his wealth transfers to the master, then the master begins the calculation of the haul from that point.

The third condition is complete ownership. Zakat is not obligatory on wealth whose ownership is weak, such as wealth that is lost, stolen, seized, or entrusted to someone who denies the trust. In such cases, scholars have two opinions. According to the earlier opinion, zakat is not obligatory because the owner cannot benefit from the wealth. According to the later and stronger opinion, zakat remains obligatory because ownership still exists, although the zakat is not paid until the wealth is recovered.

The same principle applies to debts owed to someone. If the debt is irregular or uncertain, zakat is not obligatory because ownership is weak. If it is a stable debt, then the ruling differs according to circumstances.

If the debt consists of livestock not yet received by the owner and a year passes, zakat is not obligatory because livestock zakat requires grazing. However, if the debt consists of money, zakat remains due because money retains its function as a means of payment.

If the debt consists of money or trade goods, the stronger opinion in the school holds that zakat remains obligatory. However, if the debt is difficult to recover because the debtor is poor, denies the debt, or is absent, it takes the ruling of seized property, and zakat is not paid until it is recovered.

In some cases, such as purchased goods not yet received, scholars have differing opinions. The majority of scholars state that zakat remains obligatory because ownership has already been established.

Debt may also affect the obligation of zakat. If a person possesses wealth that reaches the nisab but also owes debts equal to or greater than that wealth, scholars have differing opinions. The stronger opinion in the Shafi‘i school states that debt does not prevent the obligation of zakat, whether the debt is due immediately or later. However, if a judge prohibits the person from using his wealth and creditors take control of it, zakat is no longer obligatory because ownership becomes weakened.

The fourth condition is reaching the *nisab*. Wealth that does not reach the nisab is not subject to zakat.

The fifth condition is the passage of one lunar year (*haul*). The Prophet ﷺ said:

> لا زَكَاةَ فِي مَالٍ حَتَّى يَحُوْلَ عَلَيْهِ الْحَوْلُ

“There is no zakat on wealth until one year has passed over it.”  
(Narrated by Abu Dawud)

The scholars of the early generations agreed upon this condition.

The sixth condition is grazing (*saum*), meaning that livestock graze freely in permissible pasture. The evidence for this is the letter of Abu Bakr regarding livestock zakat, which explains that zakat is obligatory on grazing sheep. Livestock that are specially fed are not subject to zakat because of the cost of their maintenance.

If livestock are fed most of the year, there is no zakat. However, if they are fed only briefly to maintain their survival, zakat remains obligatory.

Livestock used for labor, such as plowing land, carrying goods, or irrigation, are not subject to zakat because they are prepared for use rather than for reproduction.

Thus, these six conditions form the fundamental basis for determining whether zakat becomes obligatory upon a person's wealth.

## Conditions for Zakat on Gold and Silver

Gold and silver function as monetary instruments and are subject to zakat. Scholars state that zakat on them has five conditions: Islam, freedom, complete ownership, reaching the nisab, and the passage of one year.

The nisab for silver is two hundred dirhams, a ruling established by consensus. Ibn al-Mundhir confirmed this consensus.

In a hadith narrated in the two authentic collections it is stated:

> لَيْسَ فِيْمَا دُوْنَ خَمْسٍ أَوَاقٍ صَدَقَةٌ

“There is no zakat on less than five *uqiyyah* of silver.”

One *uqiyyah* during the time of the Prophet equaled forty dirhams, so five uqiyyah equal two hundred dirhams.

The nisab for gold is twenty *mithqal*. If someone possesses gold reaching this amount for one year, zakat becomes obligatory.

## Conditions for Zakat on Agricultural Produce

Zakat on crops is obligatory under three conditions: the crop is cultivated by humans, it is a staple food that can be stored, and it reaches the nisab.

Grains that are staple foods and provide sustenance are subject to zakat, provided they are cultivated by humans.

This ruling also applies to crops that grow from seeds owned by someone even if they were not directly planted, such as seeds carried by wind or water. Examples include wheat, barley, corn, sorghum, rice, mung beans, lentils, and similar legumes.

The obligation of zakat on these crops is based on the verse:

> ... وَآتُوا حَقَّهُ يَوْمَ حَصَادِهِ ...

“Give its due on the day of its harvest.” (Qur’an, Al-An‘am: 141)

Zakat is specifically imposed on staple foods because they are essential for human survival. Thus, the Sharia obligates zakat upon them as a means of caring for those in need.

Seeds used only for planting, such as carrot seeds, or vegetables like cucumbers and watermelons, are not subject to zakat because they are not staple foods but supplementary foods.

In addition, crops must reach a specific nisab for zakat to become obligatory. The details of this nisab are discussed in later sections.

## Zakat on Fruits

Zakat on fruits is only obligatory for two types: dates and grapes. The conditions for its obligation are four: Islam, freedom, complete ownership, and reaching the *nisab*. Whoever possesses dates or grapes and fulfills these conditions must pay zakat on them according to the consensus of scholars.

It is mentioned in a hadith that the Messenger of Allah ﷺ ordered that the yield of grapes be estimated just as dates are estimated, and that its zakat be taken in the form of raisins just as the zakat of dates is taken in the form of dried dates. This hadith was narrated by Al-Tirmidhi, who graded it as *hasan*, and it was authenticated by Ibn Hibban.

Dates and grapes are specified because both are staple foods that can be stored, making their ruling similar to grains. Other fruits, however, are generally consumed as supplements or for enjoyment, such as pears, pomegranates, peaches, quinces, and figs. Since they are not staple foods, zakat is not obligatory upon them.

In some classical works it is stated that there is no zakat on figs without disagreement. However, some scholars argued that figs should also be subject to zakat because their nature resembles raisins and they can function as a staple food. If the hadith regarding grapes is considered authentic, figs could be analogically compared to them. Even if the hadith is considered weak, the combination of grapes and dates could still serve as a basis for analogy. Some scholars rejected the obligation of zakat on figs because they are difficult to estimate.

In general, zakat is not obligatory on walnuts, almonds, bananas, apricots, olives, and similar fruits according to the stronger opinion within the school of law. This is because such fruits are not staple foods that form the basis of human sustenance. And Allah knows best.

## Conditions for Zakat on Trade Goods

Zakat on trade goods is obligatory under the same conditions as zakat on gold and silver. These conditions are: Islam, freedom, complete ownership, reaching the *nisab*, and the passage of one lunar year (*haul*). Trade goods refer to all items other than gold and silver that are prepared for commercial exchange. Any item intended for trade, once the necessary conditions are fulfilled, becomes subject to zakat.

The evidence for the obligation of zakat on trade goods is the statement of Allah:

> ... أَنفِقُوا مِن طَيِّبَاتِ مَا كَسَبْتُمْ ...

“Spend in the way of Allah from the good things that you have earned.” (Qur’an, Al-Baqarah: 267)

Mujahid explained that this verse was revealed concerning trade. In the Sunnah it is also reported that the Prophet ﷺ said, “There is zakat on clothing.” This hadith was narrated by Al-Hakim, who stated that it meets the conditions of Bukhari and Muslim. The meaning of clothing in this hadith refers to garments prepared for sale by merchants. Since zakat is not obligatory on clothing used for personal wear, the hadith is understood as evidence for zakat on trade goods. And Allah knows best.

In addition to the general conditions mentioned earlier, trade goods must truly be intended for commerce. This means that the owner must intend trade at the time of acquiring the goods, and that ownership is obtained through a pure commercial transaction.

If someone owns an item for personal use and later intends to sell it as merchandise, the stronger opinion states that it does not become trade goods. This view is held by the majority of scholars, whether the item was obtained through inheritance, gift, or purchase.

Ownership through pure exchange refers to ownership acquired through a transaction involving compensation, whether through money, goods, or debt that is immediate or deferred. Once an item legitimately becomes trade merchandise, a new intention is not required for every subsequent transaction.

Similarly, if a person settles a debt by accepting goods and intends to trade them, those goods become trade goods because they entered his ownership through exchange along with the intention of trade. In contrast, a pure gift that involves no exchange does not make the item trade goods.

Activities such as gathering firewood, cutting grass, catching fish, or receiving inheritance do not make those items trade goods, even if one intends to sell them later. Likewise, if someone returns an item due to a defect or receives back goods that were previously sold for personal use and then intends to sell them, they do not become trade goods.

If a person owns clothing for personal use and exchanges it for a slave intended for trade, then the clothing is returned to him due to a defect, the trading cycle (*haul*) is interrupted. The returned clothing does not become trade goods unless it had originally been intended for trade.

If two merchants conduct a transaction and then cancel it, the ruling of trade remains upon the two items. However, if someone owns clothing for trade and exchanges it for a slave intended for personal ownership rather than trade, then the clothing is returned due to a defect, the ruling of trade does not return because the intention for personal ownership has interrupted the trade cycle.

If a person performs *khul‘* with his wife and intends the compensation for trade, or if a woman marries and intends her dowry to be trade merchandise, then according to the stronger opinion those goods become trade goods. This is because they enter ownership through exchange accompanied by the intention of trade.

Similarly, if a person rents out his property or his services and intends that the payment received in goods will be used for trade, those goods become trade merchandise, since leasing is a form of exchange.

The general principle in this matter is that any item acquired through a pure exchange transaction along with the intention of trade becomes trade goods. Conversely, if the item is not obtained through exchange, or the exchange is not purely commercial, it does not become trade goods even if one intends to sell it.

The value of trade goods is calculated at the end of the haul according to their market selling price. And Allah knows best.

## Conclusion

From the entire discussion concerning *zakat al-mal*, it becomes clear that Islamic law establishes the system of zakat in a detailed, balanced, and wise manner. Zakat is not obligatory upon every type of wealth, but only upon specific types of wealth that possess the qualities of growth, benefit, and the ability to fulfill human needs. This demonstrates that zakat is not merely a ritual obligation, but also part of a social and economic system built upon justice and public welfare.

Scholars explain that zakat is obligatory on five main categories of wealth: livestock, gold and silver, agricultural produce, certain fruits, and trade goods. Each of these categories has specific conditions that must be fulfilled before zakat becomes obligatory.

Among the most fundamental conditions are Islam, freedom, complete ownership, reaching the nisab, and the passage of one lunar year. For livestock, the additional condition of grazing also applies.

These detailed rulings demonstrate that zakat is founded upon the principle of justice. Wealth that has not reached the nisab is not subject to zakat because it is considered insufficient to meet the owner's needs. Likewise, wealth whose ownership is weak, such as lost property, seized wealth, or debts that are difficult to collect, is not subject to zakat until ownership becomes secure again. This reflects the principle that Islamic law does not burden individuals beyond their capacity.

At the same time, zakat carries a profound spiritual dimension. Linguistically, zakat means growth, increase, and blessing. This indicates that zakat is not a reduction of wealth, but rather a cause of its blessing and growth. Wealth from which zakat is paid becomes purified, and its owner receives reward and the prayers of those who benefit from it. In this way, zakat purifies the soul from greed, excessive attachment to worldly wealth, and dependence upon material possessions.

Furthermore, zakat functions as a mechanism for economic redistribution within society. Through zakat, wealth does not circulate only among the rich but also reaches the poor and those in need. This shows that zakat is not merely an individual act of worship, but also part of a broader social system that preserves balance in society.

The detailed rulings concerning zakat on different types of wealth—such as gold, silver, crops, fruits, and trade goods—also demonstrate the comprehensiveness of Islamic jurisprudence in regulating economic life. Each type of wealth has distinct regulations based on its nature and benefits. Wealth that constitutes essential human needs, such as staple foods, receives particular attention. Similarly, wealth that grows through trade or livestock is subject to zakat because of its potential for growth.

From these explanations, it becomes clear that zakat integrates three major dimensions: worship of Allah, purification of the soul, and social justice. It not only trains individuals to obey Allah but also helps build a society characterized by mutual care, cooperation, and protection from harmful economic disparities.

For this reason, learning the rulings of zakat is not only the responsibility of scholars but also an obligation for every Muslim who possesses wealth. By understanding the laws of zakat correctly, a person can fulfill this obligation properly, protect their wealth from sin, and contribute to building a just and prosperous society.

Ultimately, zakat is not merely an annual duty, but part of the comprehensive system of Islam that unites worship, morality, and social justice. It is a means of drawing closer to Allah, purifying wealth, increasing blessing, and strengthening the bonds of brotherhood within society. Whoever fulfills it with awareness and sincerity has upheld one of the greatest pillars of Islam and contributed to maintaining balance in the life of the Muslim community. And Allah knows best.
