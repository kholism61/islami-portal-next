### Nisab of Gold and Silver

The initial nisab of gold is as stated by the scholars:

> وَأَوَّلُ نِصَابِ الذَّهَبِ عِشْرُونَ مِثْقَالًا، وَفِيهِ رُبُعُ الْعُشْرِ وَهُوَ نِصْفُ مِثْقَالٍ، وَفِيمَا زَادَ فَبِحِسَابِهِ، وَنِصَابُ الْوَرِقِ مِائَتَا دِرْهَمٍ، وَفِيهَا رُبُعُ الْعُشْرِ وَهُوَ خَمْسَةُ دَرَاهِمَ، وَفِيمَا زَادَ فَبِحِسَابِهِ

This means that the nisab of gold is **twenty mithqāl**, and its zakat is **one-fortieth (2.5%)**, which equals **half a mithqāl**. Any amount exceeding this is calculated proportionally.  
The nisab of silver is **two hundred dirhams**, and its zakat is also **one-fortieth (2.5%)**, which equals **five dirhams**, and anything beyond that is calculated accordingly.

The obligation of zakat on gold and silver is established by the **Qur’an, the Sunnah, and the consensus (ijma’) of the Muslim community**.

### Evidence from the Qur’an

> وَالَّذِينَ يَكْنِزُونَ الذَّهَبَ وَالْفِضَّةَ وَلَا يُنفِقُونَهَا فِي سَبِيلِ اللَّهِ فَبَشِّرْهُم بِعَذَابٍ أَلِيمٍ

“And those who hoard gold and silver and do not spend it in the way of Allah—give them tidings of a painful punishment.”

(QS. At-Taubah: 34)

The meaning of **hoarding (kanz)** here refers to wealth upon which zakat has not been paid.

### Evidence from the Sunnah

> مَا مِنْ صَاحِبِ ذَهَبٍ وَلَا فِضَّةٍ لَا يُؤَدِّي مِنْهَا حَقَّهَا إِلَّا إِذَا كَانَ يَوْمَ الْقِيَامَةِ صُفِحَتْ لَهُ صَفَائِحُ مِنْ نَارٍ فَأُحْمِيَ عَلَيْهَا فِي نَارِ جَهَنَّمَ فَتُكْوَى بِهَا جَبْهَتُهُ وَجَنْبُهُ وَظَهْرُهُ كُلَّمَا بَرَدَتْ أُعِيدَتْ لَهُ

“There is no owner of gold or silver who does not fulfill its due right except that on the Day of Resurrection plates of fire will be prepared for him. They will be heated in the fire of Hell and used to brand his forehead, his sides, and his back. Whenever they cool down, they will be reheated for him.”

In **Sahih Muslim**, it is explained that the “due right” of gold and silver refers to **their zakat**.

It is also reported in a hadith:

> فِي الرِّقَةِ رُبُعُ الْعُشْرِ

“For silver (and gold), the obligation is one-fortieth.”

The term **riqqah** refers to gold and silver.

### Scholarly Consensus and Weight Standards

Ibn al-Mundhir reported a scholarly consensus that the **nisab of silver is two hundred dirhams** and the **nisab of gold is twenty mithqāl**, when the value of gold equals two hundred dirhams. This is because during the time of the Prophet ﷺ, **one dinar was equal to twelve and a half dirhams**.

The price of gold may rise or fall, but this ruling remains agreed upon. There is no distinction between coins and other forms of gold and silver.

A **dirham equals six daniq**, and **every ten dirhams equal seven mithqāl of gold**. This measurement functions as a strict threshold; therefore, if the amount falls short of the nisab even by a grain or part of a grain, no zakat is due, even if the quality is excellent.

If the nisab is incomplete according to one scale but complete according to another, the correct opinion is that **zakat is not due**.

### Conditions and Rules for Mixed Metals

The wealth must reach the nisab, be owned for a full **lunar year (haul)**, and the gold or silver must be **pure**. Therefore, zakat is not due on mixed metals until the pure gold reaches **twenty mithqāl** and the pure silver reaches **two hundred dirhams**.

If zakat is paid from mixed silver, the amount of pure silver must equal the required amount. Thus, if someone gives **five mixed dirhams** from **two hundred pure dirhams**, it does not suffice.

If a person possesses **two hundred mixed dirhams** that do not contain the required amount of pure silver, no zakat is due. However, if the mixture contains the equivalent of the pure silver nisab, zakat becomes obligatory and must be paid at **2.5% of the pure amount**.

The phrase **“وَفِيمَا زَادَ فَبِحِسَابِهِ”** indicates that any increase above the nisab is also subject to zakat, even if small. This differs from livestock, where certain intermediate amounts (awqāṣ) are excused due to shared ownership, whereas gold and silver have no such exemption.

## Permissible Jewelry

As mentioned in **Matan Abi Shuja’**:

> وَلَا تَجِبُ فِي الْحُلِيِّ الْمُبَاحِ زَكَاةٌ

“There is no zakat on permissible jewelry.”

There are **two scholarly opinions** regarding this matter.

The first opinion states that zakat is obligatory on it, based on the hadith that a woman came to the Prophet ﷺ while her daughter wore **two thick gold bracelets**. The Prophet ﷺ asked:

“Do you pay zakat on them?”  
She replied, “No.”  
He said:  
“Would you be pleased if Allah were to place two bracelets of fire upon you on the Day of Resurrection?”

So she removed them and said:  
“They are for Allah and His Messenger.”  
(Narrated by Abu Dawud with a sound chain)

The second opinion—and the stronger one adopted by the Shaykh—is that **zakat is not obligatory on permissible jewelry**, because it is prepared for lawful use and resembles working camels and cattle.

Imam Malik narrated in **Al-Muwatta’** with a sound chain from **Ibn Umar and Aisha** that Aisha adorned her orphan nieces under her care with jewelry and **did not pay zakat on it**.

The first hadith is interpreted in two ways:  
1. In the early period of Islam, jewelry was temporarily prohibited for women.  
2. The hadith refers to jewelry involving **extravagance**, as indicated by the phrase “two thick bracelets.”

Thus, jewelry involving excess or extravagance becomes **impermissible**, and zakat is obligatory on it.

Jewelry that is **haram** requires zakat by scholarly consensus, as affirmed by **Imam al-Nawawi**.

This includes items inherently prohibited, such as **gold or silver vessels, spoons, incense burners, and kohl containers**, as well as items prohibited by intention—such as a man intending to wear women's jewelry or giving it to a male servant.

If a person owns jewelry and intends to **store it**, the majority of scholars say zakat is obligatory.  
If he intends to **rent it out**, the stronger opinion states that zakat is not required, since the intention is to earn profit, similar to working animals.

If jewelry breaks but can still be used, it has no effect.  
If it breaks and cannot be used until repaired, zakat becomes obligatory and the haul begins from the time it broke.

Women are permitted to wear **gold and silver jewelry**, such as necklaces, bracelets, anklets, and amulets. There is scholarly disagreement regarding **gold or silver sandals**, but the stronger opinion permits them.

Regarding **pierced dinars or dirhams used as necklaces**, there are two opinions. In *Asl al-Rawdah* the stronger opinion states it is prohibited, but in *Sharh al-Muhadhdhab* the stronger view allows it, and it is confirmed as permissible for women without dislike as mentioned in *Al-Bahr*.

> والله أعلم


## Nisab of Agricultural Produce and Fruits

As stated in the text:

> وَنِصَابُ الزُّرُوعِ وَالثِّمَارِ خَمْسَةُ أَوْسُقٍ، قَدْرُهَا أَلْفُ وَسِتُّمِائَةِ رَطْلٍ بِالْبَغْدَادِيِّ، وَفِيمَا زَادَ فَبِحِسَابِهِ

The nisab for crops and fruits is **five wasq**, equivalent to **1,600 Baghdad ratl**, and anything beyond that is calculated proportionally.

### Evidence for the Five Wasq Nisab

As mentioned in **Sahih al-Bukhari and Muslim**:

> لَيْسَ فِيمَا دُونَ خَمْسَةِ أَوْسُقٍ مِنَ التَّمْرِ صَدَقَةٌ

“There is no zakat on dates less than five wasq.”

In another narration:

> لَيْسَ فِي حَبٍّ وَلَا تَمْرٍ صَدَقَةٌ حَتَّى يَبْلُغَ خَمْسَةَ أَوْسُقٍ

“There is no zakat on grains or dates until they reach five wasq.”

Ibn Hibban added in his Sahih:

> الوَسْقُ سِتُّونَ صَاعًا

“One wasq equals sixty sa‘.”

Thus, five wasq equals **three hundred sa‘**.

### Measurement Differences

One sa‘ equals **four mudd**, making five wasq equal to **1,200 mudd**. One mudd equals one ratl, resulting in **1,600 Baghdad ratl**.

The Baghdad ratl is used because it is the **recognized legal measure**.

### Increase Beyond the Nisab

The phrase:

> وَفِيمَا زَادَ فَبِحِسَابِهِ

indicates that any increase above the nisab is subject to zakat in proportion to the increase, just as in the case of gold and silver.

### The Ruling on the Yield of Waqf Property

As for the produce of villages and fruits of orchards that are endowed (waqf) for mosques, frontier ribāṭs, schools, bridges, or for the poor without specifying a particular owner, then no zakat is due on them because they do not belong to a specific owner. This is the correct opinion and is in fact the view of the majority of scholars.

However, if the waqf is designated for specific individuals, then zakat becomes obligatory if the produce reaches the nisab. For example, if the date trees of an endowed orchard produce five wasq, then zakat must be paid.

If forty goats are endowed to a specific group of people, then if it is said that ownership does not transfer in waqf, there is no zakat. If it is said that they do own them, then according to the stronger opinion there is still no zakat because their ownership is considered weak.

> والله أعلم


## Irrigation of Crops

As mentioned in the matn:

> وَفِيهَا إِنْ سُقِيَتْ بِمَاءِ السَّمَاءِ أَوِ السَّيْحِ الْعُشْرُ، وَإِنْ سُقِيَتْ بِدَوَالِيبَ أَوْ غَرْبٍ نِصْفُ الْعُشْرِ

This means that if crops are irrigated by rainwater or naturally flowing water, then the zakat is **one-tenth (10%)**. If they are irrigated using water wheels or large buckets, then the zakat is **half of one-tenth (5%)**.

### Crops Obligated with 10%

A zakat rate of **10%** is obligatory on crops and fruits irrigated by rainwater and similar natural sources such as snow water or irrigation water that flows across the land due to river channels.

The same applies to **ba‘l crops**, which are plants whose roots absorb water from nearby rivers or underground water sources.

### Crops Obligated with 5%

Crops that are irrigated through effort—such as by animals, water wheels, purchased water, or large buckets—are subject to **5% zakat**.

The difference between the two is that the first type involves **no cost**, whereas the second requires **effort and expense**.

### Evidence from Hadith

> فِيمَا سَقَتِ السَّمَاءُ وَالْعُيُونُ أَوْ كَانَ عَثَرِيًّا الْعُشْرُ، وَفِيمَا سُقِيَ بِالنَّضْحِ نِصْفُ الْعُشْرِ

“On crops watered by rain, springs, or natural groundwater (‘athari), the zakat is one-tenth. On crops irrigated by manual effort, the zakat is half of one-tenth.”

> فِيمَا سَقَتِ الْأَنْهَارُ وَالْغَيْمُ الْعُشْرُ، وَفِيمَا سُقِيَ بِالسَّاقِيَةِ نِصْفُ الْعُشْرِ

“On crops irrigated by rivers and rain clouds, the zakat is one-tenth; and on crops irrigated by water wheels, the zakat is half of one-tenth.”

> فِي الْبَعْلِ الْعُشْرُ

“On ba‘l crops (those irrigated naturally without cost), the zakat is one-tenth.”

There is consensus on this ruling as reported by **Al-Bayhaqi** and others.

The term **‘athari** refers to crops that rely solely on rainwater, with trenches dug so that rainwater flows to their roots.

### If Irrigated by Two Methods

If crops are irrigated using two methods—one requiring 10% and the other 5%—in equal proportion, the zakat is **7.5%**.

If one method is more dominant, the stronger opinion is still to divide the rate equally. If it is unknown which method predominates, the ruling remains **7.5%** because neither method has precedence.

However, if it is known that one method predominates but its exact proportion is unknown, then the amount taken should be between **5% and 10%** until the matter becomes clear, as stated by **Al-Mawardi**.

> والله أعلم


## Nisab of Trade Goods

As stated in the matn:

> وَتُقَوَّمُ عُرُوضُ التِّجَارَةِ عِنْدَ آخِرِ الْحَوْلِ بِمَا اشْتُرِيَتْ بِهِ وَيُخْرَجُ مِنْ ذَلِكَ رُبُعُ الْعُشْرِ

Trade goods are evaluated at the **end of the haul (year)** according to their selling value, and **one-fortieth (2.5%)** is taken from that value.

It is well known that both **nisab and haul** are considered in trade zakat, and there is no dispute regarding these conditions.

### The Time Considered for the Haul

Scholars differed regarding which moment of the year should be considered. The stronger opinion is that **the end of the year** is the determining point, because zakat is tied to the value of the goods rather than the goods themselves.

Another opinion states that the entire year must be considered, while another says only the **beginning and end of the year** are relevant.

### Method of Valuation

If trade goods were purchased with **dirhams or dinars** that already reached the nisab, they are valued in that currency at the end of the year.

If the value reaches the nisab and a full year has passed, zakat becomes obligatory. Otherwise, no zakat is due.

If the capital was **below the nisab**, it is still valued in the same currency according to the stronger opinion.

If the capital was in **non-monetary goods**, then the goods are valued according to the common currency of the region.

### Beginning of the Haul

The beginning of the haul is determined based on the **initial capital**.

If the capital consisted of currency that already reached the nisab, then the haul of trade goods follows the haul of that currency.

If the capital consisted of goods below the nisab, then the haul begins when those goods become trade goods.

### Livestock as Trade Capital

If the trade capital consists of grazing livestock that already reached the nisab, some scholars say the livestock haul continues.

However, the stronger opinion is that the livestock haul ends and the **trade haul begins**, because the rules differ between livestock zakat and trade zakat.

### Selling During the Haul

If goods are sold during the year for currency below the nisab and new goods are purchased with that money, the stronger opinion is that **the haul is interrupted** and a new haul begins.

Another opinion states that the haul continues because trade exchange is still part of commercial activity.

## Minerals and Rikaz (Buried Treasure)

As mentioned in the matn:

> وَمَا اسْتُخْرِجَ مِنْ مَعَادِنِ الذَّهَبِ وَالْفِضَّةِ يُخْرَجُ مِنْهُ رُبُعُ الْعُشْرِ فِي الْحَالِ

Gold and silver extracted from mines require **2.5% zakat immediately**.

Also stated:

> وَمَا يُوجَدُ مِنَ الرِّكَازِ فَفِيهِ الْخُمُسُ

Buried treasure requires **one-fifth (20%)**.

### Definition of Minerals

**Ma‘ādin (minerals)** are places in which Allah has placed valuable materials such as gold, silver, iron, copper, and others.

According to **Imam al-Nawawi**, there is scholarly consensus on the obligation of zakat on mined gold and silver.

### Conditions of Mining Zakat

The extracted minerals must reach the **nisab**, but **no haul is required** because extraction itself represents growth similar to crops.

### Definition of Rikaz

**Rikaz** refers to buried wealth from the **pre-Islamic (Jahiliyyah) era**, and its zakat is **one-fifth**, based on the hadith:

> وَفِي الرِّكَازِ الْخُمُسُ

### Difference Between Rikaz and Lost Property

If the buried wealth bears signs of Islam, such as Qur’anic inscriptions, it is treated as **lost property (luqāṭah)** and must be announced for **one year**.

If the owner is known, it must be returned immediately.

If the treasure bears no identifiable signs of either Islam or pre-Islamic origin, the stronger opinion is to treat it as **lost property**, giving precedence to the assumption of Islamic ownership.

> والله أعلم


## Conclusion

From the discussions above, it becomes clear that zakat has very detailed regulations in Islamic law regarding nisab, rates, conditions of haul, types of wealth, and circumstances affecting obligation.

The nisab of gold and silver is set at **twenty mithqal and two hundred dirhams** with a zakat rate of **2.5%**. Agricultural produce requires **five wasq**, with a rate of **10% or 5%** depending on irrigation. Trade goods are assessed at the end of the year with **2.5% zakat**. Minerals require immediate zakat without waiting for a haul, and rikaz requires **one-fifth**.

All of these rulings are established through the **Qur’an, the Sunnah, scholarly consensus, and the juristic reasoning of the great imams of the madhhabs**.

This demonstrates the perfection of Islamic law in preserving the rights of Allah and the rights of the poor, as well as the precision of the jurists in deriving legal rulings from strong evidences and principles.
