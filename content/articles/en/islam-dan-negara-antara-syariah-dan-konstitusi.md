## Introduction

The relationship between Islam and the state is one of the most important and sensitive issues in modern Islamic thought. On the one hand, Islam is believed to be a religion that brings the sharia as a guide for life. On the other hand, the modern state stands upon a constitution that regulates collective life in a plural society. It is this tension between the concept of sharia and the constitution that often gives rise to long debates among ulama, academics, and political activists.

In Islamic history, the question of the state was never separated from religious values. However, Islam also did not leave behind a fixed institutional blueprint concerning the form of the state. Therefore, understanding the relationship between sharia and the constitution requires historical, normative, and contextual approaches at the same time.

This article seeks to explain how sharia was understood in the classical tradition (*turas*), how the concept of the constitution developed in the modern state, and how the two may be brought together in a rational and beneficial way.

## Sharia as a System of Values, Not Merely Positive Law

In the classical Islamic tradition, sharia was not understood merely as a collection of written statutes. Sharia is the divine will whose purpose is to guide human beings toward welfare in this world and the Hereafter. The laws of fiqh are only the result of human ijtihad in understanding sharia, not sharia itself.

The scholars of *usul al-fiqh* emphasized that the essence of sharia lies in its objectives (*maqasid al-shari‘ah*). These objectives include the preservation of religion, life, intellect, lineage, and property. Thus, sharia is normative and ethical in nature, not merely legalistic.

This understanding is important so that sharia is not reduced to a political symbol or an instrument of state coercion, but instead becomes a source of values for life within the state.

## The State from the Perspective of Turas (Classical Tradition)

Within the treasury of Islamic *turas*, discussions about the state were never separated from the question of the welfare of the ummah. Classical ulama did not begin political discussion with the question of the form of the state, but rather with the human need for authority capable of maintaining social order and enforcing the law. The state was viewed as a means, not an end.

According to Al-Mawardi, the primary purposes of the state include safeguarding religion so that it may be practiced freely, establishing justice among human beings, protecting life and property, and managing public affairs. The state also functions to prevent oppression, whether committed by individuals or by powerful groups against the weak. Thus, the legitimacy of the state is measured by its ability to carry out these functions, not merely by its religious symbolism.

Interestingly, Al-Mawardi did not determine one particular form of government as the only legitimate one. He discussed the mechanism of appointing a leader, the conditions required of an imam, and the relationship between ruler and people, while still leaving room for flexibility according to social and political conditions. This shows that from the very beginning, Islamic political thought was adaptive and realistic.

The ulama agreed that human beings are by nature social creatures who need rules and leadership. Without legitimate authority, the law cannot be enforced, rights cannot be protected, and conflict will develop without control. Therefore, the existence of the state or political authority is considered both a rational necessity (*darurah ‘aqliyyah*) and a shar‘i necessity (*darurah shar‘iyyah*). In *Al-Ahkam al-Sultaniyyah*, Al-Mawardi explains that the imamah (state leadership) is established to replace the prophetic function in safeguarding religion and administering worldly affairs. This statement shows that the state is not a sacred institution standing on its own, but rather an instrument to continue the social function of prophethood after the passing of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, not the function of revelation.

Ibn Taymiyyah deepened this perspective with a more pragmatic approach. In *As-Siyasah Asy-Syar‘iyyah*, he affirms that the purpose of political authority is to establish justice and prevent corruption. He even states that Allah supports a just state even if it is unbelieving, and does not support an oppressive state even if it is Muslim. This statement underscores that justice is the essence of political legitimacy in Islam. For Ibn Taymiyyah, the existence of a ruler—even one who has shortcomings—is better than the total absence of authority. Social chaos, in his view, constitutes a greater evil than partial injustice. This view shows that the classical ulama gave serious consideration to the actual social consequences of political policy, not merely to normative idealism.

Other scholars such as Al-Ghazali also emphasized the close relationship between religion and the state. He stated that religion and authority are twin brothers: religion is the foundation, while the state is its guardian. Without the state, religion is difficult to practice collectively; without religion, the state loses its moral direction. However, this statement was not intended to legitimize a theocratic state, but rather to emphasize the importance of ethics in power. In *turas*, the state was also not positioned as the highest source of law. Law still originated from sharia, but its application took place through human ijtihad that was greatly influenced by social context. For this reason, the fuqaha distinguished between fixed principles (*thawabit*) and flexible areas (*mutaghayyirat*). Politics and state administration largely fall into this flexible domain.

This concept allows the state to adapt its public policies according to the needs of the age, so long as they do not contradict the principles of justice and welfare. This is what later became known as *siyasah shar‘iyyah*, namely political policy aligned with the aims of sharia even if not explicitly mentioned in the textual sources. From the perspective of *turas*, it may be concluded that the state in Islam is not a rigid ideological institution, but a social instrument charged with preserving order, establishing justice, and protecting the welfare of the ummah. The primary focus of the classical ulama was not on the symbol of an Islamic state, but on the function and moral responsibility of power.

This understanding becomes an important foundation in the dialogue between Islam and the state in the modern era. It shows that flexibility, justice, and welfare are an authentic legacy of Islamic political thought, not foreign concepts imposed from outside.

## Constitution in the Modern State

A constitution is the highest basic law in the modern state. It regulates the distribution of power, the rights and obligations of citizens, and the basic principles of state administration. In the context of the nation-state, the constitution functions as a social contract agreed upon collectively.

Modern states are generally plural, whether in terms of religion, ethnicity, or worldview. Therefore, constitutions are designed to ensure justice and the protection of rights for all citizens, without discrimination.

The question that often arises is: does the constitution contradict sharia? Or can the two be reconciled?

## Sharia and the Constitution: Conflict or Integration?

Debates over the relationship between sharia and the constitution often begin with the assumption that they are two legal systems that stand in opposition to one another. Sharia is viewed as divine law that is absolute in nature, while the constitution is regarded as a human product that is relative and changeable. From this assumption arises the notion that accepting the modern constitution means removing sharia from the public sphere.

However, such a way of seeing things is not entirely in line with either the Islamic scholarly tradition or historical reality. In Islamic *turas*, the ulama never understood sharia as a “state law code” in the modern technical sense. Sharia was understood as a set of divine values, principles, and objectives, while the formulation of positive law always involved human ijtihad.

In Islamic historical practice, there was never a single fixed constitutional document that applied universally and permanently. Even the system of government in the time of the Rightly Guided Caliphs was contextual and developed according to circumstances. This shows that legal and constitutional flexibility is an inherent part of the Islamic tradition.

Some groups who regard sharia and the constitution as irreconcilable usually depart from a legalistic-formalist approach. In this view, sharia must be implemented literally as state law, while the constitution is seen as an obstacle because it does not derive directly from revelation. This approach often ignores the fundamental distinction between sharia as revelation and fiqh as the result of ijtihad.

The scholars of *usul al-fiqh* emphasized that operational laws are always born from the process of human reasoning over the texts. Therefore, no human legal system is entirely free from relativity, including political fiqh. In this context, the constitution may be understood as a form of modern collective ijtihad for regulating life together.

From the perspective of *maqasid al-shari‘ah*, the more important question is not whether the constitution is formally “Islamic,” but whether it is capable of realizing justice, protecting fundamental rights, and preventing oppression. If these purposes are achieved, then such a constitution is in harmony with the spirit of sharia.

Classical thinkers such as Ibn Taymiyyah emphasized that the ruler’s policy is deemed legitimate so long as it brings welfare and does not contradict the basic principles of religion. The concept of *siyasah shar‘iyyah* that he developed grants legitimacy to public policy not explicitly mentioned in the textual sources, so long as its purpose is just and beneficial.

In the modern context, the constitution functions as the basic framework for organizing power, limiting the authority of rulers, and protecting the rights of citizens. This function is in fact in line with the objective of sharia in preventing tyranny and safeguarding human dignity. In other words, constitutionalism can become an instrument for realizing the values of sharia, not an obstacle to them.

Contemporary Muslim thinkers such as Yusuf al-Qaradawi emphasize that the state in Islam is not a theocracy, but a civil state founded upon Islamic moral values. Within this framework, the constitution becomes a legal instrument to translate those values into an accountable system of government.

Meanwhile, Abdullah Ahmed An-Na‘im stresses the importance of a secular constitution in the sense of religious neutrality, so that the state does not impose one particular interpretation of religion upon all citizens. Nevertheless, he still leaves room for Islamic values to play a role in the public sphere through democratic process and the ethical consciousness of society.

This integrative approach views sharia and the constitution not as two poles that negate one another, but as two different levels. Sharia functions as a source of values and moral orientation, while the constitution functions as an institutional mechanism for organizing life together within a plural society. With this approach, conflict between sharia and the constitution is not something inevitable. Conflict actually arises when sharia is reduced to a political symbol or when the constitution is completely separated from moral values. Their integration requires intellectual maturity, openness to ijtihad, and commitment to substantive justice.

In the end, the question of “conflict or integration” depends greatly on how sharia itself is understood. If sharia is understood as purpose and value, then integration with the constitution is not only possible but also necessary. But if sharia is understood merely as rigid positive law, then conflict becomes almost unavoidable. Thus, integration between sharia and the constitution represents the most realistic middle path and one most in harmony with the Islamic scholarly tradition. It preserves the sacredness of religious values while at the same time recognizing the social and political realities of modern society.

## The Maqasid Approach in the Relationship Between Sharia and the State

The *maqasid al-shari‘ah* approach offers the most mature framework for understanding the relationship between sharia and the state. Rather than stopping at debate over institutional form or formal legality, this approach directs attention toward the fundamental objectives of Islamic law in social and political life. Within the framework of maqasid, the state is understood as a historical means to realize welfare, not as an ideological end standing on its own.

In the *turas* tradition, although the term *maqasid al-shari‘ah* was only systematized by later scholars, the substance of maqasid had already been strongly present in the thought of classical ulama, including Muslim political thinkers and sociologists. One of the most important figures in this context is Ibn Khaldun.

In *al-Muqaddimah*, Ibn Khaldun does not discuss the state solely from a normative-legal perspective, but from the perspective of social reality (*‘umran*). He affirms that the state is born from the human need to live together and protect themselves from one another’s ظلم. Political authority emerges as a natural mechanism for preserving order, not as a purely theological project. This view is very much in line with the maqasid of sharia, especially the preservation of life, property, and social stability. Ibn Khaldun also emphasizes that the main purpose of political authority is to preserve civilization (*hifz al-‘umran*). When the state fails to carry out this function—for example through oppression, excessive taxation, or corruption—civilization will collapse. This analysis shows that the legitimacy of the state is measured not by formal religious claims, but by its ability to protect the public welfare. This is the essence of the maqasid approach in the sociological perspective of Islam.

Furthermore, Ibn Khaldun criticizes the tendency of rulers to use law and religion as tools of domination. According to him, laws that do not consider social realities and the capacity of society will in fact damage the very objectives of sharia. Here it is clearly seen that maqasid requires a balance between normative ideals and the actual conditions of society. This approach later received a more systematic methodological foundation through Abu Ishaq al-Shatibi in *al-Muwafaqat*. Al-Shatibi affirms that the whole of sharia was revealed to realize human welfare. The state, law, and public policy must be measured according to the extent to which they realize those objectives.

According to al-Shatibi, a law that is literally in accordance with the text but produces social harm contradicts the maqasid of sharia. This principle has major implications in the context of the state: constitutions, statutes, and public policies are not sufficiently judged by Islamic symbols or terminology, but by their impact on justice, welfare, and social stability. Within this framework, the modern constitutional state may be accepted in shar‘i terms if it is capable of safeguarding the five primary objectives of sharia. The constitution functions as an instrument to limit power, prevent tyranny, and guarantee the rights of citizens—all of which are in harmony with the maqasid of sharia. The maqasid approach thus opens space for the integration of sharia and the constitution without negating either one.

Other classical scholars such as Imam Al-Ghazali also affirmed that the purpose of sharia is to preserve the order of human life. He stated that religion and the state need one another: religion provides moral direction, while the state ensures that those values can be practiced collectively. Yet Al-Ghazali also warned of the danger of politicizing religion in ways that ultimately damage the objectives of sharia. In the political context, maqasid also functions as a tool of critique against power. A state that claims to implement sharia but fails to uphold justice, oppresses its people, or damages the social order is, in substance, in contradiction with the maqasid of sharia. This approach leaves room for moral correction of the state without requiring rejection of the institution of the state itself.

Contemporary thinkers have further developed this approach in order to answer the challenges of the modern state. They affirm that maqasid allows sharia to function as a public morality, rather than as coercive state law. In this way, Islamic values can inspire constitutions and policies without creating conflict in a plural society. Thus, the maqasid approach—as reflected in the thought of Ibn Khaldun, al-Shatibi, and other ulama—shows that the relationship between sharia and the state is dynamic and contextual. The state is a historical instrument for preserving civilization and public welfare. So long as this objective is achieved, the form and system of the state may vary.

This approach also emphasizes that integration between sharia and the state does not lie in symbolic formalization, but in the realization of the values of justice, humanity, and welfare. This is the most relevant intellectual legacy of Islamic *turas* for answering the challenges of statehood in the modern era.

## Views of Contemporary Thinkers

Contemporary Muslim thinkers generally begin from the awareness that the socio-political context of the Muslim ummah has changed drastically compared to the classical period. The nation-state, written constitutions, democracy, human rights, and plural societies are realities that cannot be avoided. Therefore, literal and formal approaches to sharia are no longer considered sufficient to address the challenges of the age. Many contemporary thinkers agree that the main problem in the relationship between Islam and the state does not lie in sharia itself, but in the way sharia is understood and operationalized in the political sphere. They make a clear distinction between sharia as a divine value and fiqh as a historical human product.

Yusuf al-Qaradawi is one of the figures who firmly emphasizes this approach. In his work *Fiqh al-Dawlah fi al-Islam*, he rejects the concept of a theocratic state that places the ruler as the representative of God. According to him, the state in Islam is a *dawlah madaniyyah*—a civil state—based on Islamic values, but managed by human beings through mechanisms of consultation, law, and public accountability. Al-Qaradawi emphasizes that the implementation of sharia must pay attention to the *maqasid al-shari‘ah* and to the social conditions of society. A law that is formally “Islamic” but gives rise to injustice, fear, or social division is in fact contrary to the objectives of sharia. Thus, the modern constitution can become a legitimate instrument for realizing Islamic values in a substantive way. A similar view is developed by Wahbah al-Zuhayli, who stresses the importance of a moderate and contextual fiqh approach. He affirms that Islamic law must not be detached from the principles of justice, welfare, and universal humanity. In the context of the state, al-Zuhayli sees the constitution as a means of organizing public life justly, so long as it does not conflict with the basic principles of sharia.

On the other hand, thinkers such as Taha Abdurrahman highlight the ethical dimension of Islamic politics. He criticizes the reduction of sharia to merely state law and insists that Islamic moral values must live within the consciousness of society, not be imposed through the apparatus of power. According to him, the state can function Islamically only if its society possesses a strong ethical consciousness. A more critical approach toward the religious state is presented by Abdullahi Ahmed An-Na‘im. He argues that the state must not impose sharia as positive law, because every attempt at formalization will inevitably involve human interpretation that is political in nature. According to An-Na‘im, a secular constitution—in the sense of being neutral toward religion—actually provides the safest space for Muslims to practice sharia freely and authentically. However, the secularism intended by An-Na‘im is not anti-religious secularism. He emphasizes that Islamic values can still play a role in the public sphere through democracy, moral advocacy, and civic participation. In this way, sharia functions as a source of ethical inspiration, not as a coercive instrument of the state.

Other thinkers such as Mohammad Hashim Kamali develop the maqasid approach systematically in the context of modern law and constitutions. Kamali affirms that constitutionalism, the limitation of power, and the protection of human rights are integral parts of the objectives of sharia. A state that fails to protect the rights of its citizens, even if it claims to implement sharia, is substantively in contradiction with Islam. In the context of the contemporary Muslim world, these views indicate an important shift: from a formalistic approach to a substantive one. The focus is no longer on the symbol of an “Islamic state,” but on the quality of justice, good governance, and the protection of human dignity. The main common ground among these contemporary thinkers is their rejection of political absolutism in the name of religion. They agree that the total fusion of religion and state risks producing authoritarianism, while total separation risks removing the moral orientation of public life. The middle path they propose is the integration of Islamic values into a constitutional system in a democratic and dignified manner.

Thus, the views of contemporary thinkers enrich the discourse on Islam and the state with approaches that are more realistic, ethical, and contextual. They continue the spirit of the classical *turas*—such as Ibn Khaldun and al-Shatibi—in forms that are relevant to the challenges of the modern age. This approach emphasizes that the future of the relationship between sharia and the state does not lie in ideological conflict, but in the ability of Muslims to formulate the values of their religion within the framework of a constitution that is just, inclusive, and oriented toward the common good.

## Islam, the Constitution, and a Plural Society

The reality of a plural society is unavoidable in modern life. Differences of religion, ethnicity, culture, and worldview are social facts that cannot be erased, but must instead be managed justly. In this context, the relationship between Islam, the constitution, and plurality becomes a crucial issue, because it concerns how religious values interact with the state legal system that encompasses all citizens. In the Islamic perspective, plurality is not a foreign phenomenon. The Qur’an explicitly recognizes human diversity as part of the will of God. Difference is understood as a means for mutual recognition, not as a reason for mutual negation. Therefore, Islam does not build social order upon homogeneity, but upon justice and moral responsibility.

The strongest historical foundation for the relationship between Islam and plural society may be found in the Constitution of Medina. This document was drafted by Muhammad ﷺ to regulate life together among Muslims, Jews, and other groups in Medina. It is often referred to as the first written constitution in Islamic history. The Constitution of Medina shows that the state led by Prophet Muhammad ﷺ was not built upon religious coercion. Each group was recognized in its identity and belief, and had equal rights and obligations in maintaining collective security and order. The principles of justice and social agreement formed the main foundation, not the domination of one group over another.

Within this framework, Islam appears as a source of public ethics, not as an instrument of exclusivism. The state functions as the guardian of the social contract that protects all citizens, without erasing their respective religious identities. This model becomes an important precedent for the concept of an inclusive constitutional state. In the context of the modern state, the constitution holds a role similar to that of the Constitution of Medina, namely as the basic agreement binding together all elements of society. The constitution is not intended to replace religion, but to guarantee justice, order, and the protection of citizens’ rights in a plural society.

For Muslims, the main challenge is not the existence of the constitution itself, but rather how Islamic values can be presented constructively within a constitutional framework. This is where the *maqasid al-shari‘ah* approach becomes relevant. So long as the constitution is able to protect life, property, intellect, freedom of religion, and human dignity, then it is in harmony with the objectives of sharia. Islam does not require the state to impose religious belief upon all citizens. On the contrary, Islam emphasizes substantive justice. In a plural society, justice can only be realized if the law is general, non-discriminatory, and protective of minorities from the tyranny of the majority. Contemporary Muslim thinkers affirm that the formalization of sharia in a plural society often in fact triggers social conflict. This is not because the values of sharia are problematic, but because their implementation through state mechanisms tends to ignore differences of interpretation and social conditions. Therefore, the constitution plays an important role as a mediator that guarantees space for peaceful coexistence. From this perspective, Islam and the constitution do not stand in a competitive relationship, but in a complementary one. Islam provides moral orientation, while the constitution provides the legal framework that makes it possible for those values to be realized without coercion. This relationship ensures that religion is not excessively politicized and that the state does not lose its ethical direction.

The experience of modern Muslim countries shows that political stability and social harmony are more easily achieved when the state adopts an inclusive approach toward plurality. The protection of religious rights, freedom of expression, and equality before the law are primary prerequisites for the creation of social justice in line with Islamic principles. Thus, Islam in a plural society does not appear as the exclusive identity of the state, but as a source of values that enriches public life. The constitution becomes the shared space in which various values meet, engage in dialogue, and are agreed upon for the sake of collective welfare.

In conclusion, the relationship between Islam, the constitution, and plural society must be built upon the principles of justice, social agreement, and respect for human dignity. The legacy of Islamic *turas*—such as the Constitution of Medina—shows that pluralism is not a threat to Islam, but rather a space for the actualization of the noble values of sharia within state life.

### Conclusion

The relationship between Islam, sharia, and the state cannot be understood simply as a relationship between religion and formal power. In the Islamic tradition—both in the classical *turas* and in contemporary thought—the state is viewed as a means to realize public welfare, not as an ideological end that must be sacralized. Sharia itself does not appear as the written constitution of the state, but as a system of values and moral objectives that guide social and political life.

A study of the thought of the classical ulama shows that from the outset Islam emphasized the function of the state, not its form. Al-Mawardi, Ibn Taymiyyah, al-Ghazali, and Ibn Khaldun all agreed that political authority is necessary in order to preserve order, establish justice, and protect society from corruption. However, the legitimacy of the state is not determined by formal religious symbols, but by its ability to realize justice and safeguard civilization.

The *maqasid al-shari‘ah* approach clarifies this relationship further by placing the objectives of sharia as the principal measure in evaluating policies and systems of governance. Through this approach, the modern constitution may be understood as an instrument of collective ijtihad for realizing the values of justice, the protection of rights, and the limitation of power—all of which are in harmony with the objectives of sharia. Thus, the tension between sharia and the constitution is not inevitable, but depends on how each is understood and operationalized.

Contemporary Muslim thinkers then affirm that the formalization of sharia as state law is not the only way—and not even the best way—to bring Islamic values into public life. In a plural society, the substantive approach that places Islam as a source of public ethics and the constitution as a shared legal framework is in fact more capable of guaranteeing justice and social harmony. The historical experience of Islam—as reflected in the Constitution of Medina—shows that plurality is not a threat to Islam, but rather a reality that must be managed through justice and social agreement. A state that is just, inclusive, and constitutional is not the antithesis of Islam, so long as it is able to preserve human dignity and the common good.

Thus, the future of the relationship between Islam and the state does not lie in conflict between sharia and the constitution, but in the ability of Muslims to translate the values of their religion wisely, contextually, and with an orientation toward justice. This is the middle path that is in harmony with the intellectual legacy of Islam while also being relevant to the challenges of statehood in the modern era.
