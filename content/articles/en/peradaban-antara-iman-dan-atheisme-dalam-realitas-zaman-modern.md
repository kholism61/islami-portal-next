## Introduction

This writing portrays the condition of modern civilization as dominated by materialism. The age faced by humanity today is described as a time when matter controls almost every aspect of life. Everything is measured by worldly standards: physical pleasure, wealth, power, and enjoyment. In such a condition, the spiritual dimension, faith in Allah, and belief in the Last Day are increasingly pushed away from the center of human life.

The materialism referred to here is not merely the use of objects or technology, but rather a worldview that places the body above the soul, the world above the Hereafter, and temporary pleasure above eternal truth. Even religion, within the materialistic view, is accepted only as a moral value or social advice, not as a system of belief that governs human life comprehensively.

As a result, faith, which should be the foundation of human life, is replaced by artificial beliefs. Human beings still need something to believe in, but when faith in Allah and the Last Day is abandoned, they will replace it with ideologies, philosophies, or systems of life not founded upon revelation.

## The Dominance of Matter and a Directionless Life

The modern age that we live in today is often viewed as a period of great progress in science, technology, and material prosperity. Human beings are able to penetrate the skies, explore the depths of the oceans, and master various forces of nature that were previously unimaginable. Yet behind all this progress lies one deeply alarming reality: the domination of matter over human life, to the point that spiritual values, faith, and the true purpose of life are pushed to the margins of civilization.

What is meant by the dominance of matter is not merely increasing prosperity or the development of technology. The dominance of matter is a condition in which human life is measured entirely by physical and worldly standards. The body is placed before the soul, worldly pleasure is preferred over the happiness of the Hereafter, and everything is judged solely on the basis of material benefit. Spiritual values, faith in Allah, and belief in the Last Day are regarded as private matters with no influence on social life, politics, or civilization.

Within such a mindset, religion is no longer regarded as truth that governs human life, but only as a collection of moral advice, cultural tradition, or psychological comfort. Religion is accepted only insofar as it functions as a “social remedy” that relieves human anxiety, not as divine revelation that directs the course of life. Belief in Allah no longer forms the center of civilization, and faith in the Last Day no longer serves as the controller of human behavior.

As a result, modern humanity walks through life with great activity but without clear direction. The world moves with tremendous force: the economy develops, technology advances, industries move forward, and the global system keeps turning. Yet all of this proceeds without any clear spiritual purpose. Civilization moves rapidly, but it does not know where it is heading.

Human beings are busy thinking about the fuel of material life: food, clothing, entertainment, power, money, and pleasure. Nations compete, make peace, wage war, form alliances, or betray one another, all in order to preserve and enlarge material interests. Science is utilized to the fullest to strengthen this domination. Technology is no longer merely a tool to ease life, but has become a giant machine that accelerates the current of materialism in every aspect of existence.

This condition gives birth to a great illusion. Human beings feel themselves capable of fully mastering the world. When the earth appears beautiful through technological progress and wealth, human beings imagine that they have reached the summit of success. Yet the most fundamental question remains unanswered: what is the purpose of all this? Where is the journey of human civilization heading?

When the human heart is empty of faith in Allah and the Last Day, that emptiness will not remain for long. Human beings cannot live without something they trust and exalt. If they do not believe in Allah, then they will believe in something else: ideology, power, wealth, nation, technology, or even themselves. In reality, human beings cannot live without “something” that they make the center of their belief.

Therefore, when true faith dies, false faiths are born. Human beings begin to believe in ideas that have no solid foundation. They become bound to various philosophies of life that actually make them victims of life rather than masters of it. They become trapped in systems that make them work without purpose, pursue pleasure without satisfaction, and live in luxury without inner peace.

This condition explains why many modern societies appear materially prosperous, but inwardly suffer from emptiness, anxiety, and a crisis of meaning. Rates of depression, suicide, family breakdown, and spiritual emptiness continue to increase, even though technology and wealth continue to grow. This shows that material progress without spiritual direction does not bring true happiness.

In truth, material progress without faith will only accelerate human destruction. Like a person running swiftly without knowing his destination, the faster he runs, the faster he becomes lost. Likewise, a civilization that moves without faith: the more advanced its technology becomes, the greater the destruction it can produce.

Therefore, the greatest problem of the modern age is not the lack of technology or economic weakness, but the loss of spiritual direction. Human beings no longer know why they live, to whom they must return, and what the final purpose of all their activity is. Civilization becomes materially strong, but spiritually fragile.

Without faith in Allah and belief in the Last Day, human life loses its center of gravity. It no longer has a purpose that transcends the world. It lives only for the world, and when that world ends, the whole meaning of life ends with it. This is the condition described as a directionless life: active, advanced, and materially strong, yet empty of true purpose.

Therefore, a healthy civilization is not one that advances only materially, but one that is balanced between matter and spirit, between this world and the Hereafter, between reason and revelation. Without this balance, material progress becomes only a fast vehicle heading toward destruction. *Wallahu a‘lam.*

## The Collapse of Religious Authority in Modern Civilization

One of the most striking symptoms in modern civilization is the collapse of religious authority in regulating human life. If in earlier periods religion served as the center of life’s orientation, the determinant of right and wrong, and the source of law and morality, then in the modern age that position has increasingly weakened, and in many places has almost disappeared altogether.

Religion is no longer seen as revelation that must be obeyed, but only as tradition, cultural symbol, or moral advice that may be accepted or abandoned according to human preference. It is no longer a source of law, no longer regulates economics, no longer directs politics, and no longer shapes civilization. Religion is merely allowed to live within a narrow space: houses of worship, religious ceremonies, and private affairs. Beyond that, life proceeds according to other rules that are entirely material and secular.

This condition did not occur suddenly. It is the result of a long process that lasted for centuries, especially in the Western world. When science developed rapidly and rationalism began to dominate thought, some religious teachings that had undergone distortion were unable to stand firmly before the challenge of reason. As a result, conflict between the church and science produced suspicion toward religion as a whole.

From here emerged the view that religion must be separated from public life. Religion came to be regarded as a matter of the heart and emotion, not a matter of the state, society, or civilization. This view later developed into secularism, namely a system of life that separates religion from all public aspects.

In such a system, the state no longer bases its policies on religious teachings. Laws are made based solely on human interests. The economy runs according to the logic of profit without regard for what is lawful and unlawful. Media, education, and culture are built upon unrestricted freedom, not upon divine values.

As a result, modern society has undergone a major change in the way it views religion. Religion is no longer regarded as a source of absolute truth, but merely as a personal option. A person may be religious or not. One may practice religious teachings or reject them. All are considered equal, so long as they do not disturb material interests and social stability.

Under such conditions, faith in Allah is no longer the center of life. Even belief in the Last Day—which once served as the primary restraint upon human behavior—is increasingly forgotten. Human beings no longer feel watched by God, and no longer fear accountability in the Hereafter. What remains is only worldly law, which can be avoided, manipulated, or negotiated.

When the authority of religion collapses, another authority immediately takes over. What replaces religion is not emptiness, but worldly forces: power, money, ideology, or human desire. The state, the market, and the media become the determiners of value. What is profitable is considered good, and what is harmful is considered bad, without any fixed moral measure.

As a result, human life loses any firm standard. The values of right and wrong shift according to interests. Something that was once considered sinful is now regarded as a personal right. Something that was once considered shameful is now promoted as freedom. Changes in values occur so rapidly that human beings no longer possess any stable foundation.

The collapse of religious authority also directly affects social life. The family institution weakens, because moral bonds and spiritual responsibility diminish. Human relationships become more transactional, based on advantage and interests rather than affection and responsibility. Life becomes more individualistic, and human beings become increasingly alienated even while living in the midst of crowds.

In the field of economics, when religion is no longer a reference point, the system that develops is one centered solely upon profit. Usury, economic oppression, and social inequality become commonplace. In politics, power is often exercised without morality, so long as it can preserve stability and the interests of certain groups.

All of this shows that when religion loses its authority, human life does not become better, but instead becomes more chaotic. Material progress indeed increases, but moral and spiritual progress declines. Human beings possess more tools, but less meaning. They have more entertainment, but less tranquility. They have more freedom, but less purpose in life.

At this point, it becomes clear that human beings actually cannot live without religion. If they do not believe in Allah, then they will believe in something else. They will make ideology, nation, money, or technology into new “gods” in their lives. Yet none of these things can provide true peace and genuine direction in life.

Therefore, the collapse of religious authority is not a sign of civilizational progress, but rather a sign of deep spiritual crisis. A civilization that loses religion will lose its direction, lose its values, and in the end lose its own humanity.

The only way out of this crisis is to restore religion to its proper place: as the center of life, the source of values, and the guide of civilization. Religion must not remain merely a symbol, but must become the soul that animates every aspect of human life—from the private heart to the social system, from worship to civilization. Without that, human beings will continue moving within magnificent material progress while remaining empty of meaning and purpose. *Wallahu a‘lam.*

## Islam and Its Relationship with Previous Religions

Islam did not come as a religion cut off from the earlier history of prophethood, but as the continuation and completion of the messages of the earlier prophets. From Prophet Adam to Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, all messengers brought the same teaching in the fundamentals of faith, namely tawhid: worshiping Allah alone, avoiding shirk, and living according to the guidance of revelation.

For that reason, Islam does not regard the previous revealed religions as enemies, but as part of a chain of revelation that came from the same source. Islam acknowledges Prophet Musa and the Torah revealed to him, and also acknowledges Prophet Isa and the Gospel that he brought. This acknowledgment is not merely a formality, but part of creed. A Muslim is not considered a believer if he rejects any prophet or any scripture revealed by Allah.

However, although Islam acknowledges the earlier prophets, Islam also affirms that those teachings underwent change and distortion in the course of history. Many original teachings of the prophets were altered, supplemented, or misinterpreted by human beings. As a result, religion, which was originally revelation from heaven, became a system of belief mixed with tradition, myth, and worldly interests.

It is mentioned that Islam in fact possesses a nobler view of the previous prophets than some of the followers of those religions. Islam honors Prophet Musa and Prophet Isa as messengers of Allah, preserves their dignity, and clears them of accusations unworthy of them.

However, Islam also criticizes the attitude of some of the People of the Book who believe in their prophets, yet reject Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. The Qur’an affirms that such an attitude is inconsistent, because all prophets come from the same source. It is not logical for someone to claim faith in Allah’s revelation, but reject the revelation that came afterward without valid reason.

The Qur’an describes this condition very clearly. There are people who desire affection and recognition from the Muslims, yet they themselves do not respond with the same attitude. They claim to believe in their scripture, but reject the book that came afterward, even though all of it is from Allah. This shows that such rejection is not due to truth, but due to interests, fanaticism, or hatred.

In addition, Islam also affirms that the true bond between human beings is not the bond of blood or nation, but the bond of faith and obedience. A person is not considered close to the prophets merely because of lineage, but because of following their teachings.

Islam also affirms that previous religions had limitations in the scope of their missions. Some prophets were sent specifically to certain peoples and at certain times. Their teachings were not intended to be universal for all ages. Therefore, when the final message came through Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, that message became binding upon all humanity until the end of time.

Islam came as a religion of universality, not restricted to one nation, one region, or one age. The Qur’an is not merely a scripture for a certain people, but for all humankind. Islamic law regulates not only worship, but also social life, economics, politics, and morality.

It is mentioned that previous religions had fulfilled their respective roles in history. The prophets completed their duties in guiding humanity according to the needs of their times. But when times changed and human needs became broader, a more complete and universal message became necessary. Here lies the role of Islam as the seal of prophetic revelation.

Islam did not come to destroy previous religions, but to correct the distortions that had occurred, to restore humankind to pure tawhid, and to complete the teachings that already existed. It became the final seal, the completion, and the guardian of the prophetic legacy.

For that reason, Islam sees itself as a religion standing upon the continuity of the history of revelation. It honors the past, but also brings correction. It acknowledges the previous prophets, but also affirms that the message of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ is the final message valid for all humankind.

Thus, the relationship of Islam with previous religions is not one of hostility, but one of continuity and completion. Islam came as the religion that safeguards the legacy of the prophets, corrects distortions, and calls human beings back to pure tawhid.

This is the position of Islam in the history of religion: not merely one religion among many religions, but as the seal of divine revelation sent down for all humankind. Whoever follows it is upon the path of the prophets. And whoever rejects it has rejected the same chain of revelation once brought by the earlier prophets. *Wallahu a‘lam.*

## Islam as the Guardian of the World’s Spiritual Balance

In the previous discussion, it was explained that modern civilization is undergoing a major crisis as a result of the dominance of materialism and the weakening of religion’s role in human life. When faith in Allah and the Last Day no longer remains at the center of life, human beings begin searching for other substitutes. They still believe, but in the wrong things: power, wealth, ideology, or desire. As a result, human life loses its direction, and civilization moves forward without a clear purpose.

Under such conditions, Islam is viewed as the only religion that still possesses the power to preserve the spiritual balance of the world. This is because Islam does not merely teach spiritual aspects in a limited sense, but offers a comprehensive system of life that includes creed, worship, morality, law, economics, and social order. Islam does not separate this world from the Hereafter, nor the soul from matter, nor worship from everyday life.

It is mentioned that previous religions had fulfilled their respective tasks, yet some of them were no longer able to play a major role in guiding human civilization. This was due to distortions in teaching, the limitations of their missions, or changes that occurred over the course of history. As a result, when the current of modern materialism arrived with great force, those religions were unable to offer any meaningful resistance.

In contrast, Islam possesses a unique quality: it is universal, eternal, and complete. The message of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ is not limited by nation, region, or a particular age. It is addressed to all humankind until the end of time. The Qur’an is not merely a spiritual book, but also guidance for life that encompasses every aspect of human existence.

For this reason, Islam possesses the ability to preserve the balance between spiritual needs and material needs. Islam does not reject scientific progress, does not oppose technological development, and does not forbid the enjoyments of the world. However, Islam provides limits, purpose, and direction for all these things. The world is not viewed as the final goal, but as a means toward a more enduring life.

When civilization is oriented only toward matter, human beings lose the meaning of life. They may achieve extraordinary technological progress, yet their hearts remain empty, their souls restless, and their societies full of conflict. On the other hand, if life focuses only on spiritual aspects without giving attention to worldly needs, then civilization becomes weak, backward, and unable to preserve the continuity of life.

Islam comes as the middle path. It commands human beings to work, seek provision, build civilization, and make use of the earth. Yet at the same time, it reminds them that all of this will be accounted for before Allah. Every deed will be reckoned, every act of ظلم will be repaid, and every good deed will be rewarded.

This awareness of the Last Day is what preserves the balance of human life. It prevents human beings from acting unjustly, from becoming greedy, and from drowning in worldly pleasures. It also prevents human beings from despair, because they know that true justice will be realized in the Hereafter.

It may therefore be affirmed that humankind must not be left to live without religion to guide it. Without religion, humanity will lose direction, and civilization will move toward destruction. For this reason, Allah chose Islam as the final religion, and placed upon its followers the responsibility to preserve and spread these values.

Muslims are not only required to worship privately, but also to bring Islamic teachings into social, economic, and political life. They must become examples in justice, compassion, and piety. Thus, Islam does not remain merely a teaching in books, but becomes a real living force in society.

However, in the reality being described, the condition of the Muslim community itself has become weak. Many have reduced Islam to an intellectual heritage preserved in books, while in real life its values are no longer applied in a complete way. The systems that operate instead follow patterns contrary to Islamic teachings.

As a result, Islam’s role as the guardian of the world’s spiritual balance has become weakened. Yet the world is in desperate need of that force. In a situation where materialism, ideologies, and worldly interests are struggling against one another, Islam ought to appear as the balancing force that returns humanity to the true purpose of life.

Therefore, there should be a call for the revival of Islamic consciousness among scholars, activists, and the general public alike. Muslims must unite, cooperate, and strive to revive Islamic values in real life. They must have the courage to uphold truth, resist injustice, and bring mercy to all humankind.

Thus, Islam as the guardian of the world’s spiritual balance is not merely a theoretical concept, but a real responsibility. If Islam lives in the hearts of its followers and is embodied in their lives, then it will become a light for the world. But if it remains only stored in books and is not applied in life, then the world will continue walking in the darkness of materialism.

Therefore, the future of the world’s spiritual balance is closely tied to the extent to which Muslims are able to understand their religion, practice it, and present it as a solution to humanity’s problems. Islam is not merely a religion for Muslims, but a mercy to all creation. When its teachings are alive, the world will rediscover direction, meaning, and balance. *Wallahu a‘lam.*

## The Decline of the Muslim Ummah in Historical Reality

In the study of civilization, faith, and the role of Islam as the balance for the spiritual dimension of human life, a question often raised by thinkers emerges: if Islam possesses comprehensive teachings and is capable of guiding humanity toward balance in life, why has the Muslim ummah nevertheless experienced decline in the course of history? This question cannot be answered by accusing Islamic teachings of weakness, but must instead be analyzed through the historical reality of the life of the ummah itself.

Various historical studies show that the decline of the Muslim ummah was not caused by any defect in the principles of Islamic teaching, but rather by deviations that occurred in the political, economic, and social practices of Muslims. Islam as a teaching has retained its purity and consistency, but its implementation in the life of society gradually experienced corruption as historical conditions changed.

In the early phase of Islamic history, especially during the first generation, the Muslim ummah displayed extraordinary strength. They were able to build a vast civilization, control strategic territories, and liberate many nations from the oppression of older powers. That strength did not arise only from military factors or political strategy, but above all from the power of faith, sincerity, moral discipline, and the spirit of sacrifice. Islamic values lived within the collective consciousness of the ummah, becoming the energy that gave birth to an advanced and influential civilization.

However, in the later course of history, a shift began to occur in the system of government. The political structure, which at first had been built upon the principles of justice, consultation, and moral responsibility, gradually changed into a system more closely resembling absolute power. Government no longer fully reflected the fundamental values of Islam, but began to be influenced by political traditions from other civilizations, especially hereditary monarchical systems.

In the early stages, this change did not immediately destroy Islamic civilization. The ummah still possessed reserves of strength in the form of faith, scholarly tradition, and the spirit of da‘wah. The generation of the Companions, the Tabi‘in, and the great scholars still played their role in preserving the purity of Islamic teachings. Therefore, despite the weaknesses in the political system, Islamic civilization was still able to endure and even continue to develop in various fields.

Yet with the passing of time, these deviations spread further. Corruption appeared not only in government, but also in the economic structure. Unjust power and unequal economic distribution produced social injustice, structural poverty, and sharp inequality within society. Two major factors—authoritarianism in politics and concentration of wealth in the economy—became the sources of corruption that gradually weakened the strength of the ummah.

In historical analysis, this condition is often likened to a disease attacking the body. In the early stages, the body may still possess strong immunity, so the disease does not immediately kill it. But if it is left untreated, there comes a point when the body weakens and loses the ability to resist. Likewise was the state of the Muslim ummah: as long as faith, knowledge, and morality remained strong, deviations did not immediately destroy civilization. But when those foundations weakened, the corruption that had previously remained hidden began to appear, and decline set in.

This decline became even more evident when the Muslim ummah, already weakened internally, faced the rise of Western civilization at the height of its material power. At that time, the West possessed major advances in science, technology, and systems of social and political organization. By contrast, many Muslim regions were politically, economically, and intellectually weak.

As a result, interaction with Western civilization did not always produce healthy reform. Some Muslims did not adopt its positive elements such as discipline, work ethic, and scholarly tradition, but instead imitated its negative aspects, such as hedonistic, materialistic, and value-free lifestyles. Thus, the old corruption inherited from earlier times met with new corruption brought by the influence of unfiltered modernity.

In such a situation, the responsibility of thinkers, scholars, and community leaders became even heavier. They were not facing only one kind of crisis, but several interconnected crises: political crisis, economic crisis, intellectual stagnation, and the pressure of foreign ideologies. Efforts at reform became increasingly complex because many interests felt threatened by the idea of Islamic revival.

## The Challenges of Da‘wah and the Role of the Ulama

In the reality of modern civilization, Islamic da‘wah no longer faces simple challenges. Social change, technological progress, the dominance of materialistic culture, and global political and economic pressures have created conditions very different from earlier times. Therefore, da‘wah cannot be carried out through traditional approaches alone, but requires historical awareness, an understanding of reality, and mature strategy. In this context, the role of the ulama becomes extremely important, for they bear the trust of knowledge, moral guidance, and the civilizational direction of the ummah.

One of the greatest challenges of da‘wah in the modern age is the dominance of materialistic values in human life. Many societies measure success only by economic standards, power, and worldly pleasure. As a result, the spiritual dimension is increasingly marginalized, and religion is often regarded as a private matter that has no role in social and political life. Under such conditions, da‘wah must be able to restore the awareness that religion is not merely ritual, but a system of values that governs every aspect of human life.

In addition to ideological challenges, da‘wah also faces structural challenges. In many places, political policies and economic systems are not always aligned with the values of justice taught by Islam. Social inequality, poverty, corruption, and abuse of power have become realities that must be confronted. Under such conditions, da‘wah cannot be limited to teaching individual acts of worship, but must also build collective awareness concerning justice, trust, and social responsibility.

In this complex situation, the ulama occupy a highly strategic role. They are not merely transmitters of religious rulings, but also guardians of society’s morality, guides of thought, and directors of social movements. Ulama who understand the reality of their age will be able to bridge the principled teachings of Islam with the actual problems faced by the ummah.

However, one problem that often arises is the lack of unity in direction among da‘wah figures and religious institutions. Some scholars or activists focus only on ritual matters and individual education, while broader social issues receive insufficient attention. On the other hand, some become too close to power and thereby lose their moral independence. When the ulama no longer dare to speak the truth, religion’s function as a restraint upon power becomes weak.

Such conditions can result in a loss of public trust in religious authority. If the ulama do not serve as examples in courage, honesty, and social concern, then da‘wah will lose its influence. Religion will be seen as a symbol without transformative power, and society will seek other values considered more relevant to life.

Another challenge faced by da‘wah is the changing mindset of the younger generation. Modern youth live in a world that is fast-paced, open, and full of information. They are accustomed to rational argument, freedom of thought, and scientific approaches. If da‘wah is unable to answer their intellectual needs, they will seek answers elsewhere, even from ideologies contrary to Islamic teachings.

Therefore, the ulama and preachers are required to improve both their scholarly quality and their methods of da‘wah. Da‘wah must be delivered in language that is easy to understand, with strong argumentation and approaches relevant to modern life. Religious knowledge must be combined with social, economic, and cultural understanding, so that da‘wah does not become disconnected from the realities of human life.

At the same time, da‘wah also requires moral courage. History shows that major change within the ummah has always begun with a small group willing to speak the truth despite pressure and risk. Honest and courageous ulama will become a source of inspiration for society, while ulama who submit to worldly interests will lose their authority.

The role of the ulama in da‘wah is not only to teach knowledge, but also to build the awareness of the ummah, criticize injustice, and direct society toward a more moral life. They must be able to act as a balancing force between political power and the interests of the people, while preserving the vitality of religious values in public life.

Moreover, da‘wah also requires organized cooperation. Da‘wah movements that operate separately without coordination will struggle to face large-scale global challenges. Therefore, unity is needed among the ulama, educational institutions, and religious movements so that da‘wah can proceed with clear direction and planned objectives.

Thus, the challenges of da‘wah in the modern era come not only from outside the ummah, but also from within it. These challenges include the dominance of materialism, social inequality, the crisis of religious authority, the changing mindset of the younger generation, and the lack of coordination in da‘wah movements. All of this demands a more active, courageous, and visionary role from the ulama.

In the end, the future of da‘wah depends greatly on the quality of the ulama and the sincerity of the ummah in supporting them. If the ulama preserve their sincerity, courage, and depth of knowledge, then da‘wah will once again become a force that revives society. But if the ulama lose their integrity, then da‘wah will weaken, and the ummah will drift farther away from Islamic values.

Therefore, the revival of the ummah cannot be separated from the revival of da‘wah, and the revival of da‘wah depends greatly on the role of ulama who are sincere, knowledgeable, and courageous in upholding the truth. *Wallahu a‘lam.*

## Conclusion

From the entire discussion concerning civilization, faith, materialism, and the role of Islam in human life, it may be concluded that the crisis experienced by the modern world is not merely an economic, political, or technological crisis, but rather a profound spiritual crisis. A civilization built only upon the foundation of matter, without faith in Allah and awareness of the Last Day, will ultimately lose its direction and purpose. It may achieve astonishing outward progress, but at the same time suffer inner emptiness, anxiety of the soul, and social instability.

History shows that when religion loses its role in guiding human life, material forces take over the whole space of existence. Religion is narrowed down into a mere moral symbol or cultural tradition, while the system of life is operated entirely according to worldly interests. In such a condition, human beings no longer see life as a trust for which they will be held accountable, but merely as an opportunity to pursue pleasure and power.

In this context, Islam has a very important role as the guardian of balance between this world and the Hereafter, between matter and spirit, between reason and revelation. Islam does not reject worldly progress, but places it within the framework of moral responsibility and the goal of the Hereafter. It teaches that material power must be guided by faith, and that civilizational progress must be accompanied by noble character.

However, historical reality shows that the decline of the Muslim ummah was not caused by weakness in Islamic teaching, but by deviations in the life of the ummah itself. When the values of justice, trust, and responsibility were abandoned, the strength of civilization weakened as well. Corruption in the political system, inequality in the economic system, and weak moral leadership became the main causes of this decline.

Amid the challenges of the modern age, Islamic da‘wah faces various obstacles, both from outside and from within the ummah itself. The dominance of materialism, changing patterns of thought in society, and the crisis of religious authority are major tests for the continuity of da‘wah. Therefore, the role of the ulama and Muslim thinkers becomes extremely important in guiding the ummah, reviving the values of faith, and directing civilization toward true balance.

The revival of the Muslim ummah cannot be achieved merely through nostalgia for the past or by blindly imitating other civilizations. Revival can only be realized by returning to the pure teachings of Islam, accompanied by a deep understanding of the realities of the age. Knowledge, faith, character, and collective effort must proceed together, so that Islam becomes not only a historical legacy, but a real living force within society.

In the end, the future of human civilization depends greatly upon the balance between material progress and spiritual depth. Without faith, civilization will lose its direction. Without morality, power will turn into oppression. Without religion, humanity will become lost in its own ambitions and desires.

Therefore, the responsibility of the Muslim ummah is not only to reform itself, but also to present once more the values of Islam as a mercy to all creation. With strong faith, sound knowledge, and real action, the Muslim ummah can once again play its role as a bearer of light for the world, and as the guardian of balance between this world and the Hereafter. *Wallahu a‘lam.*
