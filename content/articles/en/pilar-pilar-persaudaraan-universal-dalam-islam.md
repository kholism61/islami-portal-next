## Introduction

From the very beginning of its emergence, Islam brought a grand vision concerning the unity of humankind. This teaching does not merely address the relationship between human beings and God, but also regulates the relationship between human beings themselves. In the Islamic perspective, all human beings originate from the same source of creation and are bound together in a human bond that cannot be separated. Differences in ethnicity, nation, skin color, language, or social background do not erase this essential equality. Therefore, Islam establishes the principle that relations among human beings must be built upon the values of brotherhood, justice, and respect for human dignity.

The Qur’an clearly affirms the unity of the origin of humanity in the word of Allah:

> يَا أَيُّهَا النَّاسُ اتَّقُوا رَبَّكُمُ الَّذِي خَلَقَكُمْ مِنْ نَفْسٍ وَاحِدَةٍ  
>
> “O mankind, fear your Lord, who created you from a single soul.” (QS. An-Nisa: 1)

This verse contains a profound message concerning the nature of humanity. All human beings originate from a single soul, namely Prophet Adam, and thus in essence they are one large family. Awareness of this shared origin should give rise to attitudes of mutual respect, mutual assistance, and protection of the rights of fellow human beings. In the Islamic perspective, no human being is inherently superior to another except by virtue of their piety toward Allah.

However, the reality of human life often shows conditions different from this ideal. The long history of human civilization demonstrates many forms of inequality, oppression, and discrimination among people. Differences in economic power, political authority, and social status are often used as reasons to demean others. The strong oppress the weak, the wealthy dominate the poor, and those in power exploit their position to advance their own interests. Such circumstances transform human relations, which should be based on brotherhood, into relationships filled with competition, hostility, and even oppression.

Islam came to correct these conditions by placing the principle of universal brotherhood as the foundation of social life. Brotherhood in Islam is not limited to blood ties or tribal relations but encompasses all of humanity. Every human being has the right to be treated with justice and dignity. This principle rejects all forms of discrimination based on race, lineage, or social status.

The Prophet ﷺ emphasized this principle in his saying:

> الناس سواسية كأسنان المشط، لا فضل لعربي على أعجمي إلا بالتقوى  
>
> “People are equal like the teeth of a comb. No Arab has superiority over a non-Arab except through piety.”

This hadith illustrates that human beings hold equal status in their humanity. The differences among them lie only in the level of their piety and deeds, not in their origins or worldly positions. Thus, Islam views that the measure of human nobility is not determined by wealth, power, or lineage, but by the moral and spiritual qualities a person possesses.

In addition, Islam emphasizes the importance of social justice as a condition for the realization of true brotherhood. Brotherhood cannot flourish in a society filled with inequality and injustice. If a small portion of society lives in excessive luxury while others suffer in painful poverty, social relations will be filled with jealousy and hostility. Therefore, Islam demands a system of life that ensures social balance and protects the rights of all members of society.

Within this framework, the concept of universal brotherhood in Islam must be understood. Brotherhood is not merely a moral expression or religious slogan, but a principle that must be realized in a just and humane social system. Islam calls upon human beings to build a society founded upon shared responsibility, mutual assistance, and respect for the dignity of every individual.

By understanding these principles, we can see that Islamic teachings aim not only to cultivate individual piety but also to build a more just and civilized social order. The universal brotherhood taught by Islam serves as the foundation for a harmonious society in which every human being can live with dignity, justice, and security.

Therefore, discussing the pillars of universal brotherhood in Islam becomes extremely important to study deeply. Through understanding these principles, it is hoped that the humanitarian values that lie at the core of Islamic teachings can be rediscovered and serve as solutions to many social problems that humanity still faces today.

## The Nature of Human Equality

One of the fundamental principles emphasized by Islam in building a social order is the equality of human beings. Islam views that all human beings share the same origin, namely from a single soul created by Allah. From this single soul emerged all humanity with their various nations, tribes, languages, and skin colors. Therefore, these differences must not be used as justification to claim absolute superiority over others.

The Qur’an affirms this principle in the word of Allah:

> يَا أَيُّهَا النَّاسُ اتَّقُوا رَبَّكُمُ الَّذِي خَلَقَكُمْ مِنْ نَفْسٍ وَاحِدَةٍ  
>
> “O mankind, fear your Lord, who created you from a single soul.” (QS. An-Nisa: 1)

This verse demonstrates that the unity of human origin is the foundation of human equality. All people are descendants of one father and one mother, namely Prophet Adam and Eve. Thus, no human being possesses a more noble origin than another. All humans stand upon the same basis of humanity, possess the same rights as servants of Allah, and bear the same responsibilities in life.

However, equality in Islam does not mean that all human beings are identical in every respect. In reality, people differ in their abilities, intelligence, talents, and potentials. Some individuals possess high intellectual capacity, others greater physical strength, while others excel in areas such as art, leadership, or practical skills.

These differences are part of the divine order (*sunnatullah*) in human life. Allah created human beings with diverse abilities so that they may complement one another. Through such diversity, social life becomes dynamic and societies can develop through cooperation among individuals with different capabilities.

Nevertheless, these differences in ability must not become grounds for oppression or humiliation. In Islam, all people are equal before the law and religious obligations. No one has the right to seize the rights of others simply because they possess greater strength or status.

The Prophet ﷺ affirmed this principle of equality in a well-known hadith:

> الناس سواسية كأسنان المشط، لا فضل لعربي على أعجمي إلا بالتقوى  
>
> “People are equal like the teeth of a comb. No Arab has superiority over a non-Arab except through piety.”

This hadith carries a deep meaning. The teeth of a comb are aligned and equal in height, symbolizing the equality of human beings in their humanity. Differences among people must not produce discrimination or oppression, for the true measure of honor is only piety toward Allah.

The Qur’an also emphasizes this principle in another verse:

> يَا أَيُّهَا النَّاسُ إِنَّا خَلَقْنَاكُم مِّن ذَكَرٍ وَأُنثَىٰ وَجَعَلْنَاكُمْ شُعُوبًا وَقَبَائِلَ لِتَعَارَفُوا ۚ إِنَّ أَكْرَمَكُمْ عِندَ اللَّهِ أَتْقَاكُمْ  
>
> “O mankind, indeed We created you from a male and a female and made you into nations and tribes so that you may know one another. Indeed, the most honorable of you in the sight of Allah is the most pious among you.” (QS. Al-Hujurat: 13)

This verse affirms that human diversity is not meant to demean one another, but to encourage mutual recognition and cooperation. Islam does not reject diversity; rather, it makes it a means to strengthen human relationships within the broader framework of brotherhood.

## Principles of Universal Brotherhood

One of the most fundamental teachings in Islam is the principle of universal brotherhood that encompasses all of humanity. This brotherhood is not limited to blood relations, tribal affiliation, nationality, or specific groups, but includes all human beings as creations of Allah who originate from the same source.

Islam views that relationships between people should be built upon brotherhood grounded in justice, compassion, and mutual respect.

This principle stems from the belief that all humans are descendants of Prophet Adam. Therefore, they share a fundamental kinship within humanity. Awareness of this shared origin creates a moral responsibility to preserve the dignity of others and treat them with justice and kindness.

The Qur’an reminds humanity of this reality:

> يَا أَيُّهَا النَّاسُ اتَّقُوا رَبَّكُمُ الَّذِي خَلَقَكُمْ مِنْ نَفْسٍ وَاحِدَةٍ  
>
> “O mankind, fear your Lord, who created you from a single soul.” (QS. An-Nisa: 1)

This verse demonstrates that human brotherhood is not merely a social relationship but a consequence of the unity of creation itself.

## Social Justice as the Foundation of Brotherhood

True brotherhood cannot exist without social justice within society. Islam teaches that relations among people cannot rely solely on emotional sympathy or moral rhetoric, but must be supported by a fair and balanced social system.

When justice is neglected, human relationships easily become filled with jealousy, tension, and conflict. For this reason, Islam places justice as the primary foundation for universal brotherhood.

The Qur’an clearly commands justice in human life:

> إِنَّ اللَّهَ يَأْمُرُ بِالْعَدْلِ وَالإِحْسَانِ  
>
> “Indeed, Allah commands justice and excellence.” (QS. An-Nahl: 90)

This verse shows that justice is one of the core principles of Islamic teachings. Without justice, societies become filled with inequalities that undermine the spirit of brotherhood.

Islam therefore establishes social mechanisms such as zakat, charity, and mutual support to maintain social balance within society.

Allah says:

> كَيْ لَا يَكُونَ دُولَةً بَيْنَ الْأَغْنِيَاءِ مِنكُمْ  
>
> “So that wealth does not circulate only among the rich among you.” (QS. Al-Hasyr: 7)

This verse demonstrates that Islam rejects economic systems that concentrate wealth among a small elite.

Through justice, solidarity, and mutual care, Islam seeks to build a civilization founded upon compassion and humanity. When these principles are truly implemented, social life will be filled with trust, cooperation, and mutual respect among human beings.

## The Dangers of Social Inequality

One of the greatest threats to the realization of universal brotherhood in human life is excessive social inequality. When the gap between the rich and the poor becomes increasingly sharp, then human relations, which should be built upon brotherhood and justice, will turn into relationships filled with jealousy, tension, and even hostility. Therefore, Islam pays great attention to the problem of social inequality and seeks to prevent the emergence of disparities that damage the balance of society.

Social inequality is essentially a condition in which a small portion of society controls resources and wealth excessively, while the majority lives in severe deprivation. Differences in the level of welfare within society are indeed a reality that cannot be entirely avoided, because human beings possess different abilities, efforts, and opportunities. However, when those differences become too great and no longer align with the principle of justice, such inequality will give rise to serious social problems.

In a society filled with inequality, the relationship between the rich and the poor often loses its human element. Those who possess wealth and power tend to live in environments separated from the wider society, while the poor must struggle hard merely to fulfill their most basic needs. Such a condition can create feelings of marginalization and injustice among the weaker members of society.

Islam views such circumstances as highly dangerous for the stability of social life. When some people enjoy excessive luxury while others suffer because of poverty, dissatisfaction will arise and may trigger conflict within society. Social tensions that continue to increase can cause fractures in human relations and ultimately threaten social unity.

The Qur’an has warned that wealth should not circulate only among a particular group. Allah says:

> كَيْ لَا يَكُونَ دُولَةً بَيْنَ الْأَغْنِيَاءِ مِنكُمْ
>
> “So that wealth does not circulate only among the rich among you.” (QS. Al-Hasyr: 7)

This verse shows that Islam rejects an economic system that allows the accumulation of wealth in the hands of only a few people. Wealth should be a means of building collective welfare, not a tool to strengthen the domination of one group over another.

Extreme social inequality can also damage the moral values of society. When some people live in excessive luxury, they may lose empathy for the suffering of others. On the other hand, those who live in prolonged poverty may lose hope and feel that life offers them no fair opportunity.

Such conditions can encourage the emergence of various forms of social deviance. Some people may seek shortcuts to improve their living conditions through improper means, such as corruption, fraud, or other criminal acts. Thus, social inequality not only damages human relations but also threatens moral stability within society.

In human history, many social upheavals and revolutions have occurred as a result of severe economic inequality. When the gap between the rich and the poor reaches an extreme level, the oppressed often rise to demand change. Such social movements frequently emerge as reactions to a system perceived as unjust.

However, Islam does not encourage social change through violence or destructive conflict. Rather, Islam seeks to prevent inequality from arising in the first place by establishing various principles of social justice that must be implemented in society. One of these is the obligation of zakat, which functions as a mechanism for wealth distribution so that economic balance may be preserved.

In addition to zakat, Islam also encourages its followers to increase charity (*sadaqah*) and spending in the way of Allah (*infaq*) as forms of concern for fellow human beings. Through these mechanisms, wealth does not remain merely private property, but also becomes a means of helping those in need.

The Messenger of Allah ﷺ also gave a stern warning against excessive hoarding of wealth without regard for the condition of the surrounding society. In a hadith it is stated:

> ما آمن بي من بات شبعان وجاره جائع إلى جنبه وهو يعلم
>
> “He does not truly believe in me who sleeps full while his neighbor beside him is hungry and he knows it.”

This hadith shows that Islam considers social concern to be an essential part of faith. A Muslim must not remain indifferent to the suffering of others, especially when he has the ability to help them.

Social inequality can also destroy the spirit of brotherhood that should be the foundation of social life. When economic differences become too great, relationships among people tend to be filled with suspicion and distrust. The poor may view the rich as people who are unconcerned with their suffering, while the rich may view the poor as threats to their security and stability.

Such a situation causes society to lose the sense of togetherness that forms the basis of harmonious social life. The brotherhood taught by Islam cannot flourish in an environment filled with disparity and injustice.

Therefore, Islam emphasizes the importance of maintaining social balance so that inequality does not develop into a problem that destroys the life of society. Islam does not reject differences in human welfare, but it requires that such differences remain within reasonable limits and do not cause suffering for part of society.

The danger of social inequality lies not only in economic problems, but also in its impact on moral life and human relationships. When social justice is upheld and economic balance is preserved, society will find it easier to build sincere and respectful bonds of brotherhood. On the other hand, if inequality is allowed to grow unchecked, social life will be filled with conflict, jealousy, and instability that will ultimately damage the unity of humankind.

## Criticism of the Misuse of Religion

One of the issues that often arises in social life is the misuse of religious teachings to justify unjust social conditions. Throughout human history, religion has not infrequently been used as a tool to preserve the interests of certain groups, especially those who possess power and wealth. Religious teachings, which should serve as a source of justice and liberation from oppression, are instead misinterpreted in ways that support oppressive social systems.

In the context of Muslim societies, this phenomenon has also occurred in various historical periods. Some people have sought to instill the understanding that poverty is a destiny that must be accepted passively, while the wealth possessed by a small group is the will of God and should not be questioned. In this way, the social inequality present in society is treated as something natural and not something that should be struggled against and corrected.

In fact, such an understanding contradicts the fundamental spirit of Islamic teachings. Islam never teaches people to accept injustice passively without striving to correct it. On the contrary, Islam commands its followers to establish justice and oppose all forms of oppression.

One form of religious misuse that often occurs is the incorrect use of the concept of destiny (*qadar*) to justify social inequality. Some people assume that poverty and wealth are entirely the will of God and therefore should not be questioned. They often quote verses that speak about Allah distributing provision to justify unjust social conditions.

For example, Allah says:

> اللَّهُ يَبْسُطُ الرِّزْقَ لِمَن يَشَاءُ وَيَقْدِرُ
>
> “Allah expands provision for whom He wills and restricts it.” (QS. Ar-Ra’d: 26)

This verse is often misunderstood as if Islam teaches that people do not need to strive to improve the economic condition of society. In fact, the verse is not meant to remove human responsibility in creating social justice. Allah has given human beings the ability to think, work, and organize their lives so that they may create a more just society.

If the concept of destiny is understood incorrectly, it can become a reason to justify various forms of injustice. The oppressed will be asked to accept their condition patiently, while those who enjoy power and wealth may continue to maintain their status without feeling guilty. Such an understanding ultimately serves only to reinforce an unjust social system.

Islam rejects this kind of false resignation. The Qur’an clearly commands human beings to uphold justice and resist wrongdoing. Allah says:

> وَلَا تَبْخَسُوا النَّاسَ أَشْيَاءَهُمْ
>
> “Do not deprive people of their rights.” (QS. Al-A’raf: 85)

This verse shows that Islam places great emphasis on preserving human rights. Any reduction of another person’s rights is an act contrary to religious teaching.

Moreover, Islam also affirms that human beings possess freedom in choosing and acting. This freedom is the foundation of moral responsibility in life. If human beings had no freedom to choose, then commands and prohibitions in religion would have no meaning.

Allah says:

> وَقُلِ الْحَقُّ مِن رَّبِّكُمْ فَمَن شَاءَ فَلْيُؤْمِن وَمَن شَاءَ فَلْيَكْفُرْ
>
> “And say: The truth is from your Lord. So whoever wills—let him believe; and whoever wills—let him disbelieve.” (QS. Al-Kahfi: 29)

This verse shows that human beings have the freedom to determine their attitudes and actions. Therefore, they are also responsible for striving for justice in social life.

The misuse of religion also often appears in teachings that excessively glorify poverty and belittle efforts to improve worldly life. In the history of Muslim societies, there have emerged groups that taught that poverty is more noble than wealth, and that people should distance themselves from worldly life.

Such an understanding does not truly accord with the teachings of Islam. Islam never commands its followers to live in poverty or abandon efforts to attain a decent life. On the contrary, Islam encourages its followers to work hard and utilize the resources of this world in order to build a better life.

The Qur’an even affirms that the enjoyments of worldly life are blessings from Allah that may be enjoyed by human beings. Allah says:

> قُلْ مَنْ حَرَّمَ زِينَةَ اللَّهِ الَّتِي أَخْرَجَ لِعِبَادِهِ
>
> “Say: Who has forbidden the adornment of Allah which He has brought forth for His servants?” (QS. Al-A’raf: 32)

This verse shows that Islam does not forbid people from enjoying worldly life so long as it is done in a proper way and does not transgress the limits set by religion.

In the Islamic view, worldly life and the Hereafter are not two things that must be set in opposition. The world is a place where human beings strive and build a good life, while the Hereafter is the place of accountability for all deeds done in this world.

Therefore, Islam rejects all forms of understanding that turn religion into a tool to weaken human determination in striving for justice and welfare. Religion should be a source of inspiration for people to improve their lives, not a tool for maintaining unjust conditions.

Criticism of the misuse of religion is an important part of restoring Islamic teachings to their original purpose. Islam was revealed as a mercy to all creation and as guidance for building a just and dignified society. When religious teachings are properly understood, they become a moral force capable of liberating human beings from various forms of oppression and injustice.

## Brotherhood and Justice in Islamic History

Islamic history provides many concrete examples of how the principles of brotherhood and social justice were practically implemented in society. Islamic teachings did not stop at theoretical concepts, but were realized in actual conduct that shaped a just and harmonious social order. During the time of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ and the Companions, the values of brotherhood and justice became the main foundation in building the social life of the Muslim community.

From the earliest period of Islamic preaching, the Messenger of Allah ﷺ instilled the awareness that relations among people must be founded upon brotherhood and shared responsibility. This teaching became especially important when the Muslims experienced pressure and persecution in Makkah. In those difficult circumstances, the Companions helped and strengthened one another.

The clearest manifestation of the principle of brotherhood in Islamic history can be seen when the Messenger of Allah ﷺ migrated from Makkah to Madinah. After arriving in Madinah, the Prophet immediately established bonds of brotherhood between the *Muhajirun* and the *Ansar*. The *Muhajirun* were the Companions who left Makkah behind, leaving their wealth and livelihoods, while the *Ansar* were the people of Madinah who welcomed them warmly.

The Messenger of Allah ﷺ joined these two groups together in a bond known as *mu’akhah* (brotherhood). This bond was not merely symbolic, but carried real social consequences. The *Ansar* sincerely shared their wealth, homes, and even their means of livelihood with their brothers from among the *Muhajirun*.

This event illustrates how strong the spirit of solidarity was in Muslim society at that time. Brotherhood was not only expressed in words, but also in concrete actions that reflected care and sacrifice.

The Qur’an praises the attitude of the *Ansar* in receiving their brothers from among the *Muhajirun*. Allah says:

> وَيُؤْثِرُونَ عَلَىٰ أَنفُسِهِمْ وَلَوْ كَانَ بِهِمْ خَصَاصَةٌ
>
> “They give preference to others over themselves even though they are themselves in need.” (QS. Al-Hasyr: 9)

This verse describes the attitude of *ithar* (preferring others over oneself), which is one of the noble values in Islamic teaching. This value shows that true brotherhood is not merely based on formal relationships, but also on the willingness to share and sacrifice for the common good.

In addition to brotherhood, Islamic history also shows the implementation of a very strong principle of justice in social life. The Messenger of Allah ﷺ upheld justice without regard to social status, position, or family ties.

In a famous hadith, the Messenger of Allah ﷺ said:

> لو أن فاطمة بنت محمد سرقت لقطعت يدها
>
> “If Fatimah the daughter of Muhammad were to steal, I would cut off her hand.”

This statement shows that the law in Islam applies fairly to all people without exception. Even the daughter of the Messenger of Allah herself would not receive special treatment if she violated the law. This principle affirms that justice in Islam must not be influenced by social status or personal relationships.

Another example of the implementation of justice in Islamic history can be seen in the leadership of the caliphs after the Messenger of Allah ﷺ. The caliphs sought to emulate the principle of justice that had been taught by the Prophet.

Caliph Umar ibn al-Khattab is known as one of the leaders who was very firm in upholding social justice. He often went around at night to ensure that none of his people were suffering from hunger or hardship without receiving attention from the government.

In one famous story, Umar once found a mother cooking stones to calm her hungry children. When he learned of her situation, Umar immediately took wheat from the public treasury and carried it himself to the family. He even helped cook the food for them until the children were able to eat their fill.

This story shows that leadership in Islam is not only about power, but also about moral responsibility to ensure the welfare of society.

Besides Umar ibn al-Khattab, many of the Prophet’s Companions set examples in upholding the values of brotherhood and justice. They understood that wealth and power were not the ultimate goals of life, but means to improve society and help those in need.

In daily life, the Messenger of Allah ﷺ himself set an example in simplicity and togetherness with his Companions. He never separated himself from society or indulged in excessive luxury. When the Companions were digging the trench in the Battle of Khandaq, the Messenger of Allah ﷺ worked alongside them without distinguishing himself as a leader.

This attitude shows that brotherhood in Islam recognizes no rigid social hierarchy. Leaders and ordinary people alike share responsibility in working together for the good of society.

The values of brotherhood and justice that were implemented in the early Islamic period became the foundation for the formation of a strong and solid society. With such a strong sense of togetherness, the Muslims were able to face various challenges and build a great civilization in a relatively short time.

This history provides an important lesson: brotherhood and justice are not merely moral ideals, but also social forces capable of building a stable and prosperous society. When these values are consistently applied, human relations will be filled with mutual trust and mutual respect.

Islamic history shows that the teachings of universal brotherhood and social justice are not merely theoretical concepts, but have proven capable of being realized in actual life. These values remain relevant today as guidance for facing various social challenges in the present age.

### Conclusion

From the entire discussion of the pillars of universal brotherhood in Islam, it can be understood that Islam does not merely teach a spiritual relationship between human beings and God, but also builds a foundation for social life based on justice, equality, and compassion. Islamic teachings place human beings as creatures possessing equal dignity before Allah, even though they differ in tribe, nation, skin color, language, and social position.

Brotherhood in Islam is not a narrow concept limited only to the relationship of certain groups. This brotherhood is universal in nature, encompassing all humanity who come from one origin of creation. The Qur’an affirms that all human beings are descendants of a single soul, and therefore they possess a human bond that cannot be separated. Awareness of this common origin should produce mutual respect, mutual assistance, and the safeguarding of one another’s rights.

However, Islam also recognizes that human beings differ in abilities, talents, and life circumstances. Such differences are part of *sunnatullah* that cannot be avoided in human life. These differences are not intended to produce inequality or oppression, but rather to create cooperation and mutual complementarity in social life. Therefore, Islam rejects all forms of discrimination and arrogance based on lineage, wealth, or power.

In order for this brotherhood to be realized in practice, Islam emphasizes the importance of social justice in society. Justice is the primary foundation that preserves balance in human relationships. Without justice, brotherhood becomes only an empty slogan with no meaning in real life. When justice is upheld, each individual can obtain his rights properly and society can function harmoniously.

Islam also pays great attention to the danger of social inequality that may damage brotherhood within society. Excessive economic disparity between the rich and the poor can create social jealousy, conflict, and weaken solidarity among people. Therefore, Islam establishes various social mechanisms such as zakat, charity, and *infaq* in order to maintain economic balance and prevent the concentration of wealth in the hands of only a few people.

In addition, Islam strongly criticizes all forms of religious misuse that are employed to justify social injustice. Religious teaching must not be turned into a tool to preserve the interests of certain groups or to justify oppressive conditions. Rather, religion should become a source of inspiration for human beings to strive for justice and improve social life.

Islamic history provides concrete examples of how the principles of brotherhood and justice can be implemented in society. During the time of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ and the Companions, these values were embodied in strong social solidarity, concern for others, and just and responsible leadership. The stories of brotherhood between the *Muhajirun* and the *Ansar*, as well as the leadership of the caliphs who paid attention to the welfare of their people, show that Islam possesses the capacity to build a just and harmonious society.

These values remain relevant until the present time. In the modern world, which is often marked by conflict, social disparity, and injustice, Islamic teachings on universal brotherhood can serve as important guidance for building a more peaceful and dignified life. By upholding justice, respecting human rights, and nurturing social solidarity, society can create an environment that enables every individual to live with dignity.

Ultimately, universal brotherhood in Islam is not merely a theoretical moral ideal, but a principle of life that must be realized in social practice. When people truly understand and practice these values, human relations will be filled with mutual trust, mutual respect, and mutual help.

Thus, the pillars of universal brotherhood taught by Islam provide a clear direction for the construction of a just and humane society. Through the implementation of these values, humankind can build a civilization that is not only materially advanced, but also elevated in moral and humanitarian values.
