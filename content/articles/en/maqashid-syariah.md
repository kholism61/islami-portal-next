## Introduction

Islamic law was revealed by Allah not as a rigid collection of commands and prohibitions, but as a divine system full of wisdom, purpose, and benefit for human beings. The Shariah came to organize human life upon justice, welfare, and balance, both in matters of religion and in matters of worldly life. For that reason, understanding the Shariah cannot stop at the outward wording of legal texts alone; it must also uncover the greater purposes (*maqasid*) that stand behind them.

Blessings and peace be upon the Prophet Muhammad ?, the bearer of the message who built Islamic civilization upon reform after humanity had been immersed in moral and social confusion. Blessings also be upon his Companions, the Followers, and the scholars who became guiding stars in the sky of Islamic learning, through whom the light of the sacred law has remained preserved until today.

This article aims to present some important discussions related to *maqasid al-shariah*, to explain their urgency in understanding Islamic law, and to show their role in easing disagreement and conflict within the Muslim community.

## Background and the Urgency of Maqasid al-Shariah

Differences of opinion among jurists are inevitable. Yet such differences often become sharp disputes that are difficult to reconcile, especially when each side holds tightly to partial evidences without looking to the greater purposes of the Shariah. In many cases, legal disagreement ends without a solution that truly brings the community closer to benefit.

The discussion of maqasid becomes necessary because of the weakness found in the method of legal reasoning among some circles. Many legal debates do not rest on decisive proof and are far removed from the fundamental principles of the Shariah itself. As a result, disagreement expands while the community is left confused.

If scholars and students of knowledge returned to the universal principles of the Shariah, such as justice, public welfare, and the removal of harm, then many disputes could be managed with greater balance and wisdom. This is the function of maqasid al-shariah: they serve as an intellectual and moral compass in understanding Islamic law.

## A Critique of Certain Classical Usul al-Fiqh Approaches

The science of usul al-fiqh has played a tremendous role in the history of Islamic legal theory. Yet in some of its later developments, many discussions became heavily focused on linguistic analysis alone: wording, literal meaning, general and specific expressions, unrestricted and restricted language, and methods of preferring one proof over another. These discussions are important, but they become problematic when detached from the wisdom and purposes of the law.

As a result, maqasid al-shariah were often mentioned only briefly and were not treated as a foundational pillar in legal reasoning. In many classical works of usul al-fiqh, maqasid receive little sustained treatment, even though practical works of fiqh often employ maqasid-based principles implicitly.

For example, the legal maxim ?There should be neither harming nor reciprocating harm? is frequently used in practical fiqh, yet it is rarely discussed in a fully systematic way within the older usul tradition. This reveals a gap between legal theory and actual legal application within the heritage of Islamic law.

## Maqasid al-Shariah as an Independent Discipline

In reality, maqasid al-shariah are the heart of Islamic law. They explain why a ruling exists, not merely what that ruling is. Scholars formulated that the major aims of the Shariah are the preservation of five foundational necessities: religion, life, intellect, lineage, and wealth. Some scholars added honor as an independent objective as well.

In everyday life, maqasid al-shariah can be seen clearly. The prohibition of intoxicants is not simply a ritual prohibition; it aims to preserve the intellect and protect human safety. The rules of financial dealings are not meant to create hardship, but to protect wealth, prevent fraud, and preserve social justice. Even penal laws in Islam aim at preserving the stability of society and preventing greater corruption.

For that reason, maqasid al-shariah deserve to be treated as an independent field of knowledge, not merely as a supplemental discussion inside usul al-fiqh. When maqasid are used as a foundation, Islamic law appears more relevant, more flexible, and more capable of responding to the challenges of changing times without losing its essential values.

Even so, this project still requires further refinement. Discussions of maqasid remain scattered in many places, often unsystematic, and sometimes neglected by students of knowledge who focus more on debates over subsidiary legal issues.

This article continues that spirit by placing maqasid al-shariah at the center of understanding Islamic law, especially in the fields of transactions and social life, which are dynamic and constantly changing.

## The Role of Scholars in Developing Maqasid

History shows that a number of major scholars made decisive contributions to the development of maqasid al-shariah. Among them were 'Izz al-Din ibn 'Abd al-Salam through his work *Qawa'id al-Ahkam*, al-Qarafi through *al-Furuq*, and, at its highest stage of systematization, Imam Abu Ishaq al-Shatibi through *al-Muwafaqat*. Al-Shatibi in particular was the scholar who seriously organized maqasid into a coherent framework for Islamic legal thought.

### Conclusion

Maqasid al-shariah are the living heart of Islamic law. Without understanding maqasid, Islamic law can easily be read in a narrow, rigid way and may even appear to conflict with its own original aims. By contrast, when maqasid are treated as a foundation, Islamic law emerges as a system that is just, humane, and relevant across all times.

In the context of modern life, with its complex social, economic, and cultural problems, a maqasid-based approach is urgently needed. It helps Muslims understand the Shariah in a holistic way, reduces destructive sectarian rigidity, and directs legal disagreement toward shared welfare.

May this discussion become a small step toward reviving the spirit of maqasid al-shariah and contribute to strengthening an understanding of Islamic law that is oriented toward justice, mercy, and the benefit of the ummah.
