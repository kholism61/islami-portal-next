## Introduction

Zakat al-Fitr is one of the great obligations in Islamic law, and it holds a special status because it is directly connected to the conclusion of the worship of fasting in Ramadan. It is not merely a form of social concern, but an act of worship that combines **purification of the soul (tazkiyah)**, **financial worship (ibadah maliyyah)**, and **personal responsibility** for every Muslim.

In the books of fiqh, it is stated:

> وَتَجِبُ زَكَاةُ الْفِطْرِ بِثَلَاثَةِ أَشْيَاءَ: الْإِسْلَامُ وَغُرُوبُ الشَّمْسِ مِنْ آخِرِ يَوْمٍ مِنْ رَمَضَانَ
>
> Meaning: Zakat al-Fitr becomes obligatory because of three matters: Islam, the setting of the sun on the last day of Ramadan, and the existence of financial ease.

The fact that the time of obligation is tied to sunset shows that Zakat al-Fitr is directly linked to the arrival of Eid al-Fitr. It thus becomes a symbol of completing the worship of fasting and inaugurating the joy of the festival.

It is called **Zakat al-Fitr** because it becomes obligatory due to **al-fitr**, that is, breaking the fast of Ramadan. Some scholars also call it **the zakat of the body (zakat al-abdan)**, because its obligation is attached to the person of each Muslim, rather than to a particular type of wealth as in Zakat al-Mal. It purifies the soul from shortcomings, heedlessness, and mistakes that may have occurred during fasting.

The basis for the obligation of Zakat al-Fitr is established in the hadith:

> فَرَضَ رَسُولُ اللَّهِ ﷺ زَكَاةَ الْفِطْرِ مِنْ رَمَضَانَ صَاعًا مِنْ تَمْرٍ أَوْ صَاعًا مِنْ شَعِيرٍ عَلَى كُلِّ مُسْلِمٍ
>
> Meaning: The Messenger of Allah ﷺ made Zakat al-Fitr of Ramadan obligatory as one sa‘ of dates or one sa‘ of barley upon every Muslim.

This hadith shows the universal nature of the obligation of Zakat al-Fitr. It is not restricted by social status, gender, or a person’s condition of freedom. As long as a person is Muslim and fulfills the conditions, this duty applies to him.

The obligation of Zakat al-Fitr shows the balance between individual worship and social responsibility. On the one hand, a Muslim is commanded to fast in order to discipline the soul and restrain desires. On the other hand, he is commanded to give Zakat al-Fitr so that the joy of Eid may be shared by all levels of society.

By understanding its foundations and meanings comprehensively, Zakat al-Fitr appears as a deeply meaningful conclusion to Ramadan: a purification of the self, a completion of the fast, and a bond of solidarity among the Muslim community during the blessed atmosphere of Eid al-Fitr. And Allah knows best.

## Conditions for the Obligation of Zakat al-Fitr

Zakat al-Fitr becomes obligatory through three matters, as mentioned in the text: Islam, the setting of the sun on the last day of Ramadan, and the existence of financial ease.

### First Condition: Islam

The original basis for the obligation of Zakat al-Fitr is the hadith narrated by Ibn ‘Umar:

> فَرَضَ رَسُولُ اللَّهِ ﷺ زَكَاةَ الْفِطْرِ صَاعًا مِنْ تَمْرٍ أَوْ صَاعًا مِنْ شَعِيرٍ عَلَى كُلِّ مُسْلِمٍ
>
> Meaning: The Messenger of Allah ﷺ made Zakat al-Fitr obligatory as one sa‘ of dates or one sa‘ of barley upon every Muslim.

Ibn al-Mundhir claimed that there is consensus on its obligation. Therefore, there is no Zakat al-Fitr upon a disbeliever for himself. However, if a non-Muslim owns a Muslim slave, there is a difference of opinion. The sounder opinion states that Zakat al-Fitr is still obligatory upon him because of his Muslim slave.

### Second Condition: The Setting of the Sun on the Last Day of Ramadan

The strongest opinion in the Shafi‘i school, namely the **new opinion (qaul jadid)**, is that Zakat al-Fitr becomes obligatory at sunset on the last day of Ramadan, because it is connected to Eid al-Fitr. Another opinion states that it becomes obligatory at the break of dawn on the Day of Eid. A third opinion combines both.

Based on the stronger view, if a person acquires a slave after sunset, or if his wife gives birth to a child after that time, then Zakat al-Fitr is not obligatory for that individual, because the time of obligation was not reached.

### Third Condition: The Existence of Financial Ease

The text states:

> وَوُجُودُ الْفَضْلِ عَنْ قُوْتِهِ وَقُوْتِ عِيَالِهِ فِي ذَلِكَ الْيَوْمِ وَيُزَكِّي عَنْ نَفْسِهِ وَعَمَّنْ تَلْزَمُهُ نَفَقَتُهُ مِنَ الْمُسْلِمِينَ
>
> Meaning: The existence of surplus beyond the staple food for himself and his dependents on that day, and he pays zakat for himself and for the Muslims whose maintenance he is obliged to provide.

What is meant by financial ease is that the person possesses surplus staple food for the night and day of Eid after meeting the needs of himself and those whose maintenance is upon him. Whoever does not possess such surplus is not obliged to pay Zakat al-Fitr, and this has been mentioned as a matter of consensus.

The scholars also discussed that the sa‘ to be paid must be beyond the need for housing, the servant required for service, and proper clothing. Imam al-Rafi‘i regarded this as a condition, and Imam al-Nawawi also authenticated that opinion.

### The Issue of Debt

There is a difference of opinion regarding whether debt prevents the obligation of Zakat al-Fitr. It is نقل from Imam al-Haramayn that there is consensus that debt prevents its obligation. However, Imam al-Rafi‘i considered it stronger that debt does not prevent the obligation of Zakat al-Fitr, just as it does not prevent Zakat al-Mal. Meanwhile, the author of *al-Hawi* and Imam al-Nawawi in *Nukat al-Tanbih* held that debt does prevent the obligation of Zakat al-Fitr.

### Zakat on Behalf of Those One Must Support

The causes of financial support are three: **ownership of slaves, marriage, and kinship**. Whoever must be supported due to one of these three causes, then that person’s Zakat al-Fitr is obligatory upon the one responsible for his maintenance.

As for an adult child who is financially independent, his father is not required to pay Zakat al-Fitr on his behalf. Likewise, a non-Muslim relative, a non-Muslim slave, and a non-Muslim wife are not included in Zakat al-Fitr, because Zakat al-Fitr is specifically prescribed for Muslims.

If a person possesses only enough food for the night and day of Eid, plus one sa‘ for himself, then according to the sounder opinion he gives priority to himself.

In the case of a slave needed for obligatory service, there are several opinions. The soundest view is that if the slave is needed for an obligatory service, such as serving a relative or a wife, he is treated like other wealth. But if he is only needed for tending land or livestock, Zakat al-Fitr is still obligatory.

## The Amount and Type of Zakat al-Fitr

The amount of Zakat al-Fitr is **one sa‘** of the staple food of the land where the person is paying it. The jurists state:

> فَيُخْرِجُ صَاعًا مِنْ قُوْتِ بَلَدِهِ وَقَدْرُهُ خَمْسَةُ أَرْطَالٍ وَثُلُثُ بِالْعِرَاقِيّ
>
> Meaning: He gives one sa‘ from the staple food of his land, and its measure is five and one-third Iraqi ratls.

This amount is established by the hadith of Ibn ‘Umar concerning the command of the Prophet ﷺ to give one sa‘ of dates or barley. Scholars estimated this in weights for ease of measurement, although the legally معتبر standard remains **volume**, not weight, because the law connected it to the sa‘, which is a dry measure.

Imam al-Rafi‘i held that one Baghdad ratl equals one hundred and thirty dirhams, making one sa‘ equal to five and one-third ratls. Imam al-Nawawi explained that one ratl equals one hundred and twenty-eight dirhams and four-sevenths of a dirham. According to the calculation approved by Imam al-Nawawi, one sa‘ is approximately **six hundred and eighty-five dirhams and five-sevenths of a dirham**. The difference in weight comes from the varying density of food types. Therefore, the real standard is the sa‘ as a measured volume, as known in the time of the Prophet ﷺ.

If a person cannot determine the measure precisely, he must give an amount that he is certain is not less than one sa‘. Some scholars also explained that one sa‘ is roughly equal to **four double-handfuls** taken by a man of average build.

### The Type of Food That Suffices

What is given must be from the **predominant staple food of the land**. Every staple food that people rely on as their main sustenance and that is among the foods subject to agricultural zakat is valid. In the well-known position of the school, wheat, rice, and what is similar to them are valid if they are the staple food of the land.

There are opinions that do not allow certain legumes, such as lentils and chickpeas, but do allow **cheese** according to the sounder opinion. Imam al-Nawawi emphasized the strength of this opinion because a sound hadith exists regarding it. Even milk and similar dairy products are treated likewise when they truly serve as the staple food of a local community.

It is not valid to give Zakat al-Fitr in the form of **clarified butter (samn)**, nor in the form of **cheese from which its butter has been extracted**, nor with **figs**, nor with **the meat of hunted animals**, even if some people in certain regions consume them. The standard is staple food that satisfies hunger and can be stored.

It is also a condition that the food given be **sound**, not spoiled, not altered in smell or color, not worm-infested, not damaged by moisture, and not eaten by moths. What is given must therefore be wholesome staple food fit for use.

According to the relied-upon opinion in the Shafi‘i school, it is not valid to give Zakat al-Fitr in **cash value**. Nor is it valid to give it as **flour**, whether coarse or fine, nor as **bread**, because the textual evidence mentions grains and similar items, which are the اصل and are more durable.

### The Standard of the Staple Food of the Land

The requirement to follow the staple food of the land is based on the welfare of the recipient. The stronger opinion is that what matters is the predominant staple food in that land **at the time of sunset on the last day of Ramadan**.

If a land has several staple foods without one being dominant, then one may choose any of them. If people consume a mixture of several staple types, then one gives the type most commonly consumed; if the proportions are equal, one may choose any of them.

### The Time of Payment

It is permissible to pay Zakat al-Fitr **from the beginning of Ramadan**. The most virtuous time to pay it is **before the Eid prayer**. It is forbidden to delay it until after the Day of Eid without a valid legal excuse.

### Additional Branch Issues

If a father pays the Zakat al-Fitr of his minor child from his own wealth, this is valid, even if the child has wealth of his own. The grandfather holds the same position as the father in this regard.

As for an adult child, his Zakat al-Fitr may not be paid except with his permission. If the child is mentally unsound, it may be paid on his behalf because he is treated like a minor.

A guardian other than the father or grandfather, such as an executor or the guardian of an orphan, may not pay Zakat al-Fitr from the orphan’s wealth except with the permission of the judge.

In distributing Zakat al-Fitr, it is recommended to prioritize relatives whose maintenance is not obligatory upon the giver, especially those within the prohibited degrees of kinship, then the nearer relatives, then neighbors.

Thus, the amount and type of Zakat al-Fitr have been regulated in fiqh with great precision, in terms of quantity, type of food, time of performance, etiquette of giving, and the order of those most deserving to receive it. And Allah knows best.

## Categories Eligible to Receive Zakat

Zakat must be distributed to the eight categories designated by Allah the Exalted in the Qur’an, in Surah At-Tawbah, verse 60:

> إِنَّمَا الصَّدَقَاتُ لِلْفُقَرَاءِ وَالْمَسَاكِينِ وَالْعَامِلِينَ عَلَيْهَا وَالْمُؤَلَّفَةِ قُلُوبُهُمْ وَفِي الرِّقَابِ وَالْغَارِمِينَ وَفِي سَبِيلِ اللَّهِ وَابْنِ السَّبِيلِ
>
> Meaning: Zakat is only for the poor, the needy, those employed to administer it, those whose hearts are to be reconciled, for freeing slaves, for those in debt, in the path of Allah, and for the stranded traveler.

If zakat is given to someone outside these categories who does not meet the conditions, then the zakat obligation has not been discharged.

### 1. The Poor (Faqir)

A **faqir** is a person who has no wealth and no means of livelihood at all, or who possesses both but not in an amount sufficient for his needs. For example, he may need ten dirhams but possess only two.

Owning the house in which he lives, the clothes he wears, or a servant he genuinely needs does not remove his status as poor. If he has a debt owed to him that has not yet matured, he may still receive zakat until the time for repayment arrives.

However, if he is able to work and suitable employment is available for him, then he is not entitled to receive zakat, based on the statement of the Prophet ﷺ:

> لَا حَقَّ فِيهَا لِغَنِيٍّ وَلَا لِذِي مِرَّةٍ سَوِيٍّ
>
> Meaning: There is no share of zakat for a rich person or for one who has physical strength and is able to work.

And:

> وَلَا لِذِي مِرَّةٍ مُكْتَسِبٍ
>
> Meaning: Nor for one who is strong and capable of earning.

If a person is able to work but is occupied with seeking sacred knowledge, such that working would interrupt his studies, then according to the stronger opinion he may receive zakat, provided that his knowledge is truly beneficial.

### 2. The Needy (Miskin)

A **miskin** is someone who has an income, but it is insufficient for his needs. For example, he may need ten dirhams and possess only seven. He remains entitled to receive zakat until his basic needs are met, such as food, drink, clothing, and other normal necessities.

### 3. The Zakat Administrators (‘Amil)

An **‘amil** is a person appointed by the ruler to collect and distribute zakat. He is entitled to receive a share of zakat not because of poverty, but because of his service.

It is required that the ‘amil be Muslim, free, trustworthy, and knowledgeable in the rulings of zakat, so that he knows which wealth is subject to zakat and who is entitled to receive it.

### 4. Those Whose Hearts Are to Be Reconciled (Mu’allaf)

**Al-mu’allafatu qulubuhum** refers to people whose hearts are to be inclined toward Islam for the benefit of the Muslim community. They may be Muslims whose faith is weak, influential figures whose support is hoped for, or individuals whose harm toward the Muslims may be prevented through such giving.

According to the stronger opinion in the Shafi‘i school, non-Muslims are no longer given zakat under this category, because Islam has become strong.

### 5. Slaves Seeking Freedom (Riqab)

**Riqab** refers to a mukatab slave, that is, a slave who has entered into a contract to purchase his freedom. Such people may be given zakat to help them complete the payment of their emancipation contract if they are unable to do so themselves.

### 6. Debtors (Gharim)

A **gharim** is a person in debt. He is entitled to receive zakat in several situations:

- A debt incurred for his own permissible need, when he is unable to repay it.
- A debt incurred to reconcile two disputing parties, even if he himself is wealthy.
- A debt incurred by taking responsibility for another person.

As long as the debt remains unpaid, he may receive zakat.

### 7. In the Cause of Allah (Fi Sabilillah)

**Fi sabilillah** refers to those who fight in the path of Allah and do not have a share from the public war treasury. They are given zakat to support their struggle.

### 8. The Stranded Traveler (Ibn Sabil)

An **ibn sabil** is a traveler who has run out of provisions during a journey that is not for sinful purposes. He may be given zakat even if he is wealthy in his homeland, so long as he lacks sufficient means during his travel.

## Rules of Distribution

If possible, zakat should cover all the existing categories. At a minimum, it should be given to three persons from each category, because they are mentioned in the plural form, except for the ‘amil, for whom one person is sufficient if that fulfills the task.

If three people are not found in a given category, then it is given to those who are available. If something remains after the distribution, then according to the stronger opinion the remainder should be redistributed among the existing eligible recipients.

Thus, these eight categories constitute a clear legal boundary in the distribution of zakat, so that the act of worship is valid and the zakat payer’s obligation is truly discharged. And Allah knows best.

## Categories Not Eligible to Receive Zakat

Besides the eight categories designated in the Qur’an in Surah At-Tawbah, verse 60, there are certain groups who are explicitly not permitted to receive zakat. This ruling is meant to ensure that zakat is truly delivered to those entitled to it according to the limits set by the Sharia.

### 1. The Wealthy

A wealthy person, whether wealthy through his possessions or through earning ability sufficient for his needs, is not entitled to receive zakat. The Messenger of Allah ﷺ said:

> لَا حَظَّ فِيهَا لِغَنِيٍّ وَلَا لِذِي مِرَّةٍ سَوِيٍّ
>
> Meaning: There is no share of zakat for a rich person or for one who possesses physical strength and is able to work.

A person who is able to work and earn enough to meet his needs is not entitled to zakat. However, if he cannot find suitable employment or his income does not meet his basic needs, then he returns to the status of poor or needy and may receive zakat according to his condition.

### 2. Slaves

A slave may not receive zakat because his maintenance is the responsibility of his master and because he does not have full ownership of wealth. Therefore, he is not included among the zakat recipients except in the special case of a mukatab slave under the category of riqab.

### 3. Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib

They are not permitted to receive zakat, based on the statement of the Prophet ﷺ:

> إِنَّ هَذِهِ الصَّدَقَةَ أَوْسَاخُ النَّاسِ وَإِنَّهَا لَا تَحِلُّ لِمُحَمَّدٍ وَلَا لِآلِ مُحَمَّدٍ
>
> Meaning: Indeed, this charity is the impurities of people’s wealth, and it is not lawful for Muhammad or for the family of Muhammad.

In another narration, when Hasan took a date from the zakat and put it into his mouth, the Messenger of Allah ﷺ immediately took it out and told him to spit it out.

Regarding the **mawla** (freed slave) of Banu Hashim and Banu al-Muttalib, there is a difference of opinion, but the stronger opinion states that they also are not entitled to receive zakat, because the status of a mawla follows that of the people who freed him.

### 4. Those Whom the Zakat Payer Is Obliged to Support

A person may not give his zakat to those whose maintenance he is already obliged to provide, such as his parents, children, or wife, because such giving would in reality return to himself in the form of reducing his own financial obligations.

They are considered already provided for through the maintenance he must give them. However, if that maintenance is insufficient for their basic needs, there is an opinion allowing them to receive zakat on the basis of poverty. In addition, they may still receive zakat for a reason other than poverty or need, such as being an ‘amil or a gharim, if they meet the relevant conditions.

### 5. Non-Muslims

Zakat is not given to non-Muslims, whether zakat al-fitr or zakat al-mal. The Messenger of Allah ﷺ said that zakat is taken from their wealthy and given to their poor, which has been understood to mean the poor among the Muslims.

Therefore, zakat is a right of needy Muslims and is not given to non-Muslims according to the relied-upon opinion in the Shafi‘i school.

Thus, zakat has clear limits in its distribution. Not every person in need may receive zakat if he belongs to a category excluded by the Sharia. Knowing those who are not entitled to zakat is just as important as knowing those who are entitled to it, so that zakat may be distributed validly and the zakat payer’s religious duty is truly discharged before Allah the Exalted. And Allah knows best.

## Voluntary Charity (Sadaqah Tathawwu‘)

**Sadaqah tathawwu‘** is voluntary charity that is not obligatory like zakat, yet it is highly encouraged in Islam. Its ruling is recommended, and it is especially emphasized during noble times, particularly in the month of Ramadan. In this month, increasing charity and expanding one’s spending are encouraged, because it is a month of blessing, forgiveness, and multiplied reward.

Likewise, charity is highly recommended at times of urgent need, such as during hardship, illness, travel, warfare, and the season of Hajj. Charity is also especially virtuous during times of particular merit, such as the first ten days of Dhul-Hijjah and the days of Eid.

In giving charity, it is recommended to prioritize one’s relatives and neighbors. Giving to relatives is more virtuous than giving to others because it combines two merits at once: charity and maintaining kinship ties. Even a relative who lives farther away takes precedence over a neighbor who is not a relative. Giving to the relative who is most in need and closest in blood relation is preferable, then to those more distant. Giving secretly is better than giving openly, because it is closer to sincerity and farther from showing off.

It is disliked to give charity from low-quality or defective goods. A Muslim should be careful not to take dubious or unlawful wealth and give it in charity, because charity from unlawful wealth is not accepted and instead brings sin. ‘Abdullah ibn ‘Umar said that rejecting one dirham of unlawful wealth was more beloved to him than giving one hundred thousand dirhams of unlawful wealth in charity. This shows that the purity of wealth is more important than the size of the donation.

A person who has family members to support, essential needs to meet, and debts to pay is not allowed to give charity from wealth that ought to be used for those obligations. If he has surplus wealth after fulfilling his obligations, then he may give all of that surplus in charity if he can bear possible hardship. But if he cannot bear it and would end up neglecting his obligations, then this is not permitted.

It is not lawful for a wealthy person to take voluntary charity while falsely displaying poverty. In a hadith it is stated:

> اتَّقُوا النَّارَ وَلَوْ بِشِقِّ تَمْرَةٍ
>
> Meaning: Protect yourselves from the Fire, even with half a date.

This shows that the value of charity lies not merely in its amount, but in sincerity and purity of heart.

Allah the Exalted says in Surah Az-Zalzalah, verse 7:

> فَمَن يَعْمَلْ مِثْقَالَ ذَرَّةٍ خَيْرًا يَرَهُ
>
> Meaning: Whoever does an atom’s weight of good will see it.

And in Surah Ali ‘Imran, verse 92:

> لَن تَنَالُوا الْبِرَّ حَتَّى تُنفِقُوا مِمَّا تُحِبُّونَ
>
> Meaning: You will never attain true righteousness until you spend from that which you love.

It is recommended to direct voluntary charity especially to righteous people who are genuinely in need. It is disliked for a person who has given charity to take back that same item later through purchase or as a gift. It is forbidden to remind others of one’s generosity, because doing so destroys the reward of charity.

Thus, voluntary charity is a wide gate to goodness, purification of the soul, and nearness to Allah. It complements obligatory zakat with the dimension of sincerity and generosity, strengthens social bonds, and becomes a cause of blessing and protection from the Fire. And Allah knows best.

### Conclusion

Zakat al-Fitr, Zakat al-Mal, and voluntary charity are all part of the system of financial worship in Islam, built upon the principles of purification of the soul, social justice, and the collective responsibility of the Muslim community. Zakat al-Fitr becomes obligatory due to Islam, the entrance of its time with the setting of the sun at the end of Ramadan, and the existence of financial ease. It has a specified amount, namely one sa‘ of the staple food of the local land, with detailed rulings concerning its type, quality, and time of payment explained thoroughly in fiqh.

Its distribution is not left to personal preference, but is restricted by the Sharia to the eight categories that Allah has designated in the Qur’an, while also excluding categories not entitled to receive it. Precision in understanding who is eligible and who is not is itself part of the trust of this act of worship, because zakat is invalid if it is not placed in the category prescribed for it.

As for voluntary charity, it opens a wider space for the Muslim to increase in goodness beyond what is obligatory. It becomes a means of drawing closer to Allah, strengthening kinship ties, helping those in need, and cultivating sincerity. Islam encourages that charity be given from good wealth, without ostentation, without reminding recipients of one’s favor, and without neglecting obligatory maintenance and responsibilities.

By understanding all these rulings in a complete way, a Muslim does not merely perform the obligation formally, but also appreciates the wisdom and purposes of the Sharia behind it. Zakat and charity are not simply a transfer of wealth, but a means of purifying the soul, strengthening communal solidarity, and bringing about blessing in worldly life and salvation in the Hereafter. And Allah knows best.
