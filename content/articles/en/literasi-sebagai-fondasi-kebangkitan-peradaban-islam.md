## Introduction

From the moment the first revelation descended upon the Prophet Muhammad ?, reading became one of the primary foundations of the rise of Islamic civilization. The command *Iqra'* in the Qur'an makes clear that literacy is the first step toward knowledge. Classical Muslim scholars emphasized that reading is not merely a mechanical act, but a process of reflection and understanding that shapes both intellect and character.

In the time of the Prophet ?, literacy grew through informal education in homes and mosques. The Companions actively copied the Qur'an and studied hadith, building a strong scholarly tradition. Reading, writing, and discussion became regular features of Muslim daily life. During the Rashidun and Abbasid periods, literacy expanded rapidly. The caliphs encouraged the copying of the Qur'an and the building of libraries. Baghdad became a center of world knowledge through Bayt al-Hikmah, where works from Greek, Persian, and Indian traditions were translated, opening new horizons in philosophy, mathematics, astronomy, and medicine.

Classical Islamic madrasas and universities then became wide centers of education. Institutions such as the Nizamiyyah in Baghdad, al-Qarawiyyin in Morocco, and al-Azhar in Egypt offered broad curricula covering religious sciences, philosophy, and the natural sciences. This demonstrates that literacy in Islam was not only an act of spiritual devotion but also a means of intellectual and social development.

Muslim philosophers also stressed this point. Al-Farabi argued that a society that reads and thinks critically will become intelligent and prosperous. Ibn Sina emphasized the necessity of knowledge for balancing intellect and faith, while Ibn Rushd encouraged the translation of classical books as a way to preserve and extend knowledge. Islamic literacy teaches that reading is a means to understand both the world and the Creator.

The statements attributed to major scholars underscore the importance of literacy. Imam al-Ghazali described knowledge as light for the soul, while Ibn Sina likened knowledge to a treasure beyond price. Such ideas guided generations of Muslims to pursue knowledge throughout their lives.

Literacy also contributed to the economic and administrative rise of Muslim societies. Literate merchants and scholars were able to record transactions, manage broad trade networks, and build institutions based on knowledge. Reading and writing became essential for social organization and economic effectiveness.

Women too played an important role. 'A'ishah (may Allah be pleased with her) was known as a teacher of hadith and religious understanding. Female literacy helped transmit knowledge, educate the family, and preserve the intellectual inheritance of Muslims across generations.

In the modern age, reading remains a foundation for Islamic renewal. Digital literacy, scientific research, and formal education are all extensions of the classical reading tradition. Muslims who master literacy are better equipped to face global challenges, develop knowledge-based economies, and remain grounded in faith.

### The Early History of Literacy in Islam

Literacy in Islam began with the first revelation and the command *Iqra'*, meaning ?Read.? This command established reading as the first obligation for human beings in order to know religion and knowledge. The Prophet ? not only conveyed revelation, but also encouraged his community to write hadith, record important events, and document divine commands. This became the basis of a literacy tradition that emphasized preservation, teaching, and accuracy.

The Companions were the first major transmitters of this literacy. They learned to read and write, copied the Qur'an, and preserved revelation with great care. This effort demanded discipline and precision so that later generations could study the Qur'an correctly.

Literacy in early Islam was not limited to sacred texts. The Companions also learned practical knowledge related to commerce, administration, and law. This shows that literacy became a foundation for social and economic organization as well.

Women were active in this process too. 'A'ishah (may Allah be pleased with her), for example, became a teacher of hadith and religious knowledge. Many other women wrote, copied, and taught knowledge in homes and mosques, showing that literacy was inclusive from the earliest period.

The early literate Companions taught children and adults to write letters, record transactions, and preserve legal guidance. Reading and writing became normal habits in homes and mosques. Education was direct, dialogical, and based on memorization together with understanding.

The copying of the Qur'an was one of the greatest milestones of early Islamic literacy. Figures such as Zayd ibn Thabit were entrusted to record revelation from parchments, bones, and palm leaves. This ensured the preservation of the Qur'an and laid the groundwork for Islamic education.

Literacy was also directly tied to da'wah. Through letters and written records, the Prophet ? communicated Islam to various regions, building networks of communication and learning.

Informal schools emerged in houses, mosques, and circles of knowledge. Students learned to read the Qur'an, write, and understand the basic rulings of religion. This produced educated generations ready to carry knowledge into society.

Literacy also helped develop social and political order. The Companions wrote regulations, contracts, and legal documents. This demonstrates that literacy was central not only to worship, but also to the establishment of an organized community.

Reading and writing in early Islam were never treated as empty technical skills. They were linked to understanding, discussion, and reflection. The Prophet ? emphasized literacy as a means of cultivating intellect and moral awareness, not merely collecting information.

The writing of hadith became another major component of this early literacy. The Companions documented the Prophet's words and actions in order to preserve guidance accurately. This helped form the Islamic scientific method, with its strong emphasis on verification and reliability of transmission.

Alongside religious texts, the Companions also recorded trade matters, official letters, and financial notes. In this way literacy became a foundation of economic and administrative development.

### The Era of the Caliphate and the Development of Literacy

The era of the caliphate marked a decisive phase in the history of Muslim literacy. From the time of Abu Bakr to 'Uthman ibn 'Affan, reading and writing became central to education and governance. Abu Bakr took the historic step of commissioning the codification of the Qur'an, which had previously existed in individual records. His aim was to preserve revelation so that later generations could study it accurately.

During the caliphate of 'Umar ibn al-Khattab, literacy expanded into the field of administration. Official letters, tax records, and legal documentation required broader reading and writing skills among officials and sections of society. 'Uthman later standardized the Qur'anic text and sent official copies to the provinces, preserving textual unity and minimizing differences in recitation.

The development of literacy did not remain confined to religious texts. Scholars began writing books on law, astronomy, medicine, and philosophy, laying the foundations of a strong scientific tradition. The Abbasid period represented the height of this movement. Baghdad became a global center of learning through Bayt al-Hikmah, which served as a library and translation center for scientific works from Greek, Persian, and Indian sources.

This vast translation movement opened new horizons in science and philosophy. Madrasas and scholarly gatherings became places for debating law, philosophy, and scientific disciplines. Translation flourished especially under Harun al-Rashid and al-Ma'mun, when scholars from different backgrounds collaborated in making the world's knowledge accessible in Arabic.

This environment gave rise to major scholars such as al-Khwarizmi in mathematics, al-Razi in medicine, and al-Farabi in philosophy. Their works became references not only in the Muslim world but later in Europe as well. The Nizamiyyah of Baghdad became a model for formal education, combining religious learning, logic, philosophy, and practical sciences. Al-Qarawiyyin in Morocco and al-Azhar in Egypt further expanded this scholarly network.

Women's literacy also continued to matter. Women taught, copied manuscripts, and guided younger generations. Book copying became an important craft, and the translation, commentary, and dissemination of classical works built a broad scientific culture.

Bayt al-Hikmah was not only a library. It was also a center of discussion and research, where manuscripts were compared, critiqued, and explained. The books produced in this period covered medicine, mathematics, astronomy, geography, law, ethics, and theology. Literacy thus became one of the major pillars of Islamic civilization.

It also transformed administration. Official documents, diplomatic correspondence, and tax records all required literate functionaries. Scholars such as al-Kindi wrote about philosophy and natural science, helping bridge religion and reason. Their writings were later translated into Latin and became part of the transmission of knowledge from the Islamic world to Europe.

For this reason, the era of the caliphate demonstrates that literacy was not a private cultural preference, but a collective strategy for the advancement of civilization.

### The Role of Madrasas and Universities

The madrasa became one of the chief educational institutions in Muslim society from the early centuries onward. It was not merely a place to learn how to read the Qur'an or memorize hadith. It was a center for developing the intellect, morals, and practical skills. Early madrasas grew out of mosque circles and informal classes in which both children and adults learned reading, writing, and religious understanding.

From the beginning, the madrasa combined theoretical study and practical training. Students read classical texts, wrote notes, and memorized passages while also seeking to understand their meanings. This created a literacy that was reflective and analytical rather than purely mechanical.

As the caliphates developed, madrasas increasingly received public support through waqf endowments and donations. This made it possible to establish buildings, libraries, and more complete educational facilities. Such support shows that literacy and education were treated as essential investments in the future of civilization.

One of the most famous examples was the Nizamiyyah in Baghdad, founded by Nizam al-Mulk in the eleventh century. It became a model for formal education with a systematic curriculum that included religious sciences, law, philosophy, logic, and practical sciences such as mathematics and astronomy.

At the Nizamiyyah, students were trained not only to receive information but also to participate in discussion, critical reflection, and writing commentary. This created independent intellectuals capable of analyzing social and legal problems.

Al-Qarawiyyin in Morocco, founded in the ninth century, is another early example of an Islamic university. Its curriculum covered religion, philosophy, astronomy, medicine, and literature. Al-Azhar in Egypt likewise became a central institution in the dissemination of literacy and learning, attracting students from many lands.

The curricula of classical madrasas and universities consistently emphasized balance between religion and reason. Students learned law and Qur'anic interpretation alongside philosophy and practical sciences. This created generations able to integrate faith and rational inquiry.

Madrasas also contributed to female literacy. Many women taught Qur'an and religious sciences in homes and learning spaces, broadening literacy within Muslim society. Students learned to copy manuscripts, preserve books, and document knowledge carefully. These institutions thus helped create a culture not only of reading but of scholarly production.

Madrasas and universities also became centers of research, translation, debate, and commentary. Teachers and students engaged with Greek, Persian, and Indian works while producing original scholarship in Arabic. Libraries made it possible for students to read, summarize, and investigate independently.

In this way, these institutions proved that literacy in Islam was multi-disciplinary, socially rooted, and central to the rise of a learned civilization.

### Philosophy and Literacy

In Islamic civilization, philosophy and literacy were deeply connected and mutually reinforcing. Literacy was not understood merely as the technical ability to read and write, but as a means of deep reflection, the understanding of reality, and the search for truth. Philosophy encouraged people to ask questions, analyze, and reflect on the meaning of knowledge, existence, and humanity's relation to God. Without literacy, philosophy could not flourish; without philosophy, literacy would lose much of its intellectual depth.

The first revelation, *Iqra'*, carries profound philosophical significance. It does not merely command the reading of written text, but also the reading of the world, of history, and of the self. This became the starting point of an Islamic intellectual tradition in which literacy served wisdom, not information alone.

Muslim scholars and philosophers saw knowledge as a light that illuminates the soul and intellect. Imam al-Ghazali stressed that reading and writing should purify the mind from ignorance and guide the human being toward truth and virtuous character. Al-Kindi, often described as the first Muslim philosopher, treated philosophy as humanity's effort to grasp the realities of things to the best of its ability, and literacy was the vehicle through which such thought could be preserved, debated, and transmitted.

Al-Farabi argued that the virtuous society is one built upon learning, reason, and disciplined thought. Literacy, in his view, was essential for the formation of *al-madinah al-fadilah*, the virtuous city. Ibn Sina likewise presented reading and writing as indispensable to the formation of the intellect, enabling human beings to connect sensory experience with abstract reason.

The reading tradition in Islamic philosophy was never passive. Scholars read in order to understand, critique, summarize, and develop ideas. They produced commentaries, glosses, and short treatises in an ongoing dialogue across generations. Ibn Rushd emphasized the importance of reading classical philosophy in order to understand nature and divine wisdom, arguing that sound reason and revelation do not contradict one another when properly understood.

Philosophy also shaped the ethics of reading. Reading was to be undertaken with adab, purpose, and moral responsibility. Knowledge gained through literacy was expected to benefit the individual and society, not become a cause of arrogance.

Madrasas and universities made philosophy an important component of literacy. Students learned logic in order to think systematically, distinguish valid from invalid argument, and evaluate claims rationally. This philosophical literacy enriched both religious and scientific understanding.

Scholarly debate and discussion became marks of Islamic philosophical literacy. Scholars read one another's works, wrote rebuttals and refinements, and helped build a dynamic intellectual culture. Philosophy also helped develop social and political thought, shaping conceptions of justice, leadership, and the ideal society.

In the modern context, philosophical literacy remains highly relevant. A rapidly changing world, moral crises, and technological development all require critical and reflective thinking. The literacy tradition inherited from Islamic philosophy remains an important resource for engaging modern knowledge without losing spiritual identity.

### Scholarly Quotations on Reading

- Imam al-Ghazali emphasized that reading is a path to the light of knowledge. According to him, knowledge acquired through reading cleanses the heart from ignorance and leads the human being toward sound understanding of religion and life.
- Al-Ghazali also stressed that reading should not stop at mastery of words alone. It must be accompanied by reflection so that knowledge shapes character and directs human conduct toward goodness.
- Ibn Sina viewed reading as a principal foundation of intellectual formation. Through reading, the human mind is trained to think systematically, logically, and to connect multiple fields of knowledge.
- According to Ibn Sina, knowledge preserved in books allows human beings to transcend time. By reading, the thought of earlier scholars remains alive for later generations.
- Jalal al-Din Rumi portrayed books as true companions of the human being. Through reading, a person can converse with wisdom, discover meaning, and soothe inner restlessness.
- Rumi also held that true reading is not only an activity of the eyes and mind, but also of the heart. Knowledge that does not touch the heart will not lead to spiritual transformation.
- Ibn Khaldun stated that civilization can flourish only in a literate society. Reading and writing are among the chief marks of a developed people able to preserve knowledge, history, and governance.
- In the *Muqaddimah*, Ibn Khaldun argued that reading history is essential to understanding the causes of civilizational rise and decline. Without reading, humanity repeats the same errors.
- Imam al-Shafi'i emphasized the value of reading from an early age. He believed the habit of reading sharpens the intellect and strengthens memory in the pursuit of knowledge.
- Muslim scholars generally agreed that reading is a form of intellectual worship. With the proper intention, reading becomes a means of drawing near to Allah and building a knowledgeable and ethical civilization.

### Conclusion

Literacy has played an enormous role in the development of Islamic civilization from its earliest period to the modern age. The command to read in the first revelation shows that Islam was built upon a foundation of knowledge. Through reading and writing, Muslims were able to understand revelation deeply while also developing a wide range of sciences beneficial to human life.

History demonstrates that the glory of Islamic civilization was inseparable from its rich culture of literacy. In the era of the caliphates, translation, authorship, and the building of libraries became engines of knowledge. Madrasas and universities produced major scholars, scientists, and thinkers whose works shaped both the Muslim world and the West.

Philosophy and literacy together formed habits of critical and rational reflection. Through reading, scholars not only inherited knowledge from earlier generations, but also developed new thought suited to the needs of their time. This proves that literacy is not merely an academic activity; it is a means for building a dynamic and enduring civilization.

Reading and writing therefore remain among the essential keys to preserving Islamic knowledge and renewing Islamic civilization. A strong culture of literacy can produce generations that are knowledgeable, ethical, and capable of contributing positively to society and to the wider world.
