## Fasting for the Traveler and the Sick

Islam gives relief in fasting for the sick person and for the traveler undertaking a valid long journey. Both are allowed to break the Ramadan fast and make it up later.

> وَالْمَرِيضُ وَالْمُسَافِرُ سَفَرًا طَوِيلًا يُفْطِرَانِ وَيَقْضِيَانِ
>
> The sick person and the traveler on a long journey may break the fast and make it up later.

This ruling is based on the Qur'anic verse that states whoever is ill or on a journey should fast the same number of days later. The details depend on the condition. A person with severe illness may leave the fast if fasting brings clear hardship or danger. If the illness is intermittent, the ruling is tied to the actual state at the time of fasting.

Likewise, the traveler receives this concession when the journey is long and permissible. There is no concession for a short journey or for travel undertaken in disobedience. The jurists also distinguish between a person who began the day as a resident and then traveled later, and one who was already traveling from the start.

In all such matters, the guiding principle is mercy without abuse. If fasting does not harm the traveler, then fasting may be better. If it brings harm or significant weakness, then breaking the fast is better and may even become obligatory.

## Voluntary Fasting

Voluntary fasting is strongly recommended in Islam. The jurists encouraged frequent supererogatory fasting while also discussing its limits and the circumstances in which it may become disliked.

Among the most emphasized recommended fasts are Mondays and Thursdays, the white days of each lunar month, six days of Shawwal, the ninth and tenth of Muharram, and the Day of 'Arafah for those not performing Hajj. The first ten days of Dhu al-Hijjah and the end of the month were also highlighted in many discussions.

The jurists differed on fasting continuously throughout the year. Some disliked it because of possible harm or neglect of rights, while others permitted or even praised it if the person remained physically able and did not fail in other obligations. The balanced position is that if such fasting leads to harm, neglect, or imbalance, it is disliked; otherwise, it may be permissible.

There are also important restrictions. A woman should not fast a voluntary fast while her husband is present except with his permission. Someone who starts an obligatory makeup fast may not break it without a valid excuse. A voluntary fast, by contrast, is recommended to be completed but does not carry the same binding force once begun.

The jurists also discouraged singling out Friday alone for fasting, and similarly singled out Saturday or Sunday alone in certain discussions, unless joined to another day or connected to a broader recommended practice.

## I'tikaf

I'tikaf is a recommended devotional retreat in the mosque based on intention and remaining in the mosque.

> الِاعْتِكَافُ مُسْتَحَبٌّ وَلَهُ شَرْطَانِ النِّيَّةُ وَاللُّبْثُ فِي الْمَسْجِدِ
>
> I'tikaf is recommended, and it has two conditions: intention and staying in the mosque.

Linguistically, i'tikaf means remaining with something. In the legal sense, it means a special form of staying in the mosque as worship. Its recommendation is established by the Qur'an, the Sunnah, and consensus.

It is recommended at all times, but it is particularly emphasized in the last ten nights of Ramadan, following the practice of the Prophet and in search of Laylat al-Qadr. The jurists also discuss where Laylat al-Qadr is most likely sought, with many emphasizing the last ten nights and especially the odd nights.

The essential elements of i'tikaf include intention, remaining in the mosque, the person performing it being legally fit, and the place being a mosque. A congregational mosque is often better, especially if one may need Jumu'ah.

## Vowed I'tikaf

When i'tikaf is made obligatory upon oneself through a vow, its rules become stricter.

> وَلَا يَخْرُجُ الْمُعْتَكِفُ مِنَ الِاعْتِكَافِ الْمَنْذُورِ إِلَّا لِحَاجَةِ الْإِنسَانِ ... وَيَبْطُلُ بِالْوَطْءِ
>
> The one in vowed i'tikaf does not leave except for necessary human needs and valid excuses, and it is invalidated by intercourse.

If someone vows i'tikaf for a specified period, he must fulfill it as he described it. If continuity was made part of the vow, then breaking that continuity can require restarting according to the stronger juristic view. If continuity was not explicitly required, then the matter is lighter.

Leaving the mosque is allowed only for recognized human needs or valid excuses, such as relieving oneself, necessary washing after impurity, unavoidable food needs in some cases, menstruation, nifas, or a serious illness that makes remaining impossible. Mere preference, social visits, or optional attendance at a funeral are not treated as legitimate reasons.

Sexual intercourse invalidates i'tikaf when done intentionally with awareness. The Qur'an explicitly forbids sexual approach while in i'tikaf in the mosques. Other acts connected to deliberate sexual gratification can also invalidate it according to juristic detail.

The overall spirit of these rulings is clear: i'tikaf is a protected state of withdrawal for worship, and vowed i'tikaf is even more guarded because it has become binding through the servant’s own promise to Allah.
