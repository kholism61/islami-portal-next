## History of Al-Jami' al-Azhar

Al-Jami' al-Azhar is the oldest university in the world that has continued to function without interruption until the present day. It is also one of the most important congregational mosques in Egypt and among the most influential institutions in the Muslim world. For more than a thousand years, Al-Azhar has been a destination for scholars, teachers, and students from every region. It developed not only as an educational institution, but also as a symbol of civilization, a lighthouse of moderation, a primary scholarly authority, and a pulpit of da'wah that consistently defends sound Islamic values.

Throughout its long history, Al-Azhar has carried scientific, religious, national, and humanitarian responsibilities, becoming a source of pride for Egypt and for the wider Muslim world.

### The Background of Its Foundation

Al-Azhar was founded on 24 Jumada al-Ula 359 H / 4 April 970 CE by the commander Jawhar al-Siqilli under the orders of the Fatimid caliph al-Mu'izz li-Dinillah, one year after the establishment of Cairo as the new Fatimid capital. Construction lasted about twenty-seven months, and the mosque was officially opened for Friday prayer on 7 Ramadan 361 H / 21 June 972 CE.

From the beginning, it served not only as a place of worship, but also as a center for the dissemination of knowledge and the doctrine supported by the ruling state of that time. The name “Al-Azhar” is commonly linked to Sayyidah Fatimah al-Zahra, daughter of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and wife of Sayyidina 'Ali ibn Abi Talib رضي الله عنه, whose name symbolizes nobility and light.

Soon after its foundation, Al-Azhar grew into a major institution of higher learning. A wide range of disciplines was taught within it, and over time it came to resemble a complete university with resident scholars, international students, curricula, libraries, and residential systems.

### Transformation in the Ayyubid and Mamluk Eras

After the fall of the Fatimid dynasty and the rise of Salah al-Din al-Ayyubi in 567 H / 1171 CE, major changes took place in the religious orientation of Egypt. Friday prayer at Al-Azhar was suspended for a period, and a number of Sunni madrasas were established to strengthen the doctrine of Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah. This marked the end of Al-Azhar's association with the Fatimid Shi'i state and redirected it toward Sunni scholarship.

During the Mamluk period (648-923 H / 1250-1517 CE), Al-Azhar experienced remarkable revival. Friday prayer returned in 665 H / 1267 CE. The Mamluk sultans gave strong support to Al-Azhar as a center of the four Sunni legal schools. After the fall of Baghdad to the Mongols and the weakening of Muslim power in Andalusia and North Africa, the hopes of many Muslims gathered around Al-Azhar as a fortress of learning and religion.

In this period the fields of study expanded greatly. Theology, fiqh, legal theory, tafsir, hadith, Arabic language sciences, logic, philosophy, history, astronomy, mathematics, and other rational disciplines were taught there. Additional schools such as al-Taybarsiyyah, al-Aqbughawiyyah, and al-Jawhariyyah enriched its scholarly life. One of the most important features of this age was the *arwaq* system, the residential lodges that became a sign of Al-Azhar's international character.

### Development in the Ottoman Period

Under Ottoman rule (923-1213 H / 1517-1798 CE), Al-Azhar preserved its traditions and influence over the course of three centuries. It remained a center of religious scholarship and a stronghold of the Arabic language, helping preserve Egypt's intellectual and cultural identity under Ottoman political authority. During this period, the office of Shaykh al-Azhar emerged as the highest scholarly authority, and the teaching circles remained active with leading scholars from the eastern and western regions of the Muslim world.

### The Traditional Educational System

For centuries, Al-Azhar's educational system was natural, open, and flexible. There were no rigid age limits, no formal grade levels in the modern sense, and no written examinations or official diplomas in the earliest period. A teacher would teach near a designated pillar in the mosque at a known time, while students sat around him in circles, listening, taking notes, and debating. When a student had mastered the material, he would be examined through scholarly discussion and direct questioning. If successful, he would receive an *ijazah* from his teacher authorizing him to teach.

More than twenty branches of knowledge were studied, including fiqh, usul al-fiqh, tafsir, hadith transmission and methodology, mustalah al-hadith, theology, tasawwuf, philosophy, grammar, morphology, rhetoric, logic, mathematics, algebra, astronomy, and prosody. Lessons continued from after Fajr until 'Isha'. Traditionally, learning moved through three broad stages: introductory, intermediate, and advanced texts, with students progressing according to intellectual readiness rather than formal bureaucracy.

Over time this structure was gradually regulated and renewed until Al-Azhar became the modern university system known today.

### Architecture and Renovation

Across the centuries, Al-Azhar received sustained care from caliphs, sultans, and rulers through repeated renovation and expansion. The Mamluk period is often regarded as one of the golden ages of its architecture. The latest major renovation was completed in 1439 H / 2018 CE after nearly three years of restoration. The mosque complex now covers approximately 12,000 square meters and combines deep historical significance with majestic Islamic architecture.

### Arwaq: A Symbol of Universality

One of Al-Azhar's most distinctive features was its *arwaq* system of lodges and student residences. Each community of students from different lands had its own riwaq that provided lodging and basic necessities free of charge during study. There were arwaq for students from Turkey, Syria, Hijaz, Yemen, Kurdistan, Iraq, Afghanistan, India, the Malay-Indonesian world, the Maghrib, Sudan, Chad, West Africa, Mauritania, Ethiopia, and many others. Even Egyptian students had arwaq connected to their regions and legal affiliations.

This system endured until the establishment of Madinat al-Bu'uth al-Islamiyyah in 1959, which became a modern residential center for international students.

### National Role and Public Struggle

Al-Azhar has never been merely a center of learning. It has also been a major force in Egypt's national and social life. Its scholars stood in the front ranks in defending the rights of the people and resisting injustice. Al-Azhar led resistance against the French campaign of 1798-1801, supported the appointment of Muhammad 'Ali in 1805 on the condition of justice and consultation with scholars, and stood against the British expedition of 1807.

It also played a role in the 'Urabi Revolution of 1882, the Revolution of 1919, and many decisive national moments, including the era of World War II and the Tripartite Aggression of 1956. Throughout these events it emerged as a symbol of national dignity, freedom, and commitment to justice.

### Conclusion

With its rich and far-reaching history, Al-Jami' al-Azhar is not merely a mosque or a university. It is a civilizational institution that unites scholarship, da'wah, moderation, and national service. It stands as a witness to the long history of the Muslim world, a fortress of Sunni learning, a center for civilizational dialogue, and a symbol of the continuity of the Islamic scholarly tradition into the modern age.

Its influence extends far beyond geographic borders, making it one of the most important institutions in the intellectual and social history of the Muslim ummah.
