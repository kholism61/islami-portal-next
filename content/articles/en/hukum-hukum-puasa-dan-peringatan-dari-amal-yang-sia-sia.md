## Introduction

Fasting is one of the acts of worship that holds a great and noble position in Islam. It is not merely abstaining from food and drink, but also a spiritual discipline that requires guarding one’s tongue, heart, and actions. Numerous texts from the Qur’an and the Prophetic hadith explain the virtues of fasting, while also warning that this act of worship can become devoid of reward if a person fails to observe its proper etiquette and limits.

Scholars have also explained in detail the legal rulings related to fasting, including the times in which fasting is prohibited, the times when it is disliked, the matters that invalidate fasting, and the concessions given to certain individuals. All of this demonstrates that the legislation of fasting is built upon a balance between the firmness of legal rulings and the mercy of Allah toward His servants.

The following discussion presents several important explanations about fasting, beginning with warnings against deeds that render fasting fruitless, and continuing with practical rulings concerning its observance as explained in the classical books of fiqh.

## Legal Rulings of Fasting and Warnings Against Deeds that Nullify its Reward

It is mentioned in a hadith:

> رُبَّ صَائِمٍ لَيْسَ لَهُ مِنْ صِيَامِهِ إِلَّا الْجُوعُ، وَرُبَّ قَائِمٍ لَيْسَ لَهُ مِنْ قِيَامِهِ إِلَّا السَّهَرُ  
>
> “How many people fast but gain nothing from their fasting except hunger, and how many stand in prayer at night but gain nothing from their prayer except sleeplessness.” (Narrated by Al-Hakim)

Imam Al-Hakim stated that this hadith meets the conditions of Al-Bukhari. This is because *hajr*—meaning obscene or useless speech—can invalidate the reward of fasting. This was clearly stated by Al-Mawardi and Ar-Ruyani.

We say: among the great calamities is what some oppressive people do when they follow a tyrant, take wealth unlawfully, then prepare food from that wealth and distribute it in charity. As a result, their misfortune spreads even to the poor who receive that food.

An even greater calamity is the presence of corrupt jurists and misguided Sufis who repeatedly accept food from such oppressors. They justify this by claiming, “We purchase it as a debt obligation.” However, dealing with someone whose wealth is mostly unlawful is considered disliked, and in *Sharh Muslim* it is mentioned that it is even prohibited. This ruling is analogous to the issue of accepting gifts from rulers, since the underlying meaning is the same.

The ignorant do not realize that such actions encourage the consumption of unlawful wealth and sitting with immoral people. This act is forbidden, even if done under the excuse of courtesy, and there is no recognized scholarly disagreement about this. Some scholars even considered it a major sin. Qadhi Iyadh attributed this opinion to scholars of rigorous scholarship.

In such situations, they fail to forbid wrongdoing, which becomes a cause for calamities to descend upon the community. It may even lead to destruction and curse upon them through the tongues of the prophets. The Noble Qur’an has clearly stated this. A more detailed discussion can be found in the book *Qama‘ al-Nufus*. And Allah knows best.

## Times in Which Fasting Is Prohibited

> وَيُحَرَّمُ صِيَامُ خَمْسَةِ أَيَّامٍ: الْعِيدَيْنِ وَأَيَّامِ التَّشْرِيقِ الثَّلَاثَةِ  

“Fasting is prohibited on five days: the two days of Eid and the three days of Tashriq.”

Fasting on the days of Eid al-Fitr and Eid al-Adha is invalid according to the consensus of the Muslims. Fasting on those days is prohibited, and whoever does so commits a sin, because the act of worship itself becomes an act of disobedience.

It is mentioned in the two authentic collections:

> نَهَى رَسُولُ اللَّهِ ﷺ عَنْ صِيَامِ يَوْمَيْنِ: يَوْمِ الْفِطْرِ وَيَوْمِ الْأَضْحَى  

“The Messenger of Allah ﷺ forbade fasting on two days: the Day of Eid al-Fitr and the Day of Eid al-Adha.”

There is no difference between voluntary fasting, obligatory fasting, or fasting due to a vow. If someone vows to fast on those days, the vow itself is invalid.

Just as fasting on the two days of Eid is prohibited, fasting on the days of Tashriq—three days after Eid al-Adha—is also prohibited. This is the sound opinion in the later view of the Shafi‘i school because the Prophet forbade fasting on those days.

It is reported in *Sahih Muslim*:

> إِنَّهَا أَيَّامُ أَكْلٍ وَشُرْبٍ وَذِكْرِ اللَّهِ  

“These are days of eating, drinking, and remembering Allah.”

## Times When Fasting Is Disliked

> وَيُكْرَهُ صَوْمُ يَوْمِ الشَّكِّ إِلَّا أَنْ يُوَافِقَ عَادَةً لَهُ أَوْ يَصِلَهُ بِمَا قَبْلَهُ  

“It is disliked to fast on the Day of Doubt unless it coincides with one’s habitual fasting or is connected with the previous days.”

Voluntary fasting on the Day of Doubt without reason is prohibited, and fasting on that day as a precaution for Ramadan is also prohibited. This is based on the statement of Ammar ibn Yasir:

> مَنْ صَامَ يَوْمَ الشَّكِّ فَقَدْ عَصَى أَبَا الْقَاسِمِ  

“Whoever fasts on the Day of Doubt has disobeyed Abu al-Qasim.”

This hadith was authenticated by At-Tirmidhi, Ibn Hibban, and Al-Hakim.

If someone fasts on the Day of Doubt, his fast is not valid according to the stronger opinion, by analogy with fasting on the day of Eid, since both are prohibited.

However, an exception is made if the day coincides with a person’s habitual fasting, such as fasting on Mondays and Thursdays, or fasting every other day.

This is based on the saying of the Prophet ﷺ:

> لَا تُقَدِّمُوا رَمَضَانَ بِصَوْمِ يَوْمٍ وَلَا يَوْمَيْنِ إِلَّا رَجُلٌ كَانَ يَصُومُ صَوْمًا فَلْيَصُمْهُ  

“Do not precede Ramadan by fasting one or two days before it, except for someone who has a habit of fasting; let him fast.”  
(Narrated by Bukhari and Muslim)

## Intercourse During the Day in Ramadan

> وَمَنْ وَطِئَ عَامِدًا فِي الْفَرْجِ فَعَلَيْهِ الْقَضَاءُ وَالْكَفَّارَةُ  

“Whoever has intercourse intentionally must make up the fast and pay the expiation.”

In Islamic law, one of the most serious acts that invalidates fasting is having intercourse intentionally during the daytime in Ramadan. This act not only invalidates the fast but also requires two obligations: making up the fast and paying an expiation.

The expiation must be carried out in a specific order:

1. Freeing a believing slave.
2. If unable, fasting two consecutive months.
3. If unable, feeding sixty poor people.

The primary evidence for this ruling is the famous hadith narrated by Al-Bukhari and Muslim in which a man came to the Prophet ﷺ and said that he was ruined. The Prophet asked what caused his ruin, and he replied that he had relations with his wife during the daytime in Ramadan.

The Prophet asked if he could free a slave. He replied that he could not. The Prophet then asked if he could fast for two consecutive months. Again, he said he could not. Then the Prophet asked if he could feed sixty poor people, and he replied that he was unable.

Later, the man was given a basket of dates, and the Prophet told him to give it in charity. The man asked whether he should give it to someone poorer than his own family, saying that there was no family poorer than his. The Prophet smiled until his molars became visible and said, “Go and feed your family with it.”

This hadith contains several important lessons, including the permissibility of helping someone pay their expiation and the permissibility of giving charity to one's family if they are eligible to receive it.

## A Person Who Dies Owing Fasts

> وَمَنْ مَاتَ وَعَلَيْهِ صَوْمٌ مِنْ رَمَضَانَ أُطْعِمَ عَنْهُ لِكُلِّ يَوْمٍ مُدٌّ  

“Whoever dies while owing fasts from Ramadan, a *mud* of food should be given for each day.”

If a person dies while owing Ramadan fasts, his situation must first be examined. If he died while still having a valid excuse, such as an illness that continued until death, then nothing is required from him. There is no obligation of making up the fast or paying compensation.

However, if he had the opportunity to make up the fasts but failed to do so, then for each missed day a *mud* of food should be given to the poor.

According to the earlier opinion in the Shafi‘i school, it is recommended that the guardian of the deceased fast on his behalf, based on authentic hadiths. Imam Al-Nawawi considered this opinion stronger due to the clarity of its evidence.

## Fasting for the Elderly

An elderly person who is unable to fast, or would suffer extreme hardship if fasting, is no longer obligated to fast. Islamic law grants them a concession.

Instead, they must pay *fidyah*, which is feeding one poor person with one *mud* of food for each day missed.

The same ruling applies to someone who is chronically ill with no hope of recovery. Such a person does not need to make up the fast but must provide *fidyah*.

## Fasting for Pregnant and Breastfeeding Women

> وَالْحَامِلُ وَالْمُرْضِعُ إِنْ خَافَتَا عَلَى أَنْفُسِهِمَا أَفْطَرَتَا وَعَلَيْهِمَا الْقَضَاءُ  

“Pregnant and breastfeeding women may break their fast if they fear harm for themselves, and they must make up the fast.”

Islamic law grants concessions for pregnant and breastfeeding women because their condition involves concern for both themselves and their children. If a woman fears harm to herself, she may break her fast and later make it up.

However, if the concern is for the child, such as fear of miscarriage or reduction in milk that may harm the baby, then she must both make up the fast and pay *fidyah*.

This ruling is based on the verse:

> وَعَلَى الَّذِينَ يُطِيقُونَهُ فِدْيَةٌ طَعَامُ مِسْكِينٍ  

“And upon those who are able to fast but with hardship is a ransom: feeding a poor person.”  
(QS. Al-Baqarah: 184)

This opinion was narrated from Ibn Umar and Ibn Abbas, and no Companion is known to have opposed it.

If a pregnant or breastfeeding woman breaks her fast due to travel or illness, then *fidyah* is not required.

And Allah knows best.

### Conclusion

The rulings related to fasting in Ramadan demonstrate the great importance of this act of worship in Islamic law. Violations such as intercourse during the daytime of Ramadan not only invalidate the fast but also require both making up the fast and expiation, emphasizing the sanctity of this sacred month.

At the same time, Islamic law demonstrates mercy and ease. A person who dies owing fasts is not burdened if they died with a valid excuse. The elderly and those with incurable illness are allowed not to fast and may replace it with *fidyah*. Likewise, pregnant and breastfeeding women are given concessions according to their circumstances.

May Allah ﷻ make our fasting accepted, cleanse our sins, and make it a cause of salvation in this world and the Hereafter. And Allah knows best.
