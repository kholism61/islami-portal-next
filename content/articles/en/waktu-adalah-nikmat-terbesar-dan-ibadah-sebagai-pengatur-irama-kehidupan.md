### Ramadan Notes: Time Is the Greatest Blessing and Worship Orders the Rhythm of Life

On the fourth night of Ramadan 1447 H, after the tarawih prayer, Al-Azhar Mosque hosted a Multaqa Al-Azhar session under the theme “The Importance of Time in Islam.” The event featured Dr. Mahmoud Al-Hawari and Prof. Hamdy Salah Al-Hadhed and was attended by worshippers and international students.

The speakers emphasized that time is among the greatest blessings, yet it is one of the blessings most often abused by human beings. Many people spend their lives in things that bring no benefit, and some even spend their years in ways that damage both their worldly life and their Hereafter. Human beings, they explained, fall into three broad categories in the way they deal with time: those who recognize its value and invest it in goodness; those who waste it in neutral things that yield no real result; and those who consume it in actions that ruin them.

Allah swears by time in the Qur'an:

> وَالْعَصْرِ ۝ إِنَّ الْإِنسَانَ لَفِي خُسْرٍ ۝ إِلَّا الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا وَعَمِلُوا الصَّالِحَاتِ وَتَوَاصَوْا بِالْحَقِّ وَتَوَاصَوْا بِالصَّبْرِ
>
> “By Time. Indeed, mankind is surely in loss, except those who believe, perform righteous deeds, exhort one another to truth, and exhort one another to patience.” (Al-'Asr: 1-3)

This oath shows that time is the primary capital of human life. Whether a person gains or loses depends largely on how his lifetime is spent.

Islamic law organizes the life of the Muslim through a remarkably disciplined structure of time. The five daily prayers each have a fixed schedule, and fasting also has a precise beginning and end. Allah says:

> وَكُلُوا وَاشْرَبُوا حَتَّىٰ يَتَبَيَّنَ لَكُمُ الْخَيْطُ الْأَبْيَضُ مِنَ الْخَيْطِ الْأَسْوَدِ مِنَ الْفَجْرِ ثُمَّ أَتِمُّوا الصِّيَامَ إِلَى اللَّيْلِ
>
> “Eat and drink until the white thread of dawn becomes distinct to you from the black thread, then complete the fast until night.” (Al-Baqarah: 187)

In this way, a Muslim's life moves within a rhythm of worship that trains discipline, awareness, and reverence for time.

The Prophet ﷺ said, “There are two blessings about which many people are deceived: health and free time.” The greatest loss occurs when a person realizes the value of time only after it has passed.

Ramadan stands as a clear example of how time is honored in Islam. It is the month in which the Qur'an was sent down, the month of Laylat al-Qadr, and the month in which the reward of righteous deeds is multiplied. Ramadan teaches that the best use of time lies in reciting the Qur'an, increasing righteous works, correcting one's intention, and preparing to meet Allah.

This reminder teaches that life is made up of moments that never return. Whoever protects his time is protecting his life. Whoever wastes his time has wasted his greatest capital.
