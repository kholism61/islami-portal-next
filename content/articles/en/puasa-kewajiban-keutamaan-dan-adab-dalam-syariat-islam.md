## Introduction

Fasting is one of the pillars of Islam and holds a very noble position in the Sharia. This act of worship is not merely about abstaining from hunger and thirst, but also serves as a means of purifying the soul, controlling one’s desires, and attaining piety. Therefore, the Qur’an and the hadith of the Prophet ﷺ give great attention to fasting, whether in terms of its obligation, its virtues, or its etiquettes.

The scholars have also explained the wisdom and secrets behind fasting, so that a Muslim does not merely practice it outwardly, but also understands its spiritual purpose. Among the beautiful explanations regarding fasting is that of Imam ‘Izzuddin ibn ‘Abd al-Salam, who elaborated in depth on the obligation, virtues, and etiquettes of fasting.

## The Obligation of Fasting

Allah ﷻ says:

> يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا كُتِبَ عَلَيْكُمُ الصِّيَامُ كَمَا كُتِبَ عَلَى الَّذِينَ مِنْ قَبْلِكُمْ لَعَلَّكُمْ تَتَّقُونَ
>
> “O you who believe, fasting has been prescribed for you as it was prescribed for those before you, so that you may attain piety.” (QS. Al-Baqarah)

This verse is the primary proof for the obligation of fasting in Ramadan. Allah begins the verse with the call, “O you who believe,” which indicates that this command is addressed to those who possess faith. Whenever Allah addresses the believers with such a call, the scholars say that it contains either a great command or an important prohibition, and therefore a believer should pay close attention to it.

Allah’s statement, “fasting has been prescribed for you,” shows that fasting is not merely a recommended act of worship or an encouragement, but rather an obligation that must be fulfilled. The word “kutiba” in the verse means prescribed or firmly ordained, just like other obligations in the Sharia.

Then Allah says, “as it was prescribed for those before you,” which shows that fasting is not a new act of worship in Islamic law. It was also prescribed for previous nations. This contains several wisdoms. Among them is that Muslims may realize that this obligation is neither strange nor excessively burdensome, since earlier communities also observed it. Moreover, this indicates that fasting is an act of worship with universal significance throughout the history of the prophetic messages.

Allah then mentions the primary purpose of fasting in His words: “so that you may attain piety.” This is the essence of fasting. Taqwa means protecting oneself from the wrath and punishment of Allah by carrying out His commands and avoiding His prohibitions. Through fasting, a person learns to refrain from things that are actually lawful, such as eating and drinking. If one is able to restrain oneself from lawful things for the sake of Allah’s command, then one should be even more capable of restraining oneself from what is unlawful.

For that reason, some scholars interpreted this verse to mean: so that you may protect yourselves from the Fire through fasting, since fasting becomes a cause for the forgiveness of sins that may drag a person into Hellfire.

The obligation of fasting is also affirmed in an authentic hadith. In the narration of al-Bukhari and Muslim, the Prophet ﷺ said:

> بُنِيَ الإِسْلَامُ عَلَى خَمْسٍ: شَهَادَةِ أَنْ لَا إِلَهَ إِلَّا اللَّهُ، وَإِقَامِ الصَّلَاةِ، وَإِيتَاءِ الزَّكَاةِ، وَحَجِّ البَيْتِ، وَصَوْمِ رَمَضَانَ
>
> “Islam is built upon five: testifying that there is no god but Allah, establishing prayer, giving zakat, pilgrimage to the House, and fasting رمضان.”

This hadith shows that fasting in Ramadan is one of the five pillars of Islam. That means it is one of the principal pillars of the religion, not merely a supplementary or secondary act of worship. Just as a building cannot stand without its pillars, likewise a person’s Islam is not complete without observing these pillars, including fasting in Ramadan.

Thus, the obligation of fasting is not merely a legal command, but also a means of spiritual education. It trains patience, cultivates piety, cleanses sins, and strengthens the relationship between the servant and his Lord. Therefore, a Muslim should observe fasting with full awareness, sincerity, and understanding of its meaning, so that one does not merely abstain from hunger and thirst, but truly attains the rank of piety which is the main goal of fasting.

## The Virtues of Fasting

Fasting has many virtues and tremendous benefits in the life of a Muslim, both in relation to Allah and in the cultivation of the soul. Among these benefits are elevating one’s rank, erasing sins, restraining desires, encouraging charity, increasing obedience, nurturing gratitude, and preventing oneself from sin and wrongdoing. All of these virtues show that fasting is not merely an outward act of worship, but rather a means of spiritual education that touches every aspect of a servant’s life.

As for the virtue of fasting in elevating one’s rank, this is explained in the saying of the Prophet ﷺ:

> إِذَا جَاءَ رَمَضَانُ فُتِحَتْ أَبْوَابُ الجَنَّةِ، وَغُلِّقَتْ أَبْوَابُ النَّارِ، وَصُفِّدَتِ الشَّيَاطِينُ
>
> “When Ramadan comes, the gates of Paradise are opened, the gates of Hell are closed, and the devils are chained.”

This hadith shows how great the status of the month of Ramadan is, as the gates of Paradise are opened as a sign of the abundance of mercy, while the gates of Hell are closed as a sign of the diminishing causes of destruction. The devils are also chained, so the opportunity for human beings to do good becomes even greater. This becomes a chance for the believers to increase their righteous deeds, so that their rank before Allah rises higher.

In a hadith qudsi, Allah says:

> كُلُّ عَمَلِ ابْنِ آدَمَ لَهُ إِلَّا الصِّيَامَ فَإِنَّهُ لِي وَأَنَا أَجْزِي بِهِ، وَالصِّيَامُ جُنَّةٌ
>
> “Every deed of the son of Adam is for him, except fasting. Indeed, it is for Me, and I Myself shall reward it. Fasting is a shield.”

The attribution of fasting to Allah in this hadith is a form of honoring and exalting this act of worship. Fasting is a deeply hidden act of worship, because it cannot be seen by people. A person may appear not to eat or drink, but the true reality of that person’s fast is known only to Allah. Therefore, fasting is further removed from ostentation and closer to sincerity. Because of this distinction, Allah declares that He Himself will reward it.

Fasting is also described as a shield, meaning a protection from the punishment of Allah and from sinful acts. A fasting person finds it easier to restrain himself from sin because he is in a state of worship and heightened spiritual awareness.

In another hadith it is mentioned:

“The odor from the mouth of a fasting person is more fragrant to Allah on the Day of Resurrection than the scent of musk. The fasting person has two joys: when he breaks his fast, he rejoices in his breaking of the fast; and when he meets his Lord, he rejoices in his fasting.”

The odor from the mouth of a fasting person is described as more fragrant than musk, not because of the smell itself, but because of the reward and the devotional value contained in it. As for the two promised joys, the first is the joy at the time of breaking the fast, because one has successfully completed the act of worship. The second is the joy upon meeting Allah, because one receives the reward for the fast.

The Prophet ﷺ also said:

> إِنَّ فِي الجَنَّةِ بَابًا يُقَالُ لَهُ الرَّيَّانُ، يَدْخُلُ مِنْهُ الصَّائِمُونَ يَوْمَ القِيَامَةِ
>
> “Indeed, in Paradise there is a gate called Ar-Rayyan. Those who fast will enter through it on the Day of Resurrection.”

This gate is specifically reserved for those who fast as a form of honor and distinction for them. This shows how lofty the status of fasting is before Allah, to the point that its practitioners are granted a special gate in Paradise.

In addition to elevating ranks and erasing sins, fasting also serves to weaken desire. The Prophet ﷺ said:

> يَا مَعْشَرَ الشَّبَابِ، مَنْ اسْتَطَاعَ مِنْكُمُ البَاءَةَ فَلْيَتَزَوَّجْ… وَمَنْ لَمْ يَسْتَطِعْ فَعَلَيْهِ بِالصَّوْمِ فَإِنَّهُ لَهُ وِجَاءٌ

“O young people, whoever among you is able to marry, let him marry… and whoever is not able, then let him fast, for fasting will be a restraint for his desire.”

This hadith shows that fasting has a profound effect in restraining the impulses of desire. Hunger and thirst can weaken one’s passions, making it easier for a person to protect himself from sin. Therefore, fasting becomes a shield for one who is not yet able to marry.

Fasting also nurtures empathy and encourages a person to give charity. When a Muslim experiences hunger, he remembers the poor who experience the same feeling every day. This condition nurtures compassion and encourages him to help them. This is illustrated in the verse of poetry:

> فَإِنَّمَا يَرْحَمُ العُشَّاقَ مَنْ عَشِقَا
>
> “Indeed, the one who can truly understand the suffering of lovers is the one who has himself loved.”

It is narrated that Prophet Yusuf or Prophet Sulayman did not eat until all those under his care had eaten. When he was asked about that, he replied, “I fear that if I become full, I will forget the one who is hungry.” This story shows how hunger can cultivate concern for others.

Fasting also teaches gratitude. When a person restrains himself from hunger and thirst, he realizes how great the blessing of food and drink is, blessings that he normally enjoys. For indeed, a blessing is not truly appreciated except when it is lost.

Moreover, fasting restrains the soul from inclining toward sin. When a person is full, the soul tends toward pleasure and transgression. But when one is hungry, the soul becomes more subdued and easier to direct toward obedience. For that reason, some of the early righteous predecessors preferred fasting over other acts of worship, because they preferred that Allah see their souls striving against desire rather than inclining toward sin when full.

Fasting also has other benefits, such as contributing to the health of the mind and body. In one hadith it is mentioned:

> صُومُوا تَصِحُّوا
>
> “Fast, and you will be healthy.”

Among the noble aspects of fasting as well is that whoever gives food to a fasting person will receive a reward equal to that of the fasting person, without diminishing the reward of the fasting person in the least. The Prophet ﷺ said:

“Whoever gives food to a fasting person to break his fast will have a reward like his, without reducing the reward of the fasting person in the slightest.”

Therefore, whoever feeds many fasting people is as though he has gained an immense reward of fasting, even like fasting for a lifetime, according to his intention.

Among its other virtues is that whoever spends the nights of Ramadan in prayer with faith and hope for reward, his previous sins will be forgiven. The Prophet ﷺ said:

> مَنْ قَامَ رَمَضَانَ إِيمَانًا وَاحْتِسَابًا غُفِرَ لَهُ مَا تَقَدَّمَ مِنْ ذَنْبِهِ
>
> “Whoever stands in prayer during Ramadan out of faith and hoping for reward will have his previous sins forgiven.”

All of these virtues show that fasting is a most noble act of worship. It elevates rank, erases sins, purifies the soul, and brings the servant closer to his Lord. Therefore, a Muslim should make use of fasting as a means of self-reform, increasing goodness, and drawing near to Allah with full sincerity.

## The Etiquettes of Fasting

The etiquettes of fasting are recommended practices that complete the act of fasting so that it is not only legally valid but also perfect in meaning and reward. Fasting that is observed without maintaining its proper etiquettes may result only in hunger and thirst without bringing about any transformation in the heart or behavior. Therefore, scholars have explained the etiquettes of fasting as elements that perfect this act of worship, as mentioned in the texts of Islamic law.

The first etiquette is guarding the tongue and all bodily limbs from sinful actions. The Prophet ﷺ said:

> مَنْ لَمْ يَدَعْ قَوْلَ الزُّورِ وَالعَمَلَ بِهِ فَلَيْسَ لِلَّهِ حَاجَةٌ فِي أَنْ يَدَعَ طَعَامَهُ وَشَرَابَهُ

> “Whoever does not abandon false speech and acting upon it, then Allah has no need for him to abandon his food and drink.”

This hadith indicates that fasting is not merely refraining from food and drink, but also refraining from sins. If a person fasts while his tongue continues to be used for lying, backbiting, insulting, or hurting others, then the reward of his fast may be diminished or even lost. In daily life, this often occurs—for example, when someone continues to argue harshly, becomes angry at work, spreads slander on social media, or speaks with foul language while fasting. All of these contradict the etiquettes of fasting.

The second etiquette is that when a fasting person is invited to eat, he should say, “Indeed, I am fasting.” This is based on the statement of the Prophet ﷺ:

“If one of you is invited to a meal while he is fasting, let him say: Indeed, I am fasting.”

This statement is not meant as a form of showing off one’s worship, but rather as a polite apology so that the host’s feelings are not hurt. In daily life, this is important because sometimes people decline invitations without explanation, which may lead to misunderstanding. By mentioning that one is fasting, social harmony is maintained and no offense is taken.

The third etiquette is making supplication when breaking the fast. Among the supplications narrated from the Prophet ﷺ is:

> ذَهَبَ الظَّمَأُ، وَابْتَلَّتِ العُرُوقُ، وَثَبَتَ الأَجْرُ إِنْ شَاءَ اللَّهُ

> “The thirst has gone, the veins are moistened, and the reward is assured, if Allah wills.”

Another supplication reported is:

“O Allah, for You I have fasted, and with Your provision I break my fast.”

It is also narrated:

“All praise is due to Allah who helped me to fast and provided me with sustenance so that I could break my fast.”

These supplications show that breaking the fast is not merely eating after hunger, but a moment of gratitude to Allah. In reality, many people rush into eating or become absorbed in conversation and forget to supplicate and express gratitude, even though the time of breaking the fast is one in which supplications are accepted.

The fourth etiquette is breaking the fast with something light, such as dates or water. The Prophet ﷺ used to break his fast before prayer with a few dates, and if none were available, he would break his fast with water. This reflects simplicity and balance in eating. In modern life, however, the time of breaking the fast often becomes an occasion for excessive eating, sometimes far beyond one’s needs. Yet the Sunnah teaches moderation so that the body is not burdened and acts of worship afterward remain easy.

The fifth and sixth etiquettes are hastening to break the fast and delaying the pre-dawn meal (suhoor). The Prophet ﷺ said:

> تَسَحَّرُوا فَإِنَّ فِي السُّحُورِ بَرَكَةً

> “Take the pre-dawn meal, for there is blessing in suhoor.”

He also said:

> لَا يَزَالُ النَّاسُ بِخَيْرٍ مَا عَجَّلُوا الفِطْرَ

> “People will remain in goodness so long as they hasten to break the fast.”

In another narration, in a hadith qudsi, Allah says:

“My most beloved servants to Me are those who are quickest to break their fast.”

The companions of the Prophet were known for hastening to break the fast and delaying the pre-dawn meal. They would postpone suhoor until close to dawn so that they would have strength to worship throughout the day.

In reality, however, these etiquettes are sometimes reversed. Some people delay breaking the fast without reason, or they skip the pre-dawn meal because they stay up too late. Yet hastening to break the fast is an act of obedience, and suhoor is a source of strength for worship throughout the day.

Delaying suhoor is recommended so that the body remains strong during the fast and does not become weak, allowing a person to continue performing acts of worship. Hastening to break the fast is also recommended because extending hunger and thirst beyond the prescribed time carries no additional virtue and may even harm the body.

Thus, the etiquettes of fasting teach balance between worship, health, social relations, and spiritual awareness. Fasting observed with these etiquettes nurtures a personality that is patient, gentle, grateful, and close to Allah. Conversely, if these etiquettes are neglected, fasting may become nothing more than a physical routine without real influence on the life of a Muslim. And Allah knows best.

### Conclusion

Fasting is a great act of worship that holds a high position in Islamic law. It is not only obligatory as one of the pillars of Islam, but it is also a means of attaining piety, erasing sins, elevating ranks, and purifying the soul from various spiritual diseases.

Through fasting, a Muslim is trained to restrain himself, control his desires, and draw closer to Allah with sincerity. The many virtues of fasting mentioned in the Qur’an and hadith demonstrate the immense reward and status it holds before Allah. Fasting serves as a shield from punishment, a gate to Paradise, and a path toward forgiveness.

It also cultivates empathy toward the poor, encourages charity, and teaches gratitude for blessings that are often forgotten when a person lives in comfort and abundance.

The etiquettes of fasting taught by the Prophet ﷺ show that the true essence of fasting is not merely abstaining from food and drink, but also guarding the tongue, behavior, and heart from all forms of sin. Hastening to break the fast, delaying suhoor, increasing supplication, and maintaining good character while fasting are all part of the perfection of this act of worship. By observing these etiquettes, fasting will have a real impact on shaping the character of a Muslim.

Therefore, a believer should observe fasting with full awareness, understanding its obligation, attaining its virtues, and maintaining its etiquettes. In this way, fasting will not merely become an annual routine, but truly a means of self-purification and a path toward the pleasure of Allah.

May Allah accept our fasting, forgive our sins, and make us among His servants who attain piety. And Allah knows best.
