## Introduction

Ramadan is not only a season for increasing personal devotion. It is also a time to reform social and economic conduct. In Islam, worship is never limited to the relationship between the servant and Allah; it also includes the believer's responsibility toward other people. For that reason, honesty, fairness, and concern for the public good are inseparable from religious life.

Economic activity receives serious attention in Islam because trade is one of the main ways by which human needs are fulfilled and society is sustained. At the same time, Islamic law insists that commercial activity must not be built on exploitation, manipulation, or harm to others.

One of the practices explicitly condemned in Islam is *ihtikar*, the hoarding of goods in order to raise prices in the market. This often occurs with essential items such as food, medicine, and daily necessities. In many cases, hoarding creates artificial scarcity and makes life more difficult for the wider public.

The prohibition of this practice is rooted not only in economic reasoning, but also in the moral and human principles taught by Islam. The objectives of the Shariah include protecting people's welfare and preventing harm and injustice. Any act that brings suffering to society for the sake of private gain contradicts these higher principles.

This issue was revisited in one of Al-Azhar's scholarly forums, where scholars and jurists explained the danger of hoarding and its damaging effects on public life. The lesson is clear: blessing in wealth is not determined only by profit, but by the manner in which wealth is earned and used.

## The Meaning of Ihtikar in Islamic Law

In the language of Islamic jurisprudence, *ihtikar* means withholding essential goods from the market in order to increase their price. The hoarder waits until supply becomes limited so that he can sell at a far higher price and secure greater profit.

Scholars explain that this behavior arises from greed and a desire to enrich oneself without regard for the harm caused to society. In reality, hoarding most severely affects those with the weakest financial ability.

Islam teaches that trade must be governed by honesty and social responsibility. A merchant is accountable not only for personal gain, but also for the effect of his commercial conduct on society. Every practice that harms the public, including hoarding, opposes the justice that Islam seeks to establish.

The Qur'an lays down the general principle of fairness in financial dealings. Allah says:

> يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا لَا تَأْكُلُوا أَمْوَالَكُمْ بَيْنَكُمْ بِالْبَاطِلِ
>
> “O you who believe, do not consume one another's wealth unjustly.” (An-Nisa: 29)

This verse is a foundational principle in Islamic commercial law: every transaction that wrongs others or is built on injustice falls under what is forbidden.

## The Social Harm of Hoarding

Hoarding does not harm only a few individuals. It damages the wider life of society. When essential goods are withheld from the market, supply becomes restricted and prices rise. As a result, people must pay more for necessities that they urgently need.

This burden falls most heavily on those with limited means. For struggling families, a sharp rise in the price of basic goods can become a serious hardship and even threaten their well-being.

Hoarding also disrupts market stability. When prices rise in an unnatural way, trust in commercial life is weakened. Over time, this can create economic instability that harms the whole community.

For this reason, Islam strongly emphasizes balance and transparency in economic activity. Trade should be healthy and open so that public needs are met without exploitation.

## The Prophetic Prohibition of Ihtikar

The prohibition of hoarding is stated clearly in the teachings of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. In a hadith narrated in Sahih Muslim, the Messenger of Allah ﷺ said:

> لَا يَحْتَكِرُ إِلَّا خَاطِئٌ
>
> “No one hoards except a sinner.”

This hadith shows that hoarding is a deeply blameworthy act in Islam. The hoarder harms society and violates the moral order that religion seeks to build.

Another hadith says:

> الْجَالِبُ مَرْزُوقٌ وَالْمُحْتَكِرُ مَلْعُونٌ
>
> “The one who brings goods to the market is blessed with provision, while the hoarder is cursed.”

This teaching delivers a strong moral message: Islam encourages open and honest trade and condemns practices that block the fair circulation of goods in society.

## The Example of Muslim Scholars and Leaders

Throughout Islamic history, scholars and leaders paid close attention to justice in the marketplace. They understood that the market is a vital part of public life and must be protected from harm and manipulation.

One famous example is 'Umar ibn al-Khattab, the second caliph, who personally inspected the market to ensure that merchants were not harming the public. He is reported to have said:

> لَا يَبِعْ فِي سُوقِنَا إِلَّا مَنْ تَفَقَّهَ فِي الدِّينِ
>
> “No one should sell in our market except one who has understanding in religion.”

This statement shows that trade cannot be separated from ethics and religious responsibility. A merchant must understand the rules of the Shariah so that business is conducted honestly and fairly.

### Conclusion

The Islamic prohibition of hoarding shows how deeply the religion cares about social welfare and economic justice. Islam does not forbid profit through trade, but it insists that profit must be earned honestly and without harming others.

Trade governed by truthfulness and social responsibility benefits both the merchant and society. Hoarding, by contrast, is driven by greed, creates hardship, and damages public stability.

Ramadan is an ideal time to reflect on these moral values in daily life, including in economic behavior. A Muslim is called not only to improve worship toward Allah, but also to improve conduct toward fellow human beings.

By upholding honesty, rejecting greed, and caring for the public interest, trade can become a means of lawful livelihood and a source of blessing. This is the Islamic vision of economics: not profit alone, but justice, benefit, and mercy for all.
