## Introduction

Ramadan is one of the greatest blessings that Allah grants to the Muslim community. Its arrival is not merely a change in the Hijri calendar. It opens a wide opportunity for every Muslim to renew the heart, strengthen the bond with Allah, and improve the quality of worship and righteous deeds. For this reason, scholars constantly remind believers that success in Ramadan is not measured only by hunger and thirst, but by how far a person uses this month to draw nearer to Allah and reform his life.

In one of the tarawih lectures delivered at Al-Azhar Mosque during Ramadan, Dr. Hasan Al-Saghir emphasized that reaching Ramadan is itself a tremendous divine favor. Not everyone is given another chance to witness this season of mercy. Therefore, gratitude for this blessing must appear in abundant prayer, fasting, recitation of the Qur'an, and every form of righteous action.

Ramadan also has a very clear spiritual purpose in Islamic law: the formation of taqwa. Allah states this purpose explicitly:

> يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا كُتِبَ عَلَيْكُمُ الصِّيَامُ كَمَا كُتِبَ عَلَى الَّذِينَ مِنْ قَبْلِكُمْ لَعَلَّكُمْ تَتَّقُونَ
>
> “O you who believe, fasting has been prescribed for you as it was prescribed for those before you so that you may become God-conscious.” (Al-Baqarah: 183)

This verse shows that fasting is not merely an individual ritual. It is a spiritual training that shapes taqwa within the believer.

## Fasting Is Not a New Religious Duty

Dr. Hasan Al-Saghir also explained that fasting was not introduced for the first time in the law of Islam. It had already been prescribed for previous communities before the nation of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. This continuity shows that fasting holds an important place in the spiritual history of humankind.

Because of this continuity, fasting belongs to the long prophetic tradition of worship. Its purpose remains the same: to educate the human being, restrain desire, deepen spiritual awareness, and strengthen nearness to Allah.

The central aim of fasting is taqwa, the inner state in which a servant remains conscious that Allah sees every action. Taqwa is the key to success in worldly life and the path to salvation in the Hereafter.

Allah also promises His support and nearness to people of taqwa:

> إِنَّ اللَّهَ مَعَ الَّذِينَ اتَّقَوْا وَالَّذِينَ هُم مُّحْسِنُونَ
>
> “Indeed, Allah is with those who are mindful of Him and those who excel in goodness.” (An-Nahl: 128)

This verse shows that taqwa is not only a moral virtue. It is also a cause for divine help and blessing.

## Ramadan as a Month of Ease

One of the beautiful dimensions of fasting is that Allah made it an act of worship filled with mercy and ease. Allah says:

> يُرِيدُ اللَّهُ بِكُمُ الْيُسْرَ وَلَا يُرِيدُ بِكُمُ الْعُسْرَ
>
> “Allah intends ease for you and does not intend hardship for you.” (Al-Baqarah: 185)

This verse shows that Islamic law is fundamentally built upon mercy and facilitation. In the context of fasting, this ease appears in the concessions that Allah grants to His servants.

For example, those who are ill or traveling are permitted to postpone the fast and make it up later. The Qur'an also describes the days of fasting as a limited number of days, reminding the believer that the obligation is not beyond human capacity when it is met with sincerity and patience.

This principle shows that Islam never seeks to burden people with excessive hardship. Rather, the law is designed so that worship can be carried out with awareness, willingness, and devotion.

## Fasting as a Road to Paradise

Fasting also occupies a distinguished place in Islam because it is among the deeds that lead to Paradise. The Messenger of Allah ﷺ taught that fasting is one of the beloved acts of worship in the sight of Allah.

The Qur'an describes the reward of the people of taqwa as a garden as wide as the heavens and the earth:

> وَجَنَّةٍ عَرْضُهَا السَّمَاوَاتُ وَالْأَرْضُ أُعِدَّتْ لِلْمُتَّقِينَ
>
> “And a Garden whose width is like the heavens and the earth, prepared for the God-conscious.” (Ali 'Imran: 133)

The taqwa cultivated through fasting not only earns reward in the Hereafter, but also bears fruit in this world. Allah promises openings and unexpected provision to those who remain mindful of Him:

> وَمَن يَتَّقِ اللَّهَ يَجْعَل لَّهُ مَخْرَجًا ۝ وَيَرْزُقْهُ مِنْ حَيْثُ لَا يَحْتَسِبُ
>
> “Whoever is mindful of Allah, He will make a way out for him and provide for him from where he does not expect.” (At-Talaq: 2-3)

This verse shows that taqwa affects every dimension of life, from spiritual well-being to everyday livelihood.

## Ramadan as a Month of Prayer and Goodness

Ramadan is not only the month of fasting. It is also a month of prayer, forgiveness, and nearness to Allah. The Qur'an says:

> وَإِذَا سَأَلَكَ عِبَادِي عَنِّي فَإِنِّي قَرِيبٌ أُجِيبُ دَعْوَةَ الدَّاعِ إِذَا دَعَانِ
>
> “When My servants ask you about Me, indeed I am near. I answer the supplication of the one who calls upon Me when he calls upon Me.” (Al-Baqarah: 186)

This verse reminds the believer that Ramadan is a special season for abundant supplication and closeness to Allah. In this month the doors of goodness are opened, and righteous deeds carry extraordinary weight.

The Prophet ﷺ also taught that every act of goodness can become a form of charity. This opens a vast field for Muslims to multiply worship, kind speech, and beneficial service to others.

## Al-Azhar's Role in Reviving Ramadan

As one of the great centers of Islamic learning in the world, Al-Azhar continues to revive the spirit of Ramadan through educational, devotional, and social programs. At Al-Azhar Mosque, the community is offered a rich spiritual experience throughout the sacred month.

The Ramadan program includes nightly tarawih prayers with mutawatir qira'at across twenty rak'ahs, tahajjud during the last ten nights, and many scholarly lessons taught by leading scholars and professors of Al-Azhar.

Another important dimension is the Qur'an recitation circles and the social programs organized by Bayt az-Zakah wa as-Sadaqat al-Misri, which provide iftar and suhur meals to international students studying at Al-Azhar.

All of these efforts are carried out under the direction of Shaykh al-Azhar, Dr. Ahmad Al-Tayyib, as part of Al-Azhar's mission to spread learning, faith, and social care.

### Conclusion

Ramadan is a priceless opportunity for every Muslim to reform the self and elevate the quality of worship. The fasting prescribed in this month is not merely abstention from food and drink. It is a spiritual discipline meant to cultivate taqwa.

Through fasting, the believer learns self-restraint, deepens the relationship with Allah, and grows in concern for others. The taqwa produced by this worship becomes a vital provision for life in this world and a road to happiness in the Hereafter.

Ramadan also reminds us that Allah's mercy is always open to anyone who sincerely seeks renewal. By filling this month with worship, moral reform, and righteous action, a Muslim can make Ramadan the beginning of a better life.

For this reason, every Muslim should receive Ramadan with gratitude and seize every opportunity within it to draw near to Allah. In that way, Ramadan becomes not only a month of ritual worship, but a season of spiritual rebirth that carries goodness for this life and the next.
