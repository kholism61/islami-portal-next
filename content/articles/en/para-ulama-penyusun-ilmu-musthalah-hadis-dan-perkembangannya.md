## Introduction

The science of **Musthalah al-Hadith** (Hadith methodology) is one of the important branches in the intellectual heritage of Islam. This discipline explains the principles used to evaluate the sayings of the Prophet ﷺ, whether in terms of narration, chains of transmission (sanad), or the reliability of the reported information. Without this science, Muslims would face great difficulty in distinguishing between hadith that can be used as authoritative evidence and those that cannot.

Since the earliest period of Islam, hadith scholars have shown great concern for preserving the Sunnah of the Prophet ﷺ. They did not merely transmit hadith, but also formulated rigorous scholarly principles to ensure their authenticity. From this effort emerged major works in the science of Hadith methodology, which continued to develop from generation to generation.

## Early Scholars in the Science of Musthalah al-Hadith

The writing of works in Hadith methodology began in the early period of Islamic scholarship. Among the first scholars to compose a work in this field was **Qadhi Abu Muhammad ar-Ramahurmuzi**, through his book *Al-Muhaddith al-Fasil*. This work played an important pioneering role, although it did not yet comprehensively cover all aspects of Hadith methodology.

After him came **Al-Hakim Abu ‘Abdillah an-Naisaburi**, one of the great hadith scholars. However, his work in this field was considered not fully organized and lacked a systematic structure.

Later, **Abu Nu‘aim al-Ashbahani** produced another work that complemented and improved earlier discussions, though it still left room for further refinement and scholarly critique.

## The Central Role of Al-Khatib Al-Baghdadi

The early development of the science of Hadith methodology reached its peak during the time of **Al-Khatib Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi**. He authored many important works, including:

- *Al-Kifayah fi ‘Ilm ar-Riwayah* (on the principles of transmission)  
- *Al-Jami‘ li Akhlaq ar-Rawi wa Adab as-Sami‘* (on the ethics of narrators and students of hadith)

There is hardly any branch of hadith sciences that Al-Khatib did not address in his writings. Scholars who came after him acknowledged that their works were greatly dependent on his contributions. As Ibn Nuqtah stated, anyone who is fair will recognize that the hadith scholars after Al-Khatib essentially “lived from” his works.

## The Completion by Ibn al-Salah

After Al-Khatib came **Imam Ibn al-Salah** (Taqiyuddin Abu ‘Amr Uthman ibn ‘Abdurrahman ash-Shahrazuri). While teaching hadith at the Ashrafiyyah School in Damascus, he compiled his famous work known as *Muqaddimah Ibn al-Salah*.

The notable features of this work include:

- Gathering discussions scattered across earlier books  
- Organizing and refining the terminology of Hadith methodology  
- Presenting essential principles in a comprehensive manner

Although its structure was not entirely systematic—because it was delivered gradually in dictation sessions—it became a primary reference for later generations. Nearly all later works on Hadith methodology are either summaries, commentaries, or expansions of Ibn al-Salah’s work.

## The Emergence of *Nukhbat al-Fikar* and Its Commentary

Because the discussions of Ibn al-Salah were extensive and sometimes difficult for students, **Ibn Hajar al-‘Asqalani** composed a concise summary titled *Nukhbat al-Fikar fi Musthalah Ahl al-Athar*. This work was designed to be easy to memorize and suitable for beginners.

At the request of his students, Ibn Hajar later wrote a commentary on this summary known as *Nuzhat an-Nazhar*, aiming to clarify and explain the concise expressions found in the original text.

## The Meaning of Khabar, Hadith, and Athar

In the terminology of hadith scholars:

- **Khabar** is often considered synonymous with **hadith**.  
- Some scholars distinguish between them, stating that **hadith** refers specifically to reports from the Prophet ﷺ, while **khabar** includes reports from others as well.  
- Another opinion states that every hadith is a khabar, but not every khabar is a hadith.

The term **khabar** is often used because it has a broader scope.

## Classification of Khabar Based on Its Transmission

Based on the way a report reaches us, **khabar** is divided into two categories:

- **Mutawatir reports**  
- **Ahad reports**

## Mutawatir Hadith

A **mutawatir hadith** is a report transmitted by such a large number of narrators that it is normally impossible for them to agree upon a lie. This number:

- Is not fixed at a specific figure  
- What matters is the impossibility of collective fabrication  
- Occurs at every level of the chain of transmission  
- Is based on information perceived through the senses (such as hearing or seeing)

Mutawatir hadith produces **certain knowledge (ilm dharuri)**—knowledge that is self-evident and does not require deep reasoning, and which cannot be rejected by sound intellect.

In everyday life, this can be compared to our knowledge of a major event witnessed by many people and reported through numerous reliable sources.

## Necessary Knowledge and Theoretical Knowledge

- **Ilm Dharuri (Necessary Knowledge):** knowledge obtained immediately without the need for deep analysis.  
- **Ilm Nazhari (Theoretical Knowledge):** knowledge that requires investigation and reasoning.

Mutawatir hadith belongs to the category of necessary knowledge, because even a layperson can accept it without advanced analytical skills.

## Mashhur, Mustafidh, and Aziz Hadith

### Mashhur Hadith

A **mashhur hadith** is one narrated by more than two narrators but does not reach the level of mutawatir. It is widely known and recognized.

### Mustafidh Hadith

Some scholars consider it synonymous with mashhur. Others state that mustafidh refers to a hadith whose number of narrators remains stable at every level of the chain.

### Aziz Hadith

An **aziz hadith** is a narration reported by at least two narrators at every level of the chain. It is called *aziz* either because it is rare or because its chains strengthen one another.

However, aziz narration is **not a requirement for a hadith to be authentic**, contrary to the opinion of some scholars such as Abu ‘Ali al-Jubba’i from the Mu‘tazilah.

## Conclusion

The science of Musthalah al-Hadith serves as a crucial safeguard for preserving the authenticity of the Sunnah of the Prophet ﷺ. Through the efforts of great scholars across generations—from ar-Ramahurmuzi to Ibn Hajar al-‘Asqalani—the Muslim community inherited a strong and rational scholarly methodology.

Understanding the classification of hadith, especially mutawatir and ahad, enables Muslims to approach the Sunnah with fairness and scholarly rigor in accepting and practicing it. In this way, the Sunnah of the Prophet ﷺ remains preserved, relevant, and applicable in everyday life with wisdom and responsibility.
