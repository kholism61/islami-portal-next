## Introduction

All praise is due to Allah, Lord of all worlds, who has bestowed countless blessings upon His servants, among them the blessings of faith, Islam, and the opportunity to witness the month of Ramadan. May peace and blessings be upon Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, his family, his companions, and those who follow them in righteousness until the Day of Judgment.

Eid al-Fitr is one of the great occasions in Islam that comes after the Muslims have completed the obligation of fasting during the month of Ramadan. This day is not merely a celebration, but carries a profound meaning as an expression of gratitude for successfully performing acts of worship and as a moment to return to one’s natural state (*fitrah*), which is purity of the soul and obedience to Allah the Exalted.

Ramadan serves as a spiritual school that educates the believers to strengthen their faith, refine their character, and increase in acts of worship. During this month, a servant is trained to exercise self-restraint, patience, and sincerity in drawing closer to Allah.

However, the true value of Ramadan is not limited to the acts of worship performed within the month itself, but lies in the ability to preserve and continue those good deeds after it has passed. Therefore, Eid al-Fitr becomes a crucial moment for self-reflection and the beginning of a renewed commitment to a better life.

This article discusses the meaning of Eid al-Fitr as the fruit of Ramadan, along with important lessons that every Muslim should uphold in matters of worship, character, and social life.

May this writing serve as a reminder and motivation for all of us to remain steadfast in obedience and to orient our lives toward the pleasure of Allah and success in the Hereafter.

## Ramadan as a School of Obedience and Moral Refinement

Indeed, the Prophet ﷺ taught this الأمة during the month of Ramadan how to truly become servants who sincerely worship Allah. Thus, Ramadan becomes a great school for learning obedience, drawing closer to the Lord of the heavens and the earth, and disciplining oneself to follow the guidance of the Prophet ﷺ in both major and minor matters.

Ramadan is not merely a temporary season of worship, but rather a process of spiritual education that shapes the character of a believer. Within it, a servant is trained to restrain desires, purify the heart, and strengthen the relationship with Allah. Whoever truly benefits from Ramadan will emerge from it closer to Allah, softer in heart, and better in character.

Allah, the Lord of all worlds, has made fasting a means of education so that we learn how to worship Him and attain piety. Allah says:

> ﴿ كُتِبَ عَلَيْكُمُ الصِّيَامُ كَمَا كُتِبَ عَلَى الَّذِينَ مِن قَبْلِكُمْ لَعَلَّكُمْ تَتَّقُونَ ﴾  
>
> “O you who believe, fasting has been prescribed upon you as it was prescribed upon those before you so that you may attain taqwa.”

Fasting teaches a person to restrain from what they love, so that they may prioritize what Allah loves. From this emerges taqwa — a constant awareness of Allah in all situations, whether in public or in private.

Ramadan also trains a servant to establish prayer and qiyam al-layl (night prayer). Allah has made night prayer an honor for the believer, for while others sleep, he stands before his Lord in sincerity and devotion.

> اعْمَلْ مَا شِئْتَ فَإِنَّكَ مَجْزِيٌّ بِهِ، وَاعْلَمْ أَنَّ شَرَفَ الْمُؤْمِنِ قِيَامُهُ بِاللَّيْلِ  
>
> “Do whatever you wish, for you will be recompensed for it. And know that the honor of a believer lies in his night prayer.”

During Ramadan, a Muslim learns how to stand before Allah with humility, to feel closeness to Him, and to direct all hopes and reliance solely to Him. This is the true essence of worship: the presence of the heart and the submission of the soul to Allah alone.

However, these lessons must not end with the conclusion of Ramadan. Ramadan is not the final destination, but the beginning of a lifelong journey toward istiqamah (steadfastness). A successful believer is one who preserves the spirit of Ramadan throughout the entire year.

Worship is not confined to Ramadan; rather, it is a continuous way of life for a believer. He lives in obedience, walks upon the guidance of Allah, and turns his entire life into an act of devotion to Him.

## Worship Does Not End After Ramadan

Worship does not end with the passing of the month of Ramadan. Rather, Ramadan marks the beginning of a believer’s journey in maintaining obedience throughout the entire year. Whoever treats Ramadan as a period of training will continue their good deeds afterward, instead of returning to heedlessness.

Fasting during Ramadan teaches a Muslim how to restrain the self from what it desires, so that they learn to prioritize the pleasure of Allah above all else. From this emerges a deeper awareness that drives a believer to continue worship even after Ramadan has ended.

The Prophet ﷺ demonstrated that fasting is not limited to Ramadan, but extends throughout the year. Among the most recommended acts is fasting six days in the month of Shawwal.

> مَنْ صَامَ رَمَضَانَ، ثُمَّ أَتْبَعَهُ سِتًّا مِنْ شَوَّالٍ، كَانَ كَصِيَامِ الدَّهْرِ  
>
> “Whoever fasts Ramadan and follows it with six days of Shawwal, it is as if he fasted for a lifetime.”

This is because every good deed is multiplied tenfold. Thus, one month of Ramadan equals ten months, and six days of Shawwal equal two months, completing the reward of a full year.

In addition, the Prophet ﷺ encouraged fasting three days of every month, a simple practice with immense reward.

> صِيَامُ ثَلَاثَةِ أَيَّامٍ مِنْ كُلِّ شَهْرٍ يُذْهِبْنَ وَحَرَ الصَّدْرِ  
>
> “Fasting three days of every month removes the ailments and bitterness of the heart.”*

This fasting is also equivalent to fasting the entire year, due to the multiplication of rewards. The Prophet ﷺ specified the best days for this practice: the 13th, 14th, and 15th of each Hijri month, known as *Ayyam al-Bidh*.

Furthermore, the Prophet ﷺ highlighted the virtue of fasting in the month of Muharram.

> أَفْضَلُ الصِّيَامِ بَعْدَ رَمَضَانَ شَهْرُ اللَّهِ الْمُحَرَّمُ  
>
> “The most virtuous fasting after Ramadan is fasting in the month of Allah, Muharram.”

He also explained that the most beloved form of fasting to Allah is the fasting of Prophet Dawud (David), peace be upon him, which consists of fasting one day and breaking the fast the next.

> أَحَبُّ الصِّيَامِ إِلَى اللَّهِ صِيَامُ دَاوُدَ، كَانَ يَصُومُ يَوْمًا وَيُفْطِرُ يَوْمًا  
>
> “The most beloved fasting to Allah is the fasting of Dawud: he would fast one day and break his fast the next.”

The Prophet ﷺ also encouraged fasting on the Day of Arafah, the Day of Ashura, and increasing fasting during the first ten days of Dhul-Hijjah. All of this demonstrates that fasting is not a seasonal act of worship, but a lifelong path of spiritual discipline for a believer.

From this, it becomes clear that Ramadan is not the end of worship, but the beginning of consistency. A believer who truly understands Ramadan will use it as a foundation to continue progressing in obedience.

He does not treat worship as something temporary, but as a constant spiritual necessity. He continues to discipline himself, increase good deeds, and maintain a strong connection with Allah at all times.

Therefore, whoever returns to heedlessness after Ramadan has not truly benefited from it. As for the one who remains steadfast, that is a sign of acceptance and success in the school of Ramadan.

## Qiyam al-Layl Throughout the Year

Qiyam al-layl (night prayer), which is revived during the nights of Ramadan, is not an act of worship limited to that month alone. Rather, it is a devotion that should be maintained throughout the entire year. Ramadan serves as a period of training, but in essence, qiyam al-layl is an honor that defines the believer.

The Prophet ﷺ emphasized that the true honor of a believer lies in his night prayer.

> وَاعْلَمْ أَنَّ شَرَفَ الْمُؤْمِنِ قِيَامُهُ بِاللَّيْلِ  
>
> “Know that the honor of a believer lies in his night prayer.”

Qiyam al-layl is the moment when a servant stands before his Lord in the quiet of the night, بعيدًا عن أعين الناس، filled with sincerity and humility. He pours out his supplications, hopes, and concerns solely to Allah, free from showing off or seeking recognition.

Allah has made the night, especially the last third of it, a time when the servant is closest to his Lord. It is a time when supplications are more readily answered, sins are forgiven, and hearts are softened.

> يَنْزِلُ رَبُّنَا تَبَارَكَ وَتَعَالَى كُلَّ لَيْلَةٍ إِلَى السَّمَاءِ الدُّنْيَا حِينَ يَبْقَى ثُلُثُ اللَّيْلِ الآخِرُ  
>
> “Our Lord descends every night to the lowest heaven during the last third of the night.”

The Prophet ﷺ never abandoned qiyam al-layl, whether at home or during travel. He performed it with such dedication that his feet would swell.

> أَفَلَا أُحِبُّ أَنْ أَكُونَ عَبْدًا شَكُورًا  
>
> “Should I not love to be a grateful servant?”

This shows that qiyam al-layl is not merely an optional act, but a manifestation of love, gratitude, and closeness to Allah.

The Prophet ﷺ also taught that even a small portion of night prayer carries great virtue.

> مَنْ قَامَ بِعَشْرِ آيَاتٍ لَمْ يُكْتَبْ مِنَ الْغَافِلِينَ  
>
> “Whoever stands in night prayer with ten verses will not be recorded among the heedless.”

Even if one cannot perform much, he is still encouraged to maintain qiyam al-layl, even in small amounts. At the very least, one should not abandon the witr prayer, which is strongly emphasized as the closing of the night prayer.

Qiyam al-layl does not need to be lengthy; what matters most is consistency. A believer should strive to maintain this act regularly, even if it is only a few units of prayer, for the most beloved deeds to Allah are those that are consistent, even if small.

Ramadan trains the soul to taste the sweetness of qiyam al-layl. Therefore, after Ramadan, a believer should not abandon it, but rather make it a permanent part of his life.

This is the sign that one has truly benefited from Ramadan: he continues to stand before Allah in the night, preserves his connection with Him, and constantly seeks closeness to Him throughout his life.

## The Importance of Consistency in Righteous Deeds

Consistency (*istiqamah*) in righteous deeds is a sign of sincere faith and proof that a servant has truly benefited from Ramadan. The value of worship is not merely in the quantity of actions performed, but in their continuity and steadfastness.

Ramadan trains a Muslim to become accustomed to obedience: maintaining prayer, increasing recitation of the Qur’an, giving charity, and restraining oneself from sin. After Ramadan passes, the true measure of success is whether one continues these deeds or returns to heedlessness.

The Prophet ﷺ emphasized that the most beloved deeds to Allah are those that are performed consistently, even if they are small.

> أَحَبُّ الْأَعْمَالِ إِلَى اللَّهِ أَدْوَمُهَا وَإِنْ قَلَّ  
>
> “The most beloved deeds to Allah are those that are consistent, even if they are small.”

Consistency does not mean performing great deeds for a short period and then stopping. Rather, it is about maintaining even small acts of worship with persistence and sincerity.

A servant who is consistent will find that righteous deeds become a natural part of his life. He no longer feels burdened by them, because his heart has grown accustomed to obedience and his soul has been trained to love it.

The Prophet ﷺ himself was the best example of consistency. His deeds were continuous and never interrupted, and if he missed an act due to a valid reason, he would make it up later. This demonstrates the importance of maintaining continuity in worship.

Consistency is also a cause for tranquility and divine support. A person who remains steadfast is not only preserved in his deeds but is also strengthened in his heart when facing the trials of life.

> إِنَّ الَّذِينَ قَالُوا رَبُّنَا اللَّهُ ثُمَّ اسْتَقَامُوا  
>
> “Indeed, those who say: ‘Our Lord is Allah’ and then remain steadfast...”

Therefore, a believer should not treat worship as something seasonal. He should not be diligent at certain times and negligent at others. Rather, he maintains his devotion in both ease and hardship, in times of comfort and difficulty.

Ramadan is the beginning of discipline, while consistency is the proof of its success. Whoever continues righteous deeds after Ramadan has truly benefited from this blessed month.

Thus, every Muslim should strive to maintain their acts of worship, even if they are small: preserving the five daily prayers, regularly reciting the Qur’an, giving charity, and engaging in remembrance of Allah.

For indeed, small deeds performed consistently are more beloved to Allah than large deeds that are abandoned.

Through consistency, a servant continues his journey toward Allah, step by step, until he attains His pleasure.

## Studying the Qur’an Does Not End with Ramadan

One of the most defining features of Ramadan is the strong connection a Muslim develops with the Qur’an. During this blessed month, believers strive to recite, complete, and reflect upon the Qur’an. However, this relationship must not end with the conclusion of Ramadan.

The Qur’an is not meant only for Ramadan; it is a lifelong guide. It is a light that illuminates the path, a direction for every step, and a source of tranquility for restless hearts.

Allah revealed the Qur’an as guidance for mankind.

> ﴿ شَهْرُ رَمَضَانَ الَّذِي أُنزِلَ فِيهِ الْقُرْآنُ هُدًى لِلنَّاسِ ﴾  
>
> “The month of Ramadan is that in which the Qur’an was revealed as guidance for mankind.”

Therefore, a believer should maintain a continuous relationship with the Qur’an throughout the year: by reciting it, understanding its meanings, reflecting upon it, and implementing its teachings in daily life.

The Prophet ﷺ also explained that the Qur’an is a means of elevation and honor.

> إِنَّ اللَّهَ يَرْفَعُ بِهَذَا الْكِتَابِ أَقْوَامًا وَيَضَعُ بِهِ آخَرِينَ  
>
> “Indeed, Allah raises some people by this Book and lowers others by it.”

Those who take the Qur’an as their guide will be elevated in rank and honored in both this world and the Hereafter. In contrast, those who turn away from it will lose direction and blessing in their lives.

Studying the Qur’an is not limited to recitation alone; it must be accompanied by understanding and application. The purpose of its revelation is to be reflected upon and acted upon, not merely recited.

A believer should have a daily portion of the Qur’an, even if small. He maintains his recitation, improves his reading, and gradually strives to understand its meanings.

Ramadan revives a Muslim’s connection with the Qur’an. After Ramadan, this connection should not only be maintained but strengthened.

One of the signs that Ramadan has been accepted is that a person becomes closer to the Qur’an afterward. He does not confine his recitation to Ramadan, but makes the Qur’an his constant companion throughout his life.

With the Qur’an, the heart finds peace, the soul gains strength, and life becomes guided. Without it, the heart becomes restless, and one’s path loses clarity.

Therefore, every Muslim should strive to maintain a strong relationship with the Qur’an until it becomes an inseparable part of his life and a light that leads him to the pleasure of Allah.

## The Meaning of Eid in Islam

Eid in Islam is not merely a moment of celebration and joy, but a prescribed act within the religion that carries deep meaning. It is an expression of gratitude from a servant to Allah for the عظيمة blessing granted after completing great acts of worship.

In Islam, Eid follows the completion of major acts of devotion. Eid al-Fitr comes after Ramadan as a sign of success in fulfilling the fast, while Eid al-Adha follows the completion of Hajj. This shows that joy in Islam is not empty or purposeless, but rather a joy that is born from obedience.

Allah the Exalted says:

> ﴿ وَلِتُكْمِلُوا الْعِدَّةَ وَلِتُكَبِّرُوا اللَّهَ عَلَىٰ مَا هَدَاكُمْ وَلَعَلَّكُمْ تَشْكُرُونَ ﴾  
>
> “And that you complete the prescribed period and glorify Allah for having guided you, and that you may be grateful.”

Eid is an expression of gratitude for the guidance and success granted by Allah in performing acts of worship. For this reason, it is accompanied by takbir, praise, and remembrance of Allah as a form of glorifying Him.

The word ‘Eid itself comes from the meaning of “return,” as it returns every year with renewed joy. However, joy in Islam is not without limits; it is a joy that is governed by the principles of the Sharia.

The Prophet ﷺ clarified that Islam has two prescribed Eids.

> إِنَّ اللَّهَ أَبْدَلَكُمْ بِهِمَا خَيْرًا مِنْهُمَا: يَوْمَ الْفِطْرِ، وَيَوْمَ الْأَضْحَى  
>
> “Indeed, Allah has replaced them with two better days: Eid al-Fitr and Eid al-Adha.”

This shows that Eid in Islam is an act of worship, not merely a cultural tradition or worldly celebration.

Eid is also a moment to manifest the symbols of Islam, strengthen brotherhood, and spread happiness within the community. It is a day when Muslims gather, forgive one another, and restore relationships that may have been strained.

Furthermore, Eid teaches balance between devotion and happiness. Islam does not forbid joy, but it directs it within the bounds that are pleasing to Allah.

A believer rejoices not merely because of worldly pleasures, but because he has been granted the opportunity to worship and draw closer to Allah. His joy is a form of worship in itself.

Eid also reminds us that true happiness in Islam is always connected to obedience to Allah. Whoever uses Eid as a means to remain close to Allah will attain genuine happiness.

On the other hand, if Eid is filled with heedlessness and distance from Allah, then its true meaning is lost.

Therefore, a Muslim should make Eid a moment to strengthen faith, improve oneself, and continue the obedience that was cultivated during Ramadan.

## The Wisdom Behind the Legislation of Eid

The legislation of Eid in Islam carries profound wisdom, encompassing spiritual, social, and moral dimensions. Eid is not merely a moment of joy and celebration, but a means of manifesting the symbols of Islam and strengthening the relationship between a servant and his Lord, as well as among fellow human beings.

Among the greatest wisdoms of Eid is the public manifestation of Allah’s symbols and the glorification of Him. For this reason, the التكبير is prescribed from sunset on the night of Eid until the performance of the Eid prayer.

Allah the Exalted says:

> ﴿ وَلِتُكْمِلُوا الْعِدَّةَ وَلِتُكَبِّرُوا اللَّهَ عَلَىٰ مَا هَدَاكُمْ وَلَعَلَّكُمْ تَشْكُرُونَ ﴾  
>
> “And that you complete the prescribed period and glorify Allah for having guided you, and that you may be grateful.”

The recitation of takbir in mosques, homes, and public spaces reflects the glorification of Allah and serves as an expression of gratitude for His blessings.

Another wisdom of Eid is to provide ease and joy for Muslims after completing acts of worship. Islam allows its followers to experience happiness, as long as it remains within the boundaries set by Allah.

The Prophet ﷺ demonstrated that Eid is a time to express permissible joy.

> إِنَّ اللَّهَ أَبْدَلَكُمْ بِهِمَا خَيْرًا مِنْهُمَا: يَوْمَ الْفِطْرِ، وَيَوْمَ الْأَضْحَى  
>
> “Indeed, Allah has replaced them with two better days: Eid al-Fitr and Eid al-Adha.”

Eid is also a means of strengthening brotherhood among Muslims. On this day, they gather, forgive one another, and remove any resentment or conflict that may exist between them.

Furthermore, the obligation of zakat al-fitr before the Eid prayer carries significant social wisdom. It ensures that the poor and needy are able to share in the joy of Eid.

> فَرَضَ رَسُولُ اللَّهِ ﷺ زَكَاةَ الْفِطْرِ طُهْرَةً لِلصَّائِمِ وَطُعْمَةً لِلْمَسَاكِينِ  
>
> “The Messenger of Allah ﷺ prescribed zakat al-fitr as a purification for the fasting person and as food for the poor.”

Thus, Eid becomes a day of happiness for all members of society, not only for those who are well-off.

Another wisdom of Eid is that it reminds people of the great gathering in the Hereafter. The assembly of Muslims in the Eid prayer reflects how humanity will be gathered before Allah on the Day of Judgment.

Eid also teaches balance in life: between worship and joy, between one’s relationship with Allah and with others. It shows that true happiness cannot be separated from obedience to Allah.

Therefore, a Muslim should understand that Eid is not the end of worship, but a continuation of the journey toward Allah. It is a moment to strengthen faith, improve oneself, and continue the righteous deeds cultivated during Ramadan.

By understanding this wisdom, a believer will use Eid as a means to draw closer to Allah, rather than as a reason to drift away from Him.

## Unity of the Ummah and Islamic Brotherhood

Among the great objectives of Islamic law is the realization of unity within the Muslim ummah and the strengthening of Islamic brotherhood. Islam does not only regulate the relationship between a servant and his Lord, but also governs the relationships among Muslims so that they are built upon love, mutual support, and strong unity.

The Prophet ﷺ described the relationship between believers as that of a single body.

> مَثَلُ الْمُؤْمِنِينَ فِي تَوَادِّهِمْ وَتَرَاحُمِهِمْ وَتَعَاطُفِهِمْ كَمَثَلِ الْجَسَدِ، إِذَا اشْتَكَىٰ مِنْهُ عُضْوٌ تَدَاعَىٰ لَهُ سَائِرُ الْجَسَدِ بِالسَّهَرِ وَالْحُمَّى  
>
> “The example of the believers in their love, mercy, and compassion is like a single body; when one part suffers, the entire body responds with sleeplessness and fever.”

This hadith shows that every Muslim bears responsibility toward his fellow believers. He should not remain indifferent to their suffering, but rather feel it and strive to help according to his ability.

The unity of the ummah is built upon a shared creed: the worship of Allah alone. This bond is stronger than ties of blood or worldly interests.

Allah the Exalted says:

> ﴿ إِنَّمَا الْمُؤْمِنُونَ إِخْوَةٌ ﴾  
>
> “Indeed, the believers are but brothers.”

Islamic brotherhood requires mutual love, sincere advice, and the protection of each other’s honor. A Muslim must not harm his brother, whether through words or actions.

The Prophet ﷺ also forbade all forms of hatred and division.

> لَا تَبَاغَضُوا وَلَا تَحَاسَدُوا وَلَا تَدَابَرُوا، وَكُونُوا عِبَادَ اللَّهِ إِخْوَانًا  
>
> “Do not hate one another, do not envy one another, and do not turn away from one another. Be servants of Allah as brothers.”

Eid is one of the greatest moments to strengthen this brotherhood. On this day, Muslims gather, forgive one another, and remove any resentment or ill feelings that may remain in their hearts.

Strong brotherhood leads to a united and compassionate society, while division weakens the ummah and opens the door to harm and فساد.

Therefore, every Muslim has a responsibility to preserve unity: by purifying intentions, avoiding unnecessary disputes, and prioritizing love and brotherhood.

Unity does not mean eliminating differences, but uniting hearts upon the truth. As long as differences remain within the bounds of the Sharia, they should not become a cause of division.

By preserving Islamic brotherhood, the ummah becomes strong, supportive, and capable of facing challenges.

Thus, every Muslim should strive to be a source of unity, not division. He should guard his speech, refine his character, and spread mercy among people.

This is among the fruits of Ramadan and the wisdom of Eid: hearts become purified, souls become softened, and relationships are restored among the believers.

## Zakat al-Fitr and Social Responsibility

Among the great legislations of Islam, especially at the end of Ramadan, is the obligation of zakat al-fitr. This act of worship is not only spiritual in nature, but also carries a strong social dimension that fosters care and solidarity among people.

Zakat al-fitr is obligatory upon every Muslim, whether male or female, adult or child. It serves as a purification for the fasting person from shortcomings and mistakes that may have occurred during Ramadan.

The Prophet ﷺ said:

> فَرَضَ رَسُولُ اللَّهِ ﷺ زَكَاةَ الْفِطْرِ طُهْرَةً لِلصَّائِمِ وَطُعْمَةً لِلْمَسَاكِينِ  
>
> “The Messenger of Allah ﷺ prescribed zakat al-fitr as a purification for the fasting person and as food for the poor.”

From this hadith, it is clear that zakat al-fitr has two main purposes: purifying the soul and supporting those in need. It completes the fasting of Ramadan and serves as a tangible expression of compassion toward others.

Through zakat al-fitr, Islam teaches that happiness should not be limited to a select group. The joy of Eid must be shared by all members of society, including the poor and the needy.

For this reason, zakat al-fitr is prescribed to be given before the Eid prayer, so that those in need can benefit from it on the day of Eid and share in its happiness.

Zakat al-fitr also trains the soul not to be attached to wealth. It reminds a person that wealth is not solely his own, but that it contains the rights of others which must be fulfilled.

Through this act, empathy and compassion grow within the community. The wealthy learn to give, while the needy feel cared for and supported by their fellow المسلمين.

Moreover, zakat al-fitr strengthens Islamic brotherhood. It reduces the gap between rich and poor and creates a sense of unity and social harmony.

Islam does not only build the vertical relationship between humans and Allah, but also establishes the horizontal relationship among people. Zakat al-fitr is one of the clearest manifestations of this balance.

A Muslim who understands the wisdom of zakat al-fitr will not see it as a burden, but as an opportunity to give, purify himself, and draw closer to Allah.

Thus, zakat al-fitr is not merely an obligation, but a means of building a compassionate, caring, and supportive society.

This is one of the beauties of Islam: acts of worship that benefit not only the individual, but also the wider community.

## Eid and Maintaining Family Ties (Silaturahmi)

Eid in Islam is not only a moment of worship and celebration, but also the best time to strengthen family ties and repair relationships among people. It is a moment to reconnect what has been broken, resolve misunderstandings, and revive love and compassion among Muslims.

Maintaining family ties (*silaturahmi*) is one of the important teachings in Islam and holds a high status. It is not merely a social act, but an act of worship that brings great reward and becomes a cause of blessing in one’s life.

The Prophet ﷺ said:

> مَنْ سَرَّهُ أَنْ يُبْسَطَ لَهُ فِي رِزْقِهِ وَيُنْسَأَ لَهُ فِي أَثَرِهِ فَلْيَصِلْ رَحِمَهُ  
>
> “Whoever would like his provision to be expanded and his lifespan to be extended, let him maintain ties of kinship.”

This hadith shows that maintaining family ties does not only affect social relationships, but also brings blessings in sustenance and life.

Eid becomes a great opportunity to practice this command. On this day, Muslims visit one another, greet each other, forgive one another, and strengthen bonds of family and brotherhood.

Maintaining ties is also a means of erasing sins and cleansing the heart from resentment and hatred. A Muslim who forgives his brother will be honored in the sight of Allah.

The Prophet ﷺ also clarified that maintaining ties is not limited to those who treat us well, but includes those who cut us off.

> لَيْسَ الْوَاصِلُ بِالْمُكَافِئِ، وَلَكِنَّ الْوَاصِلَ الَّذِي إِذَا قُطِعَتْ رَحِمُهُ وَصَلَهَا  
>
> “The one who truly maintains ties is not the one who reciprocates, but the one who reconnects when ties are broken.”

This shows that maintaining family ties is a manifestation of sincerity and noble character, not merely a reciprocal relationship.

Eid also teaches the importance of starting anew with a чистое heart. Through forgiveness, strained relationships can be restored, and brotherhood can be strengthened once again.

Maintaining ties is not limited to close family, but also includes extended relatives, neighbors, friends, and Muslims in general. By expanding these connections, a harmonious and compassionate society can be established.

During Eid, a Muslim is taught to set aside ego, humble himself, and prioritize unity. He does not wait for others to take the first step, but strives to be the first in doing good.

By maintaining family ties, a Muslim not only earns reward, but also experiences inner peace and true happiness.

This is one of the beauties of Eid in Islam: it is not merely a celebration, but a moment to unite hearts, repair relationships, and spread compassion within society.

## Unity of the Ummah and Islamic Brotherhood

Among the great objectives of Islamic law is the realization of unity within the Muslim ummah and the strengthening of Islamic brotherhood. Islam does not only regulate the relationship between a servant and his Lord, but also governs the relationships among Muslims so that they are built upon love, compassion, mutual support, and strong unity.

The Prophet ﷺ described the relationship between believers as that of a single body.

> مَثَلُ الْمُؤْمِنِينَ فِي تَوَادِّهِمْ وَتَرَاحُمِهِمْ وَتَعَاطُفِهِمْ كَمَثَلِ الْجَسَدِ، إِذَا اشْتَكَىٰ مِنْهُ عُضْوٌ تَدَاعَىٰ لَهُ سَائِرُ الْجَسَدِ بِالسَّهَرِ وَالْحُمَّى  
>
> “The example of the believers in their love, mercy, and compassion is like a single body; when one part suffers, the entire body responds with sleeplessness and fever.”

This hadith shows that every Muslim has a responsibility toward his fellow believers. He should not remain indifferent to their suffering, but rather feel it and strive to help according to his ability.

The unity of the ummah is built upon a shared creed: the oneness of Allah. This bond is stronger than ties of lineage or worldly interests.

Allah the Exalted says:

> ﴿ إِنَّمَا الْمُؤْمِنُونَ إِخْوَةٌ ﴾  
>
> “Indeed, the believers are but brothers.”

Islamic brotherhood requires mutual love, sincere advice, and the protection of each other’s honor. A Muslim must not harm his brother, whether through words or actions.

The Prophet ﷺ also forbade all forms of hatred and division.

> لَا تَبَاغَضُوا وَلَا تَحَاسَدُوا وَلَا تَدَابَرُوا، وَكُونُوا عِبَادَ اللَّهِ إِخْوَانًا  
>
> “Do not hate one another, do not envy one another, and do not turn away from one another. Be servants of Allah as brothers.”

Eid is one of the greatest moments to strengthen this brotherhood. On this day, Muslims gather, forgive one another, and remove any resentment that may remain in their hearts.

Strong brotherhood produces a strong society filled with compassion and solidarity. In contrast, division weakens the ummah and opens the door to harm and corruption.

Therefore, every Muslim has a responsibility to preserve unity: by purifying intentions, avoiding unnecessary disputes, and prioritizing love and brotherhood.

Unity does not mean eliminating differences, but uniting hearts upon the truth. As long as differences remain within the boundaries of the Sharia, they should not become a cause of division.

By preserving Islamic brotherhood, the ummah becomes strong, supportive, and capable of facing challenges.

Thus, every Muslim should strive to be a source of unity, not division. He guards his speech, refines his character, and spreads compassion among people.

This is one of the fruits of Ramadan and one of the profound meanings of Eid: purified hearts, softened souls, and restored harmony among the believers.

## Returning to the Qur’an, the Sunnah, and the Understanding of the Salaf

Among the most fundamental principles in Islam is returning to the Qur’an and the Sunnah as the primary sources for understanding the religion. These two sources are a guidance that will never lead one astray as long as they are followed correctly.

However, understanding the Qur’an and the Sunnah cannot be based solely on personal reasoning. It must be grounded in the understanding of the best generations of this ummah—the companions of the Prophet ﷺ and those who followed them with excellence.

Allah the Exalted says:

> ﴿ وَالسَّابِقُونَ الْأَوَّلُونَ مِنَ الْمُهَاجِرِينَ وَالْأَنْصَارِ وَالَّذِينَ اتَّبَعُوهُم بِإِحْسَانٍ رَّضِيَ اللَّهُ عَنْهُمْ وَرَضُوا عَنْهُ ﴾  
>
> “The foremost among the Muhajirin and the Ansar and those who follow them with excellence—Allah is pleased with them and they are pleased with Him.”

This verse shows that following the path of the companions and the early generations is the path of salvation.

The Prophet ﷺ also clarified who the saved group is among those who differ.

> مَا أَنَا عَلَيْهِ الْيَوْمَ وَأَصْحَابِي  
>
> “(They are) those who follow what I and my companions are upon today.”

This demonstrates that safety in religion lies in following the Qur’an and the Sunnah according to the understanding of the companions, not according to personal desires or misguided interpretations.

The companions were the best generation because they lived alongside the Prophet ﷺ, witnessed the revelation, and understood the objectives of the Sharia with clarity and sincerity.

The Prophet ﷺ said:

> خَيْرُ النَّاسِ قَرْنِي، ثُمَّ الَّذِينَ يَلُونَهُمْ، ثُمَّ الَّذِينَ يَلُونَهُمْ  
>
> “The best of people are my generation, then those who follow them, then those who follow them.”

Returning to the Qur’an and the Sunnah upon the understanding of the salaf is the key to the unity of the ummah and protection from division. Most deviations in religion begin when people abandon the correct understanding.

A Muslim must be careful in acquiring religious knowledge. It is not enough to merely read or listen; one must ensure that his understanding aligns with what the Prophet ﷺ taught and what the companions understood.

By adhering to this methodology, the religion remains pure, the heart remains upright, and the ummah remains united upon the truth.

On the other hand, when people follow their desires and abandon true guidance, division, اختلاف, and deviation inevitably arise.

Therefore, returning to the Qur’an and the Sunnah is not merely a slogan—it must be reflected in one’s life: in creed, worship, character, and all aspects of life.

This is the straight path—the path followed by the Prophet ﷺ and his companions—the path that leads to success in this world and the Hereafter.

## Live for the Religion and the Hereafter

A true Muslim does not live merely for this world, but makes the religion and the Hereafter the primary purpose of his life. This world is only a temporary stop, while the Hereafter is the eternal destination.

Many people are deceived by the glitter of worldly life, making it their ultimate goal. Yet this world is fleeting and will pass away, while the Hereafter is everlasting.

Allah the Exalted says:

> ﴿ وَمَا الْحَيَاةُ الدُّنْيَا إِلَّا مَتَاعُ الْغُرُورِ ﴾  
>
> “And the life of this world is nothing but a deceiving enjoyment.”

A Muslim should realize that true honor is not found in wealth, status, or position, but in closeness to Allah and in righteous deeds.

Those who live only for themselves may experience temporary comfort, but their lives lack true meaning. In contrast, those who live for their religion, for their Hereafter, and to benefit others live with purpose, dignity, and clarity.

The Prophet ﷺ is the best example in this regard. He had the opportunity to live in luxury, yet he chose a simple life in order to convey the message and uphold the religion of Allah.

A Muslim should make his life a means of worship, da‘wah, and benefit for others. Every action should be directed toward seeking the pleasure of Allah, not merely worldly gain.

He works, studies, and strives not only for this world, but as a path toward the Hereafter. He restrains himself from what is forbidden, remains patient in obedience, and stays consistent in doing good.

Living for the religion means making the Qur’an and the Sunnah the guide in every step. Living for the Hereafter means constantly remembering that every action will be accounted for before Allah.

A Muslim must not exchange what is eternal for what is temporary. He must not sell his Hereafter for fleeting worldly gain.

As scholars have said: if this world were made of gold that will perish, and the Hereafter were made of clay that will remain, the Hereafter would still be superior. So how much more when this world is insignificant and perishing, while the Hereafter is everlasting and perfect?

Therefore, a Muslim must correct his intentions, improve his deeds, and direct his life toward the right purpose.

Live for the religion, and Allah will honor you. Live for the Hereafter, and the world will come to you humbled.

Do not be deceived by this temporary world, and do not be heedless of the eternal Hereafter.

For indeed, the true life is the life of the Hereafter, and true success is when a servant meets his Lord while He is pleased with him.

### Final Advice for the Muslim Ummah

O Muslims, fear Allah as He truly deserves to be feared. Let every action we perform be sincere for His sake and in accordance with the guidance of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ.

Guard your hearts from the diseases that corrupt them: arrogance, envy, hatred, and excessive attachment to this world. Purify your hearts through remembrance of Allah, sincere repentance, and constant return to Him in all circumstances.

Strive to improve your relationship with Allah, as well as your relationships with others. Do not be a cause of division, but be among those who bring unity to the ummah.

Increase in good deeds, even if they are small, as long as they are consistent. For the most beloved deeds to Allah are those done regularly, even if they are few.

Do not let your devotion end with Ramadan. Let Ramadan be the beginning of transformation, not a temporary phase.

Recite the Qur’an frequently, reflect upon its meanings, and apply it in your daily life. The Qur’an is a guidance that elevates those who hold firmly to it.

Do not neglect prayer, for it is the pillar of the religion. Preserve your prayers, for they are your direct connection with your Lord.

Strengthen the bonds of brotherhood among yourselves. Advise one another in truth and support each other in times of difficulty.

Remember that this world is temporary. Do not be deceived by its pleasures and forget your ultimate destination—the Hereafter.

Strive to be among those who benefit others, for the best of people are those who are most beneficial to others.

Do not belittle any good deed, no matter how small, for every action is recorded before Allah.

And do not underestimate any sin, no matter how minor, for small sins that are repeated may become great in the sight of Allah.

Return to Allah before a time comes when there is no return. Repent sincerely before the door of repentance is closed.

May Allah make us among His sincere servants, grant us steadfastness, and accept all our deeds.

May Allah rectify our condition, unite our hearts, and include us among those who receive His mercy and pleasure.
