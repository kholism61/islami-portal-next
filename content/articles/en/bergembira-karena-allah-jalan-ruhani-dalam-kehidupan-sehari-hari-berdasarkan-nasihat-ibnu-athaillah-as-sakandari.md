## Introduction

The letters and counsels of Ibn ‘Ata’illah al-Sakandari to his companions contain spiritual guidance that emphasizes the relationship between a servant’s heart and his Lord. He does not begin his advice with discussions of outward legal rulings, but rather with supplication and hope that Allah may make human happiness found only with Him, not with the world and its pleasures. In his prayer, he asks that people be granted joy only through Allah, contentment with whatever comes from Him, and inclusion among those who understand Allah’s intent and will in their lives.

Those who understand Allah are described as servants who realize that the purpose of their lives is to return to Him and serve Him. They know that Allah is always with them, observing their movement and stillness, and managing all the affairs of life. This awareness prevents them from turning to created beings in seeking benefit or avoiding harm, because they understand that all things were originally nothing, and that true power belongs only to Allah.

On the other hand, there are also people who are heedless because they are preoccupied with the universe and material things. They do not understand what Allah intends for them, and therefore they are not earnest in obedience. Even if they worship, that worship appears only outwardly and does not penetrate the heart. Their obedience is formal, not born from deep spiritual awareness.

This counsel also explains that a servant’s happiness should not depend on worldly blessings, but rather on nearness to Allah. Those who are occupied with the remembrance of Allah experience a delight that cannot be matched by any worldly pleasure. Therefore, Ibn ‘Ata’illah prays that Allah may make human happiness found only in Him, not in anything besides Him, and may distance them from heedlessness caused by attachment to the world.

Thus, this counsel serves as an introduction to the discussion of the states of human beings in responding to blessings, the position of prayer as a source of spiritual happiness, and the levels of the heart in knowing Allah. All of this revolves around one principal truth: that true happiness is found only in a living relationship between the servant and his Lord. *And Allah knows best.*

## Those Who Understand Allah

Those who understand Allah, as described in the counsel of Ibn ‘Ata’illah, are those who realize their purpose in life as servants. They understand that human existence in this world is not merely to seek pleasure, status, or wealth, but to journey toward Allah and devote themselves to Him. This awareness gives their lives clear direction. In modern reality, this can be seen in people who do not make the world their final goal, but rather treat it as a means to draw nearer to Allah through deeds, character, and service.

A person who understands Allah does not attach his heart to created beings. He still works, strives, and interacts with people, but his heart does not depend on them. In daily life, this appears in a calm attitude when facing financial hardship, failure, or social pressure. He does not easily fall into despair because he is certain that all matters are under Allah’s governance. Likewise, when he receives success or enjoyment, he does not become arrogant or deceived by the world, because he realizes that all of it is only a trust that will one day return to its Owner.

The awareness that Allah constantly watches human movement and stillness also has major implications for social conduct. A person who truly understands Allah will preserve honesty, trustworthiness, and responsibility even when no human being is watching. He does not cheat in trade, deceive in work, or betray a trust, because he feels himself to be always before Allah. In society, such a person becomes a source of trust, because his conduct is determined not by human surveillance, but by awareness of Allah’s watchfulness.

Moreover, a person who understands Allah does not make the world his primary source of happiness. He may possess wealth, position, or status, but his happiness does not depend upon them. In present reality, many people experience anxiety even while living in luxury, because their hearts depend upon things that are fleeting. In contrast, the one who knows Allah may feel tranquility even in a simple life, because his happiness comes from nearness to Allah, remembrance, and obedience.

Such an attitude also produces inner resilience. When facing trials, he sees them as part of Allah’s will that contains wisdom. He is not easily angered, envious, or resentful, because he sees all things as existing within divine decree. In social life, such a person tends to bring calmness, is not easily provoked, and is able to become a source of peace amid conflict.

Thus, those who understand Allah are not figures who are cut off from the world, but rather the most balanced people in living within it. They work, strive, and interact with society, yet their hearts remain connected to Allah. In modern reality, filled with pressure, materialism, and competition, the presence of such people is extremely important, because they are the ones who preserve honesty, tranquility, and the true meaning of life. *And Allah knows best.*

## Three Categories of People in Responding to Blessings

In the counsel of Ibn ‘Ata’illah al-Sakandari, it is explained that people are divided into three categories in how they respond to Allah’s blessings. This division is not merely a spiritual classification, but also a real depiction of the human condition in everyday life. The way a person views blessings determines the direction of his life, the quality of his faith, and his nearness to Allah.

The first category consists of those who rejoice in blessings purely because of the pleasure they contain, without considering the One who gave them. They enjoy wealth, status, health, or worldly pleasures only because of the delight they feel. Their hearts are attached to the outward form of the blessing, not to its Giver. In modern reality, this category appears in people who make material things the measure of happiness. They are happy because of wealth, popularity, or physical enjoyment, but they possess no spiritual awareness. When such blessings disappear, their lives collapse, because they have no support other than the world. Such a condition often gives rise to anxiety, arrogance, or even despair, because their hearts are empty of a relationship with Allah.

The second category consists of those who rejoice in blessings because they see them as gifts from Allah. They still experience worldly enjoyment, but they realize that all of it comes from Allah and is a sign of His mercy. In everyday life, this category appears in those who give thanks when they receive provision, health, or ease. They use those blessings for good, help others, and strengthen their worship. Yet their hearts still look toward the blessing itself. They are still happy because the blessing exists, even while acknowledging its Source. This category is better than the first, because they possess spiritual awareness, but their nearness to Allah has not yet reached perfection.

The third category consists of those who rejoice only in Allah, not in His blessings. They are not influenced by the outward or inward sweetness of the blessing, because their hearts are occupied with Allah alone. In real life, this category appears in those who remain calm in times of ease as well as hardship. When they receive blessings, they do not become absorbed in worldly pleasure, because their hearts remain directed toward Allah. When they lose something, they are not shattered, because their happiness does not depend on the world. They make every condition a path toward nearness to Allah. Such people possess a deep serenity, because their happiness is not attached to anything transient.

This division shows that the difference among people does not lie in the amount of blessings they receive, but in how they view them. Two people may receive the same blessing, yet one becomes heedless while the other becomes nearer to Allah. In modern life, which is full of material competition, this division is highly relevant. Many people are trapped in the first category, pursuing enjoyment without spiritual awareness, so that their lives become filled with anxiety.

Therefore, this counsel of Ibn ‘Ata’illah calls people to elevate the quality of their perspective regarding blessings: from merely enjoying blessings, to recognizing that blessings come from Allah, until finally reaching the state in which happiness lies only in nearness to Him. This is the summit of a servant’s happiness, when his heart no longer depends on the world, but only on Allah alone. *And Allah knows best.*

## Happiness in Prayer

In the counsel of Ibn ‘Ata’illah al-Sakandari, it is explained that prayer is among the greatest gifts Allah has given to His servants. Prayer is not merely a ritual obligation, but a means of meeting between the servant and his Lord. In prayer, a servant stands before Allah, declaring his humility, need, and dependence upon Him. Therefore, prayer becomes the noblest place for a servant to experience nearness and true happiness.

The Messenger of Allah ﷺ himself affirmed the position of prayer as a source of happiness by saying that “the coolness of my eyes has been placed in prayer.” This expression shows that the peak of his tranquility and inward delight was found in prayer, not in worldly enjoyments. This is because in prayer he witnessed the greatness of Allah, and so his heart was no longer attached to anything besides Him. The happiness that arises from prayer is not outward happiness, but spiritual happiness born of nearness to Allah.

Ibn ‘Ata’illah explains that the coolness of the eye, or happiness in prayer, is directly proportional to the degree of a person’s ma‘rifah of Allah. The deeper a person’s knowledge of his Lord, the greater the delight he experiences in prayer. Since the Messenger’s ma‘rifah of Allah cannot be compared to that of any other human being, his happiness in prayer likewise cannot be matched by anyone else’s. Nevertheless, the door to that happiness remains open to anyone who strives to bring his heart حاضرًا in prayer.

In modern life, many people perform prayer only as a formal obligation. Prayer is carried out merely to discharge duty, without the presence of the heart and without the awareness that one is standing before Allah. As a result, prayer no longer becomes a source of tranquility, but merely a routine. Yet if prayer is performed with awareness, humility, and inward presence, it can become an immense source of spiritual strength. Prayer can remove anxiety, calm the heart, and provide a clear direction in life.

A person who discovers happiness in prayer no longer seeks peace from worldly things alone. He makes prayer the place to which he returns when his heart is restless, when life feels heavy, or when the world feels narrow. In his prostration, he finds a tranquility that cannot be given by wealth, status, or any other worldly pleasure. He realizes that true happiness lies not in the world, but in nearness to Allah.

Thus, happiness in prayer is not something impossible, but something that can be attained by every servant who strives to bring his heart before Allah. The deeper a person’s ma‘rifah, the greater the delight he experiences in prayer. In the end, prayer is no longer seen as a burden, but as a need of the soul and the highest source of happiness in life. *And Allah knows best.*

## The Danger of Depending on Created Beings

In the counsels of Ibn ‘Ata’illah al-Sakandari, it is explained that one of the subtlest and most dangerous diseases of the heart is dependence upon created beings. People often make other human beings, wealth, position, or worldly means into objects of reliance. Yet all of these are only intermediaries that possess no power whatsoever except by Allah’s permission. The danger of such dependence does not merely damage creed, but also gives rise to anxiety, fear, and disappointment in everyday life.

Ibn ‘Ata’illah emphasizes that what is dangerous is not the existence of worldly means themselves, but rather a heart that depends upon those means and forgets the Lord. In life, human beings indeed interact with created means: one works under an employer, seeks treatment from a doctor, trades with customers, or asks others for help. All of this is part of Allah’s established order in life. However, the real problem arises when the heart no longer sees Allah behind those means and instead regards created beings as the true determiners of benefit and harm.

Dependence on created beings makes the heart constantly unstable. If people give, one feels happy. If they refuse, one feels sad. If they praise, one feels proud. If they blame, one feels destroyed. One’s inner life becomes fully controlled by the attitudes of other people. This is a sign that the heart has become dependent upon creation rather than upon Allah. Yet created beings themselves are weak, changeable, and possess no absolute power over anything.

In contrast, the one who knows Allah regards created beings only as intermediaries, not as the object of reliance. He still works, strives, and interacts with people, but his heart is not attached to them. If people give, he sees it as a gift from Allah. If people refuse, he does not collapse, because he knows that even that refusal occurs by Allah’s will. Thus, his heart remains tranquil in every state.

In modern life, the danger of depending upon created beings has become even more apparent. Many people tie their sense of worth to position, wealth, or human approval. They feel valuable only when praised, and worthless when ignored. They fear losing status, fear not being liked, or fear losing wealth. All these fears arise because their hearts lean not upon Allah, but upon created beings.

As a result, life becomes full of pressure and anxiety. A person may lose peace merely because he loses a job, ends a relationship, or fails in business. He feels as though the world has ended, because he has no place of reliance besides worldly causes. Yet if his heart depended on Allah, he would remain calm, because he would know that all causes are only tools, while the One who determines all things is Allah.

Ibn ‘Ata’illah teaches that true happiness can be attained only when the heart depends entirely upon Allah. Created beings may be used as means, but they must never become the foundation of reliance. Once the heart depends upon Allah, a person no longer fears losing created beings, because he knows that the source of all things remains in Allah’s hand.

Thus, the danger of depending on created beings is not merely a matter of creed, but also a matter of inner peace. A heart that depends on created beings will always remain restless, whereas a heart that depends on Allah will always remain calm. Therefore, a servant must continuously train himself to see all things as coming from Allah, and to make Him alone the sole object of reliance in life. *And Allah knows best.*

## Conclusion

From the entire discussion of Ibn ‘Ata’illah al-Sakandari’s counsel concerning knowledge of Allah, the joy of the servants, the human response to blessings, happiness in prayer, and the danger of depending on created beings, it can be understood that the essence of spiritual life in Islam is the purification of the heart so that it depends upon Allah alone. All deeds, states, and blessings of life are in reality only means to draw near to Him, not independent ends in themselves.

Those who understand Allah no longer make the world the center of their happiness. They do not measure success by wealth, position, or human praise, but by nearness to Allah. Their hearts are tranquil because they realize that everything that happens is under His governance. They are not preoccupied with chasing created beings, because they have discovered the source of all good.

The division of people in responding to blessings shows that the quality of a person’s heart determines the direction of his life. Some drown in blessings until they forget the Giver of blessings. Others show gratitude and see blessings as gifts from Allah. Yet the highest rank belongs to those who rejoice only in Allah, not in His gifts. They are not attached to the outward form of blessings, because their hearts have been filled with His presence.

Happiness in prayer also teaches that the peak of a servant’s delight lies not in the world, but in the moment when he stands before his Lord. Prayer becomes the meeting place between the servant and Allah, the place where the heart feels tranquility, humility, and nearness. There the servant experiences a happiness that cannot be equaled by any worldly enjoyment.

In contrast, dependence on created beings is the source of unending anxiety. A heart that relies upon created beings will always be filled with fear and disappointment, because created beings themselves are weak and possess no real power. When that support disappears, life feels as though it has collapsed. But the heart that relies upon Allah remains firm, because it leans upon the One who never changes.

In modern life, filled with competition, pressure, and dependence on material things, these teachings become increasingly relevant. People often seek happiness outside themselves, chasing recognition, wealth, and worldly pleasures, yet they still feel empty. This happens because their hearts are not connected to Allah. Without a strong spiritual relationship, all worldly achievements are unable to provide true peace.

Therefore, the path of safety for a servant is to return his heart to Allah. He must train himself to see all things as coming from Him, to give thanks for His blessings, to worship with full awareness, and to release dependence upon created beings. In this way, his life will be filled with tranquility, and every circumstance will become a path to draw near to Allah.

In the end, true happiness does not lie in the abundance of blessings, but in nearness to the Giver of blessings. It does not lie in the abundance of outward deeds, but in the life of the heart with Allah. Whoever finds Allah loses nothing. And whoever loses Allah possesses nothing, even if the whole world is in his hands. *And Allah knows best.*
