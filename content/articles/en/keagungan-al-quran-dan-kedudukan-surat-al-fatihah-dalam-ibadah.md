## Introduction

The Qur’an is the sacred book that serves as the primary source of Islamic teachings, a guide for human life, and a path to happiness in this world and the Hereafter. Since it was revealed to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, the Qur’an has not only been recited as an act of worship, but has also been studied, memorized, interpreted, and implemented by Muslims throughout the ages. From the generation of the Companions onward, scholars gave tremendous attention to the Qur’an in every aspect: the number of its verses, the arrangement of its chapters, the meanings of its words, and the rulings contained within it.

They examined every part of the Qur’an with great precision: how many verses it contains, how many words and letters, which portions were revealed in Makkah and which in Madinah, and what is meant by terms such as *surah* and *ayah*. This demonstrates the immense reverence they had for this noble Book. No other book in human history has been studied with such detail as the Qur’an.

Among all the surahs of the Qur’an, there is one that holds a uniquely special position: Surah al-Fatihah. It is the opening of the Qur’an, the summary of Islamic teachings, and an essential pillar in prayer. Every Muslim recites it repeatedly every day, at least seventeen times in the obligatory prayers. For this reason, scholars devoted special attention to it in terms of its names, meanings, virtues, and the rulings connected to it.

What follows is a collection of scholarly explanations concerning the Qur’an in general, Surah al-Fatihah in particular, and the rulings related to it, including the discussion of seeking refuge in Allah (*isti‘adhah*).

## The Madinan Surahs in the Qur’an

Abu Bakr ibn al-Anbari narrated from Qatadah that the surahs revealed in Madinah are: Al-Baqarah, Al ‘Imran, An-Nisa’, Al-Ma’idah, Bara’ah, Ar-Ra‘d, An-Nahl, Al-Hajj, An-Nur, Al-Ahzab, Muhammad, Al-Hujurat, Ar-Rahman, Al-Hadid, Al-Mujadilah, Al-Hashr, Al-Mumtahanah, As-Saff, Al-Jumu‘ah, Al-Munafiqun, At-Taghabun, At-Talaq, as well as the verses from “Yā ayyuhan-nabiyyu lima tuharrimu” up to the tenth verse, then Az-Zalzalah and An-Nasr. All of these were revealed in Madinah, while the rest were revealed in Makkah.

This shows an important classification in the sciences of the Qur’an, namely the distinction between Makkan and Madinan revelation. This division is not based only on the place of revelation, but also on its time: whatever was revealed before the Hijrah is Makkan, and whatever was revealed after the Hijrah is Madinan, even if it was not revealed in the city of Madinah itself.

Scholars paid great attention to this classification because the content of Makkan revelation generally differs from that of Madinan revelation, as do its style and objectives. Makkan surahs usually focus on tawhid, belief in the Last Day, stories of the prophets, and strengthening creed, because Muslims in Makkah were in the phase of building faith and confronting polytheism. Madinan surahs, on the other hand, often contain legal rulings, social organization, family law, jihad, transactions, and discussion of hypocrites and the People of the Book, because in Madinah the Muslims had become a community and a state in need of detailed legislation.

Accordingly, what was narrated from Qatadah through Abu Bakr ibn al-Anbari is not merely a list of surah names; it is also an important foundation for understanding the context of revelation, the objectives of the Sharia, and the methods of Islamic da‘wah in its different stages. This helps the student of knowledge understand the Qur’an more deeply in terms of meaning, legal rulings, and wisdom.

## The Number of Verses, Words, and Letters in the Qur’an

It has been stated that the Qur’an contains approximately six thousand verses. Scholars then differed regarding what exceeds that number. Some said it does not exceed six thousand, while others said it does. One of the most well-known opinions is what Abu ‘Amr ad-Dani mentioned in his book *Al-Bayan*, where he stated that the Qur’an contains **6,236 verses**. This difference does not mean any disagreement in the text of the Qur’an, nor any omission or addition. Rather, it goes back to differences in methods of counting, such as whether the basmalah is counted as an independent verse, and differences among regional schools and reciters regarding where verses are divided.

As for the number of words in the Qur’an, Al-Fadl ibn Shadhan narrated from ‘Ata’ ibn Yasar that it contains **77,439 words**. As for the number of letters, it is narrated that Al-Hajjaj ibn Yusuf gathered reciters, memorizers, and scribes of the Qur’an and asked them to count all its letters. After careful counting, they concluded that the Qur’an contains **340,740 letters**. This reveals the extent of the early Muslims’ care in preserving the Qur’an, down to the level of individual letters.

After learning this number, Al-Hajjaj asked about the midpoint of the Qur’an. After calculation, scholars stated that its midpoint falls at the letter **fa’** in the word **walyatalattaf** in Surah Al-Kahf. This determination also belongs to the field of attention to the structure and arrangement of the Qur’an, not for a specific devotional purpose, but for study, preservation, and documentation.

The differences among scholars concerning the number of verses, words, and letters do not indicate any difference in the substance of the Qur’an. Rather, they indicate differences in methods of counting and enumeration. In fact, this itself is evidence of the scholars’ intense care for the Qur’an, since they were not content merely to preserve its meanings and recitation, but also counted its verses, words, and letters. All this points to the greatness of the Qur’an and to the fact that it is a preserved Book, as Allah says that He sent it down and that He Himself undertook its preservation.

## The Meaning of the Words “Surah” and “Ayah”

Scholars differed regarding the meaning of the word **surah** and its derivation. Some held that it comes from the meaning of elevation and loftiness, because every surah of the Qur’an possesses a high rank, and because it raises the one who reads it and acts upon it. An-Nabighah adh-Dhubyani said:

> أَلَمْ تَرَ أَنَّ اللَّهَ أَعْطَاكَ سُوْرَةً تَرَى كُلَّ مَلِكٍ دُونَهَا يَتَذَبْذَبُ

“Do you not see that Allah has granted you a lofty rank before which every king trembles?”

This indicates that the word *surah* can be used to mean a lofty station. On this basis, the surahs of the Qur’an may be viewed as levels of honor through which the reader moves from one rank to another.

Some scholars also said that it is called *surah* due to its high status and exalted position, just as the wall of a city is called a *sur* because it surrounds and protects what is within it. Likewise, a surah encompasses the verses contained in it and gathers them into a coherent whole.

Others said that the term *surah* comes from the meaning of a portion or section of something, meaning that each surah is a part of the total Qur’an. Some connected it to the sense of completeness, because Arabs may describe a complete she-camel with the word *surah*. According to this, each surah is a complete and perfect part within the arrangement of revelation.

Some also said that a surah is that which gathers and contains its verses, just as the wall of a city encompasses the houses and dwellings within it. Thus, the surah is a unified unit of meaning that contains the verses it includes.

The plural of *surah* comes in several forms, including:

- **suwar**
- **surat**
- **shurat**

As for the word **ayah**, linguistically it means a sign or indication. In the Qur’an, an ayah is a distinct section separated from what precedes and follows it by a known boundary. Allah says:

> وَإِنَّ فِي ذَٰلِكَ لَآيَةً

“Indeed in that is surely a sign.” (Al-Baqarah: 248)

Here, the word *ayah* means a sign indicating the power of Allah. This shows that the verses of the Qur’an are not merely arranged words, but mighty signs from the signs of Allah, carrying guidance and lessons.

One of the Arab poets said:

> تَوَهَّمْتُ آيَاتٍ لَهَا فَعَرَفْتُهَا لِسِتَّةِ أَعْوَامٍ وَذَا الْعَامُ سَابِعُ

“I imagined its signs and then came to know them after six years, and now this is the seventh year.”

Here the word *ayat* is used in the sense of signs and marks.

Some scholars said that an ayah is called so because it is a group of letters gathered into one structure, just as Arabs say that a people came out with their *ayah*, meaning with their group.

A poet said:

> بِآيَتِنَا تُزْجِي اللِّقَاحَ الْمُطَافِلا خَرَجْنَا مِنَ النَّقْبَيْنِ لَا حَيُّ مِثْلُنَا

In this line, the word *ayah* is used in the sense of a group.

Others said that an ayah is called such because it is a miracle that humans are incapable of producing the like of. Thus, it is a sign of miraculousness in its wording, meaning, and arrangement.

Sibawayh stated that the أصل word is **ayah**, and that its form underwent the known phonetic changes in Arabic. Its plurals include:

- **āy**
- **āyāt**

As for a **word** (*kalimah*) in the Qur’an, it means a single utterance. It may consist of two letters, such as:

- **mā**
- **lā**

Or it may consist of more than that, even up to ten letters, such as:

- **فَأَسْقَيْنَاكُمُوهُ**

Sometimes a single word can itself constitute a whole verse. Abu ‘Amr ad-Dani said: I know of no single word in the Qur’an that is itself a complete verse except the saying of Allah:

> مُدْهَامَّتَانِ

in Surah Ar-Rahman.

Al-Qurtubi also mentioned that scholars agreed that the Qur’an contains no foreign constructions in the sense of being outside Arabic, since it is an Arabic revelation. However, they agreed that it contains some proper names whose origins are non-Arabic, such as the name Ibrahim.

By understanding the meanings of the words *surah* and *ayah*, the Muslim realizes the beauty of the Qur’an’s structure and order. Every surah is an independent and noble unit, and every ayah is a sign from the signs of Allah’s power and guidance. Thus, reading the Qur’an is not merely reciting words, but beholding the signs of Allah in the form of perfect revelation.

## The Status and Virtue of Surah Al-Fatihah

Allah says:

> بِسْمِ اللَّهِ الرَّحْمَٰنِ الرَّحِيمِ

“In the name of Allah, the Most Merciful, the Especially Merciful.”

**Surah Al-Fatihah** is named so because it is the opening of the Qur’an in writing, and because recitation in prayer is opened with it. It holds an extremely lofty position in the Qur’an and in worship, for no prayer is valid without it. Because of this, scholars from the time of the Companions onward agreed upon its virtue and immense status. Anas ibn Malik mentioned that Al-Fatihah is also called **Umm al-Kitab** according to the majority of scholars.

In the authentic hadith narrated by At-Tirmidhi from Abu Hurairah رضي الله عنه, the Messenger of Allah ﷺ said that:

> الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ رَبِّ الْعَالَمِينَ

is **Umm al-Qur’an**, **Umm al-Kitab**, **As-Sab‘ al-Mathani** (the seven oft-repeated verses), and **Al-Qur’an al-‘Azim**.

It is also called by other names, including:

- **Al-Hamd**, because it begins with praise of Allah.
- **As-Salah**, because it is a pillar of prayer.
- **Ash-Shifa’**, because of the hadith stating that it is a healing.
- **Ar-Ruqyah**, because it is recited for healing from stings and other ailments.

Ibn ‘Abbas, Qatadah, and Abu al-‘Aliyah held that Al-Fatihah is Makkan. Abu Hurairah, Mujahid, ‘Ata’ ibn Yasar, and Az-Zuhri held that it is Madinan. Some scholars said that it was revealed twice: once in Makkah and once in Madinah. But the first view is stronger, because it accords with the saying of Allah:

> وَلَقَدْ آتَيْنَاكَ سَبْعًا مِنَ الْمَثَانِي

“And We have certainly given you seven oft-repeated verses.” (Al-Hijr: 87)

Al-Fatihah is agreed to consist of seven verses, although scholars differed as to whether the basmalah is counted as one of them. Some Kufan reciters and a number of Companions and Followers counted it as a verse of Al-Fatihah, while the reciters and jurists of Madinah did not count it as one of its verses.

It is also mentioned that Al-Fatihah consists of **25 words** and **113 letters**. Al-Bukhari said it was called **Umm al-Kitab** because it was written at the beginning of the mushaf and is recited at the beginning of prayer. It was also said that it is called this because it contains the summary of all the meanings of the Qur’an.

Ibn Jarir explained that Arabs call something **umm** when it is a basis for what follows it or when it gathers what comes after it. Thus, **umm ar-ra’s** is said for the membrane surrounding the brain. Likewise, the banner around which armies gather is called an *umm* because it is the point of unity and gathering.

Dhu ar-Rummah said:

> عَلَى رَأْسِهِ أَمٌّ لَنَا تَفْتَدِي بِهَا جِمَاعُ أُمُورٍ لَيْسَ نَعْصِي لَهَا أَمْرًا

meaning that the banner standing atop the spear gathers their affairs and they do not disobey its command.

Likewise, Makkah was called **Umm al-Qura** because it is the origin and center of the towns, or because the earth was spread out from it.

As for the virtue of Al-Fatihah, many hadiths have been narrated about it. Among them is what Imam Ahmad narrated from Abu Sa‘id ibn al-Mu‘alla رضي الله عنه, that the Messenger of Allah ﷺ said to him:

“I will teach you a surah that is the greatest surah in the Qur’an before you leave the mosque.”

Then he said:

> الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ رَبِّ الْعَالَمِينَ

“It is the seven oft-repeated verses and the great Qur’an that I was given.”

This was also narrated by Al-Bukhari, Abu Dawud, An-Nasa’i, and Ibn Majah. Scholars used this hadith as evidence that some surahs and verses are superior to others.

In another hadith narrated by Al-Bukhari, one of the Companions recited Al-Fatihah as a ruqyah over a tribal chief who had been stung, and he recovered. When they informed the Messenger of Allah ﷺ, he said:

“How did you know that it is a ruqyah?”

In the hadith narrated by Muslim from Ibn ‘Abbas رضي الله عنهما, an angel came to the Prophet ﷺ and said:

“Receive glad tidings of two lights that were given to you and not given to any prophet before you: the Opening of the Book and the closing verses of Surah Al-Baqarah. You will not recite a single letter from them except that you will be granted it.”

The Messenger of Allah ﷺ also said:

“Whoever performs a prayer without reciting Umm al-Qur’an, then it is deficient.”

In the Qudsi hadith narrated by Abu Hurairah رضي الله عنه, the Messenger of Allah ﷺ said that Allah تعالى said:

“I have divided the prayer between Myself and My servant into two halves.”

When the servant says:

> الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ رَبِّ الْعَالَمِينَ

Allah says: **My servant has praised Me.**

When he says:

> الرَّحْمَٰنِ الرَّحِيمِ

Allah says: **My servant has extolled Me.**

When he says:

> مَالِكِ يَوْمِ الدِّينِ

Allah says: **My servant has glorified Me.**

When he says:

> إِيَّاكَ نَعْبُدُ وَإِيَّاكَ نَسْتَعِينُ

Allah says: **This is between Me and My servant, and My servant shall have what he asks.**

When he says:

> اهْدِنَا الصِّرَاطَ الْمُسْتَقِيمَ

to the end of the surah, Allah says: **This is for My servant, and My servant shall have what he asks.**

This hadith shows the immense greatness of Surah Al-Fatihah, because in prayer it becomes a direct dialogue between the servant and his Lord.

Thus, Surah Al-Fatihah has an extraordinarily high rank in the Qur’an and in worship. It is the **Mother of the Qur’an**, gathering the meanings of tawhid, supplication, and guidance. It is a pillar in prayer, a healing, and a special light that Allah granted to His Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and his community. Therefore, reciting and understanding Al-Fatihah is not merely a ritual repetition, but the very heart of the bond between the servant and his Lord. And Allah knows best.


## The Ruling on Reciting Al-Fatihah in Prayer

The scholars agree that reciting Surah al-Fatihah in prayer holds a very important status, and indeed forms an essential core of the prayer itself. This is based on many authentic hadiths that affirm the necessity of reciting al-Fatihah in prayer. Among them is the hadith narrated by Muslim from Abu Hurairah, in which the Messenger of Allah ﷺ said:

> مَنْ صَلَّى صَلَاةً لَمْ يَقْرَأْ فِيهَا بِأُمِّ الْقُرْآنِ فَهِيَ خِدَاجٌ غَيْرُ تَمَامٍ
>
> “Whoever performs a prayer without reciting Umm al-Qur’an in it, then it is deficient and incomplete.”

In another hadith narrated by al-Bukhari and Muslim from ‘Ubadah ibn as-Samit, the Messenger of Allah ﷺ said:

> لَا صَلَاةَ لِمَنْ لَمْ يَقْرَأْ بِفَاتِحَةِ الْكِتَابِ
>
> “There is no valid prayer for the one who does not recite Fatihat al-Kitab.”

These hadiths form the main basis for the majority of scholars, such as Imam Malik, Imam al-Shafi‘i, Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal, and their followers, who hold the view that reciting al-Fatihah is a pillar of prayer. This means that the prayer is invalid without reciting al-Fatihah, whether for the imam, the follower, or the person praying alone.

Imam al-Shafi‘i and a group of scholars held that reciting al-Fatihah is obligatory in every rak‘ah of the prayer, because the hadith stating that there is no valid prayer without al-Fatihah is general and includes every rak‘ah. According to them, each rak‘ah is an independent unit and must therefore be completed with the recitation of al-Fatihah.

However, there is another opinion from some scholars. Abu Hanifah and his followers held that no specific recitation is obligatory in prayer. According to them, a person may recite any verse or surah from the Qur’an, and his prayer remains valid. They cite as evidence the saying of Allah:

> فَاقْرَءُوا مَا تَيَسَّرَ مِنَ الْقُرْآنِ
>
> “So recite whatever is easy for you of the Qur’an.” (Qur’an, Al-Muzzammil: 20)

They also cite the hadith about the man whose prayer was inadequate, in which the Messenger of Allah ﷺ said:

> إِذَا قُمْتَ إِلَى الصَّلَاةِ فَكَبِّرْ، ثُمَّ اقْرَأْ مَا تَيَسَّرَ مَعَكَ مِنَ الْقُرْآنِ
>
> “When you stand for prayer, say the takbir, then recite whatever is easy for you of the Qur’an.”

In that hadith, the Messenger of Allah ﷺ did not specifically mention al-Fatihah. Therefore, according to them, a general recitation from the Qur’an is sufficient.

The scholars also differed regarding whether the follower behind the imam must recite al-Fatihah. Some scholars held that the follower is still required to recite al-Fatihah, whether in audible prayers or silent prayers, because of the general wording of the hadith:

> لَا صَلَاةَ لِمَنْ لَمْ يَقْرَأْ بِفَاتِحَةِ الْكِتَابِ
>
> “There is no valid prayer for the one who does not recite Fatihat al-Kitab.”

Other scholars held that the follower does not need to recite al-Fatihah, because the imam’s recitation suffices for him. They cited the hadith:

> مَنْ كَانَ لَهُ إِمَامٌ فَقِرَاءَةُ الإِمَامِ لَهُ قِرَاءَةٌ
>
> “Whoever has an imam, then the imam’s recitation is a recitation for him.”

However, this hadith has weakness in its chain of transmission.

A third opinion states that the follower must recite al-Fatihah in silent prayers, but not in audible prayers. This opinion is based on the hadith:

> إِذَا قَرَأَ فَأَنْصِتُوا
>
> “When the imam recites, then remain silent.”

This hadith is narrated in Sahih Muslim, and is used as evidence that the follower listens to the imam’s recitation in audible prayers, and thus does not need to recite al-Fatihah at that time.

Thus, although there is disagreement among the scholars, the majority hold that reciting al-Fatihah is a pillar of prayer and must be recited in every rak‘ah. This shows the immense status of al-Fatihah, as it is the essence of prayer and the core of the relationship between the servant and his Lord in every rak‘ah he performs. And Allah knows best.

## Seeking Refuge in Allah (Isti‘adhah) and Its Ruling

Allah تعالى says:

> فَإِذَا قَرَأْتَ الْقُرْآنَ فَاسْتَعِذْ بِاللَّهِ مِنَ الشَّيْطَانِ الرَّجِيمِ إِنَّهُ لَيْسَ لَهُ سُلْطَانٌ عَلَى الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا وَعَلَىٰ رَبِّهِمْ يَتَوَكَّلُونَ إِنَّمَا سُلْطَانُهُ عَلَى الَّذِينَ يَتَوَلَّوْنَهُ وَالَّذِينَ هُمْ بِهِ مُشْرِكُونَ
>
> “When you recite the Qur’an, seek refuge in Allah from Satan, the accursed. Indeed, he has no authority over those who believe and rely upon their Lord. His authority is only over those who take him as an ally and those who associate partners with Allah.” (Qur’an, An-Nahl: 98–100)

This verse is the basis for the legislation of reciting isti‘adhah before reciting the Qur’an. According to the majority of scholars, isti‘adhah is recited before reciting the Qur’an in order to drive away the interference of Satan and purify the recitation from whispers that may ruin concentration and reverence. The intended meaning of the verse is: when you intend to recite the Qur’an, then seek refuge in Allah. This interpretation is similar to the saying of Allah:

> إِذَا قُمْتُمْ إِلَى الصَّلَاةِ فَاغْسِلُوا وُجُوهَكُمْ
>
> “When you rise for prayer, wash your faces.” (Qur’an, Al-Ma’idah: 6)

The meaning is that when a person intends to perform prayer, he first performs ablution.

Several hadiths also explain the practice of isti‘adhah. Imam Ahmad narrated from Abu Sa‘id al-Khudri that when the Messenger of Allah ﷺ stood for night prayer, he would begin with takbir and the supplication:

> سُبْحَانَكَ اللَّهُمَّ وَبِحَمْدِكَ، وَتَبَارَكَ اسْمُكَ، وَتَعَالَى جَدُّكَ، وَلَا إِلَهَ غَيْرُكَ

Then he would say:

> أَعُوذُ بِاللَّهِ السَّمِيعِ الْعَلِيمِ مِنَ الشَّيْطَانِ الرَّجِيمِ، مِنْ هَمْزِهِ وَنَفْخِهِ وَنَفْثِهِ
>
> “I seek refuge in Allah, the All-Hearing, the All-Knowing, from Satan, the accursed, from his whispering, his arrogance, and his blowing.”

This hadith was also narrated by the compilers of the Sunan, and at-Tirmidhi said that it is the most well-known hadith on the subject of isti‘adhah.

In another hadith narrated by al-Bukhari from Sulayman ibn Surad, it is mentioned that two men insulted one another in the presence of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ. One of them became so angry that his face turned red. The Messenger of Allah ﷺ then said:

> إِنِّي لَأَعْلَمُ كَلِمَةً لَوْ قَالَهَا لَذَهَبَ عَنْهُ مَا يَجِدُ، لَوْ قَالَ: أَعُوذُ بِاللَّهِ مِنَ الشَّيْطَانِ الرَّجِيمِ
>
> “Indeed, I know a phrase that, if he were to say it, what he feels would go away: ‘I seek refuge in Allah from Satan, the accursed.’”

This hadith was also narrated by Muslim, Abu Dawud, and an-Nasa’i, and it shows that isti‘adhah is not only recited before reading the Qur’an, but is also recommended when one is angry or disturbed by Satan.

As for the ruling of isti‘adhah, the majority of scholars hold that it is sunnah, not obligatory. Therefore, the one who leaves it is not sinful, although he loses its virtue. It is narrated from Imam Malik that he did not recite ta‘awwudh in obligatory prayers.

Imam al-Shafi‘i stated in *al-Imla’* that it is recommended to recite isti‘adhah aloud, though reciting it quietly is also acceptable. In *al-Umm*, he gave a choice: one may recite isti‘adhah or not, and if a person says:

> أَعُوذُ بِاللَّهِ مِنَ الشَّيْطَانِ الرَّجِيمِ

then that is sufficient.

According to Abu Hanifah and Muhammad, isti‘adhah is recited in prayer to begin the recitation of the Qur’an. Abu Yusuf, however, held that isti‘adhah is recited to begin the prayer itself. Based on this opinion, the follower in the ‘Id prayer recites isti‘adhah after the opening takbir before the extra takbirs. But according to the majority of scholars, isti‘adhah is recited after the takbir and before reciting al-Fatihah or any other surah.

Among the benefits of isti‘adhah is that it purifies the mouth from useless speech, making it ready to recite the word of Allah. Isti‘adhah is also an acknowledgment by the servant of his own weakness and his need for Allah’s help in facing the inward enemy, namely Satan, who cannot be seen. No one can repel his harm except Allah.

Allah says:

> إِنَّ عِبَادِي لَيْسَ لَكَ عَلَيْهِمْ سُلْطَانٌ وَكَفَىٰ بِرَبِّكَ وَكِيلًا
>
> “Indeed, over My servants you have no authority, and your Lord is sufficient as a protector.” (Qur’an, Al-Isra’: 65)

Linguistically, the word isti‘adhah means seeking protection from Allah against evil. The expression:

> أَعُوذُ بِاللَّهِ مِنَ الشَّيْطَانِ الرَّجِيمِ

means: “I seek protection in Allah from Satan, the accursed, so that he may not harm me in my religion or in my worldly affairs, nor prevent me from Allah’s commands, nor push me toward His prohibitions.” For none can repel Satan except Allah, who created him.

For this reason, Allah commands people to deal with enemies from among human beings with gentleness and goodness, but He commands them to seek refuge in Him from Satan among the jinn, because Satan cannot be softened through gifts or kindness.

Allah says:

> خُذِ الْعَفْوَ وَأْمُرْ بِالْعُرْفِ وَأَعْرِضْ عَنِ الْجَاهِلِينَ
>
> “Show forgiveness, enjoin what is right, and turn away from the ignorant.” (Qur’an, Al-A‘raf: 199)

And He says:

> وَإِمَّا يَنزَغَنَّكَ مِنَ الشَّيْطَانِ نَزْغٌ فَاسْتَعِذْ بِاللَّهِ
>
> “And if Satan should stir you to evil, then seek refuge in Allah.” (Qur’an, Al-A‘raf: 200)

Likewise, He says:

> ادْفَعْ بِالَّتِي هِيَ أَحْسَنُ السَّيِّئَةَ
>
> “Repel evil with that which is better.” (Qur’an, Al-Mu’minun: 96)

Then this is followed by the command to seek refuge from Satan.

Linguistically, the word *shaytan* comes from a root meaning to be far removed, that is, far from goodness. Because of his wickedness, he is far from the mercy of Allah. Therefore, the word *shaytan* is used for every rebellious being, whether from the jinn, humans, or animals. Allah says:

> وَكَذَٰلِكَ جَعَلْنَا لِكُلِّ نَبِيٍّ عَدُوًّا شَيَاطِينَ الْإِنسِ وَالْجِنِّ
>
> “And thus We made for every prophet an enemy—devils from mankind and jinn.” (Qur’an, Al-An‘am: 112)

Thus, isti‘adhah is the servant’s act of seeking protection in Allah before reciting His word, and at the same time an acknowledgment of his weakness and need for divine help. It becomes the opening of sacred recitation, a protection for the heart from Satanic whispers, and a means to bring about reverence in worship and in reciting the Qur’an. And Allah knows best.

## The Reality of Satan

In Arabic, the word *shaytan* comes from the root *shathana*, which means to be far removed. In other words, Satan is a being far from goodness, far from the mercy of Allah, and far from noble qualities. Because of his wickedness and rebellion against Allah’s command, he is expelled from divine mercy, and thus called shaytan.

Some scholars held that the word *shaytan* comes from the root *shata*, meaning to burn, because Satan was created from fire. However, the stronger opinion is that it comes from *shathana*, meaning to be far removed, because in Arabic usage the word *shaytan* is applied to every rebellious being who is far from goodness, whether from the jinn, human beings, or animals.

This is in accordance with the saying of Allah:

> وَكَذَٰلِكَ جَعَلْنَا لِكُلِّ نَبِيٍّ عَدُوًّا شَيَاطِينَ الْإِنسِ وَالْجِنِّ يُوحِي بَعْضُهُمْ إِلَىٰ بَعْضٍ زُخْرُفَ الْقَوْلِ غُرُورًا
>
> “And thus We made for every prophet an enemy—devils from mankind and jinn, inspiring one another with decorated speech in delusion.” (Qur’an, Al-An‘am: 112)

This verse shows that devils are not only from among the jinn, but also from among humankind. Every being who is rebellious, leads others astray, and calls to evil may be called a shaytan.

The reality of Satan is that he is a creature whose nature inclines toward evil and corruption. He cannot be softened by gifts or influenced by goodness the way a human being might be. For this reason, Allah commands human beings to face enemies from among humans with gentleness and kindness, but commands them to seek refuge in Him from Satan among the jinn, because none can repel him except Allah.

Satan works by whispering evil into the hearts of human beings, making wrongdoing appear attractive, and leading people away from the straight path. He can see human beings, while they cannot see him. That is why human beings are commanded to seek refuge in Allah, who sees Satan and has power over him.

By understanding the reality of Satan, a servant realizes that his greatest enemy is not only what appears outwardly, but also the inner enemy who constantly tempts and misguides him. Therefore, isti‘adhah, remembrance of Allah, and obedience to Him are the main fortresses that protect a person from Satan’s temptations, because no one can protect human beings from him except Allah سبحانه وتعالى. And Allah knows best.

### Conclusion

From the entire discussion above, it becomes clear that the Qur’an was revealed with an immensely profound structure, arrangement, and wisdom. The discussion of the Makkan and Madinan surahs, the number of verses, words, and letters, the meaning of the terms surah and ayah, the status of Surah al-Fatihah, the ruling on reciting it in prayer, and the discussion of isti‘adhah and the reality of Satan—all of these show how perfect this sacred Book is in guiding humanity.

The Qur’an is not merely recitation, but a complete guide for life that governs belief, worship, character, and all aspects of human life. Every surah has its rank, every ayah has its wisdom, and every letter of it contains reward for the one who recites it with faith and sincerity. Even Surah al-Fatihah, which is the heart of the Qur’an, has been made a pillar of prayer, such that a servant’s prayer is not valid without reciting it. This shows the immense status of the Qur’an in the life of a Muslim.

Likewise, the discussion of isti‘adhah teaches that human beings are never free from the disturbance of Satan, whether from among the jinn or humankind. For this reason, a servant is commanded to constantly seek refuge in Allah before reciting the Qur’an and in all situations, so that his heart may remain protected from misleading whispers.

All of this confirms that understanding the Qur’an does not mean merely reading its wording, but also studying its structure, meanings, rulings, and wisdom. In this way, a Muslim becomes able to recite the Qur’an with awareness, act upon it correctly, and make it a light that illuminates his life in this world and the Hereafter.

May Allah make us among those who love the Qur’an, recite it, understand it, and act upon it in daily life. And may He always protect us from Satan’s temptations and grant us success in walking the straight path until the end of our lives. And Allah knows best.
