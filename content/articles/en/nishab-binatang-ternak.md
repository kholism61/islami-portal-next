## Introduction

Zakat on livestock is part of *zakat al-mal* that has been prescribed in detail in Islamic law. Its discussion is among the most detailed chapters in fiqh because it deals with the types of animals, the minimum threshold (*nishab*), the age of the animals that must be given, the physical condition of the animals, and the legal rulings regarding combined ownership. All of these rulings are derived from the Qur’an, the Sunnah of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ, and the practice of the Companions such as Abu Bakr and Umar, may Allah be pleased with them.

Nishab is the minimum threshold of wealth that makes zakat obligatory. In livestock, the nishab is determined by the number of animals, not by their market value. The livestock that are subject to zakat are camels, cattle (including buffalo), and sheep/goats, provided that they are grazing animals (*sa’imah*) and that a full haul (one lunar year of ownership) has passed.

What follows is a complete explanation beginning with the initial nishab of camels, cattle, and sheep/goats, and ending with the discussion of mixed ownership of livestock.

## The Initial Nishab of Camels

> وَ أَوَّلُ نِصَابِ الْإِبِلِ خَمْسٌ، وَ فِيْهَا شَاةٌ، وَ فِي عَشْرِ شَاتَانِ، وَفِي خَمْسَ عَشْرَةَ ثَلَاثُ شِيَاءٍ، وَفِي عِشْرِينَ أَرْبَعُ شِيَاءٍ، وَفِي خَمْسٍ وَعِشْرِينَ بِنْتُ مَخَاضٍ مِنَ الْإِبِلِ، وَفِي سِتَ وَثَلَاثِينَ بِنْتُ لَبُوْنٍ وَفِي سِةٍ وَأَرْبَعِيْنَ حِقَّةً وَفِي إِحْدَى وَسِتِّينَ جَذَعَةً وَ فِي سِتّ وَ سَبْعِينَ بِنْتَا لَبُوْنٍ وَ فِي إِحْدَى وَ تِسْعِينَ حِقَّتَانِ وَفِي مِائَةٍ وَإِحْدَى وَعِشْرِينَ ثَلَاثُ بَنَاتِ لَبُوْنٍ، ثُمَّ فِي كُلِّ أَرْبَعِيْنَ بِنْتُ لَبُوْنٍ وَفِي كُلِّ خَمْسِينَ حِقَّةٌ.

The initial nishab of camels is five, and on them one sheep is due. For ten camels, two sheep are due. For fifteen, three sheep are due. For twenty, four sheep are due. For twenty-five camels, the zakat is one *bint makhad* from the camels (a female camel that has completed one year and entered the second). For thirty-six camels, the zakat is one *bint labun* (a female camel that has completed two years and entered the third). For forty-six camels, the zakat is one *hiqqah* (a female camel that has completed three years and entered the fourth). For sixty-one camels, the zakat is one *jadha‘ah* (a female camel that has completed four years and entered the fifth). For seventy-six camels, the zakat is two *bint labun*. For ninety-one camels, the zakat is two *hiqqah*. For one hundred and twenty-one camels, the zakat is three *bint labun*. After that, for every forty camels one *bint labun* is due, and for every fifty one *hiqqah* is due.

### Evidence for the Nishab of Camels

The proof that the initial nishab of camels is five is the saying of the Prophet ﷺ:

> لَيْسَ فِيمَا دُونَ خَمْسٍ ذَوْدٍ مِنَ الْإِبِلِ صَدَقَةٌ
>
> “There is no zakat on fewer than five camels.” (Narrated by Bukhari and Muslim).

The obligation of giving sheep for the zakat of camels differs from the original rule, because sheep are not of the same type as camels. However, in this legislation there is ease for the owner and benefit for the poor. If one camel were required from five camels, that would be burdensome, while if there were no zakat at all, it would deprive the rightful recipients.

This ruling is based on the letter of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, may Allah be pleased with him, which he sent to Bahrain, containing the details of zakat as prescribed by the Messenger of Allah ﷺ (Narrated by Bukhari).

### Explanation of the Ages and Terms

The sheep that must be given for camel zakat below twenty-five camels is a *jadha‘ah* from sheep (one year old). A goat is one that has reached two years. The word *shah* includes both. The owner may choose according to value and benefit.

*Bint makhad* is called that because its mother is usually pregnant (*makhad*). *Bint labun* is so called because its mother has milk. *Hiqqah* is a camel that has reached the age fit for riding. *Jadha‘ah* is a camel whose front teeth have fallen out. All these ages are counted as having entered the following year.

## The Initial Nishab of Cattle

> وَ أَوَّلُ نِصَابِ الْبَقَرِ ثَلَاثُوْنَ وَ فِيْهَا تَبِيعُ وَفِي أَرْبَعِيْنَ مُسِنَّةٌ.

The initial nishab of cattle is thirty, and on them one *tabi‘* is due (a calf one year old entering the second). For forty cattle, the zakat is one *musinnah* (a female cow two years old entering the third).

There is no zakat on cattle that have not reached thirty head. The proof is the hadith of the Prophet ﷺ when he sent Mu‘adh to Yemen, that he ordered zakat to be taken from every thirty cattle as one *tabi‘* and from every forty as one *musinnah* (Narrated by Tirmidhi, graded hasan, and authenticated by al-Hakim).

### Explanation of the Terms

*Tabi‘* is called that because it follows its mother in the pasture. Some say it is so called because its horns follow its ears, meaning they are of equal growth.

*Musinnah* is called that because its teeth have become complete. Al-Azhari said that its name comes from the appearance and completion of its teeth.

### Calculation Method

The calculation of cattle zakat proceeds according to multiples of thirty and forty. For every thirty head, one *tabi‘* is due, and for every forty, one *musinnah* is due. This continues according to the combination most suitable to the number of cattle owned.

If someone gives two *tabi‘*, then according to the sounder opinion that is permissible.

## The Initial Nishab of Sheep and Goats

> وَ أَوَّلُ نِصَابِ الْغَنَمِ أَرْبَعُوْنَ وَفِيْهَا شَاةٌ جَزَعَةٌ مِنَ الضَّأْنِ أَوْ ثَنِيَّةٌ مِنَ الْمَعْزِ وَ فِي مِائَةٍ وَإِحْدَى وَ عِشْرِينَ شَاتَانِ وَ فِي مِائَتَيْنِ وَ وَاحِدَةٍ شِيَاهِ ثُمَّ فِي كُلِّ مِائَةٍ شَاةٌ.

The initial nishab of sheep or goats is forty. At that number, the zakat due is one sheep: a *jadha‘ah* from sheep (one year old) or a *thaniyyah* from goats (two years old). At 121, the zakat is two sheep. At 201, the zakat is three sheep. After that, for every additional 100, one sheep is due.

### Conditions for the Animal Taken

It is not permissible to take an old and worn-out animal, a defective animal, an exceptionally fat and valuable animal, an animal that is nursing, or a pregnant animal, unless the owner is willing.

The proof is the saying of Allah:

> وَلَا تَيَمَّمُوا الْخَبِيثَ مِنْهُ تُنفِقُونَ

And the saying of the Prophet ﷺ:

> لا تُؤْخَذُ فِي الصَّدَقَةِ هَرِمَةٌ وَلَا ذَاتُ عَوَارٍ
>
> “An old animal and a defective animal are not to be taken in zakat.” (Narrated by Bukhari).

If all the livestock are sick or young, then one is taken from the type that exists. If all are male, then a male is taken. The principle is justice and the avoidance of harm to both parties, the owner of the wealth and the recipient of zakat.

## Mixed Ownership of Livestock (Al-Khalth)

> وَالْخَلِيْطَانِ يُزَكِّيَانِ زَكَاةَ الْوَاحِدِ بِشَرَائِطَ سَبْعَةٍ...

Two people who mix their livestock pay zakat as one person, under seven conditions: one enclosure, one pasture, one shepherd, one breeding male, one watering place, one milker, and one place of milking.

There are two types of mixing: shared ownership (*shuyu‘*) and neighboring mixture. Mixing can cause zakat to become obligatory, decrease, or increase depending on the total number of livestock that are combined.

### Evidence for Mixed Ownership

> لا يُجْمَعُ بَيْنَ مُتَفَرِّقٍ وَلَا يُفَرَّقُ بَيْنَ مُجْتَمِعٍ خَشْيَةَ الصَّدَقَةِ
>
> “What is separate should not be combined, and what is combined should not be separated, out of fear of zakat.” (Narrated by Bukhari).

It is required that the nishab be reached in the combined ownership, that both parties are among those obligated to pay zakat, and that the mixture continues throughout the entire haul. If the mixture is interrupted before a full year is completed, then the ruling of mixed ownership does not apply.

## Conclusion

From all of this explanation, it becomes clear that the legislation of zakat on livestock is built upon the principles of justice, welfare, and balance between the right of the owner and the right of the poor. The details concerning age, number, and the conditions of mixed ownership demonstrate the perfection of the Sharia in regulating wealth.

Each type of livestock has its own rules regarding nishab and amount due, yet all of them are based on strong evidence from the Sunnah of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ and the practice of the Companions. Therefore, understanding these details is an obligation for anyone who owns livestock, so that zakat may be discharged according to the guidance of the Sharia.

Wallahu a‘lam bish-shawab.
